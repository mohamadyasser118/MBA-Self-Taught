## 🧭 Course Overview

This course teaches the language of business — financial accounting. You learn how to read and prepare the three main financial statements: the balance sheet (what a company owns and owes), the income statement (how much it earns), and the statement of cash flows (where its cash comes from and goes). The course uses a real startup case (Relic Spotter Inc.) to walk through the entire accounting cycle, from recording daily transactions to closing the books at year end. It also looks at 3M Company's real annual report to connect the rules to real-world reporting. # Module 1 — Introduction and Balance Sheet

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Accounting is the system that records business transactions and produces summary reports of a company's financial health. The balance sheet is the most basic report — a snapshot of what a company owns (assets) and how those assets are paid for (liabilities and stockholders' equity) at a single point in time. This module teaches the balance sheet equation (Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity) as the "grammar rule" of accounting. It then builds the mechanics of double-entry bookkeeping using debits and credits to keep the equation balanced. The module ends with a tour of 3M's annual report to connect the classroom to the real world.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Accounting:** The system of recording business transactions to produce summary reports of a company's financial position and performance. Different from tax accounting (for the government) and managerial accounting (for internal decisions).

- **Financial Accounting:** Produces standard reports for outside users — investors, lenders, analysts, and regulators.

- **GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles):** The set of rules for financial accounting in the US. Created by the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) and overseen by the SEC.

- **IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards):** The accounting rules used in most countries outside the US.

- **SEC Filings:** Public companies in the US must file:
  - **10-K:** Annual report (audited, very detailed)
  - **10-Q:** Quarterly report (unaudited, shorter)
  - **8-K:** Report of major events (buying another company, losing a CEO, going bankrupt)

- **Four Required Financial Statements:**
  1. **Balance Sheet:** Financial position at a specific date (Assets = Liabilities + Equity)
  2. **Income Statement:** Performance over a period using accrual accounting
  3. **Statement of Cash Flows:** Where cash came from and where it went
  4. **Statement of Stockholders' Equity:** How equity changed over time

- **Balance Sheet Equation:** Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity. This must always be true.

- **Assets:** Resources that will bring future benefit — either by bringing in cash or saving cash that would have been spent. To record an asset, it must (a) come from a past transaction and (b) have a future benefit that can be measured reasonably well.

- **Liabilities:** What the company owes to others — promises to pay cash, deliver goods, or provide services in the future. To record a liability, there must be an obligation from receiving something, and the amount and timing must be reasonably certain.

- **Stockholders' Equity:** What is left for owners after paying all debts. Also called net worth. Two main parts:
  - **Contributed Capital:** Money owners put in (common stock + additional paid-in capital)
  - **Retained Earnings:** All profits ever made minus all dividends ever paid

- **Dividends:** Money paid to shareholders. Not an expense. Creates a liability when the board declares it, even if cash has not been paid yet.

- **Double-Entry Bookkeeping:** Every transaction affects at least two accounts. Total debits always equal total credits.

- **Debit (Dr):** Left side. Assets and expenses normally have debit balances (recording more of them means more debits).

- **Credit (Cr):** Right side. Liabilities, equity, and revenues normally have credit balances (recording more of them means more credits).

- **T-Account:** A simple picture of an account — debits on the left, credits on the right.

- **Journal Entry:** The formal record of a transaction. Debits come first (left-aligned). Credits come second (indented).

- **Trial Balance:** A list of all accounts and their balances. Total debits must equal total credits.

- **Entity Concept:** The business is separate from its owners. The owner's personal transactions do not go on the company's books.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The balance sheet equation is not just a math trick — it shows a real economic fact.** Assets are resources. Liabilities and equity are claims on those resources. Every resource must be claimed by someone — either lenders (liabilities) or owners (equity). This is the accounting way of showing who has rights to what.

2. **Double-entry bookkeeping catches errors.** Because debits must always equal credits, the system has a built-in check. If the trial balance does not balance, something is wrong. This was a huge advance when invented in medieval Italy and is still the backbone of accounting.

3. **Accountants do not create value — they measure it.** This is important to remember. Depreciation estimates, useful lives, and salvage values are judgments, not facts. The balance sheet shows historical cost (mostly), not current market value. Knowing what accounting does and does not measure is key to using it well.

4. **The annual report is a deal among different groups.** Management prepares the numbers. The audit committee (board members) watches over them. Outside auditors give a check (reasonable assurance, not a guarantee). The SEC reviews. Analysts and short sellers also look for problems. Each group has different goals, and understanding this helps you read the report with healthy doubt.

5. **Not every promise gets recorded.** The Relic Spotter case shows an important point: a hiring contract with no work done yet is not recorded — no exchange has happened. Also, the owner's personal loans are not on the company's books because of the entity concept. Accounting records actual exchanges, not future plans.

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Balance Sheet Equation (Basic):** Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity

- **Balance Sheet Equation (Full):** Assets = Liabilities + Contributed Capital + Retained Earnings

- **Retained Earnings Change:** Ending Retained Earnings = Beginning Retained Earnings + Net Income — Dividends Declared

- **Debit/Credit Rule:** Total Debits = Total Credits (for every entry and for the whole trial balance)

- **Normal Balances:**
  - Debit normal: Assets (+Dr, -Cr), Expenses (+Dr, -Cr), Dividends (+Dr, -Cr)
  - Credit normal: Liabilities (-Dr, +Cr), Equity (-Dr, +Cr), Revenues (-Dr, +Cr)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before analyzing any balance sheet, check that assets equal liabilities plus equity. If not, the statements have an error. This almost never happens in public company reports but can happen in internal reports.

- Before recording any transaction, ask: (1) What accounts are affected? (2) Are they assets, liabilities, equity, revenues, or expenses? (3) Do they normally have debit or credit balances? (4) Are we increasing or decreasing them? This step-by-step method prevents mistakes.

- Do not confuse accounting value with market value. A building bought for $1 million twenty years ago might be worth $5 million today, but the balance sheet still shows cost minus depreciation. Accounting is not about what things are worth — it is about recording what was paid.

- Read the footnotes. In 3M's 10-K, the footnotes explain what is really going on with inventory, taxes, pensions, and other things that the main numbers only hint at.

# Module 2 — Accrual Accounting and the Income Statement

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Cash accounting is simple but can lie. It records revenue when cash comes in and expenses when cash goes out. Accrual accounting — the method required by GAAP — records revenue when it is earned (whether cash has arrived yet or not) and expenses when they are incurred (whether cash has left yet or not). This module explains the revenue recognition rule, the expense matching rule, and the adjusting entries that turn cash records into accrual financial statements. It walks through the whole accounting cycle: unadjusted trial balance → adjusting entries → adjusted trial balance → financial statements → closing entries. The Relic Spotter case continues, with students recording depreciation, amortization, unpaid interest, prepaid advertising, and taxes owed.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Accrual Accounting:** Records revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, no matter when cash moves. Required by GAAP.

- **Cash Accounting:** Records revenue when cash is received and expenses when cash is paid. Simpler but can be very misleading.

- **Revenue Recognition Rule:** Two things must be true to record revenue: (a) the work is done (goods delivered or service provided) and (b) payment is received or will definitely be received.

- **Expense Matching Rule:** Expenses are recorded in the same period as the revenue they helped create. Product costs (like inventory) are expensed when the product sells. Period costs (like advertising) are expensed when they happen.

- **Conservatism Rule:** Record losses as soon as you know about them, but only record gains when they actually happen. This stops companies from being too optimistic.

- **Adjusting Entries:** Entries made at the end of a period to update accounts for accrual accounting. They never involve cash. Four types:
  - **Deferred Expenses:** Cash paid before the expense happens (like prepaid rent). Adjust to show the part that has been used up.
  - **Deferred Revenues:** Cash received before the work is done (like gift cards). Adjust to show the part that has been earned.
  - **Accrued Expenses:** Expense happens before cash is paid (like wages owed but not yet paid).
  - **Accrued Revenues:** Work is done before cash is received (like interest earned but not yet received).

- **Depreciation:** Spreading the cost of a long-term physical asset (building, equipment, truck) over its useful life. A type of deferred expense.

- **Accumulated Depreciation:** A contra-asset account (meaning it goes against assets) that tracks total depreciation so far. Normal credit balance.

- **Amortization:** Same as depreciation but for intangible assets (patents, software licenses, trademarks).

- **Straight-Line Depreciation:** The most common method. Annual Depreciation = (Cost — Salvage Value) ÷ Useful Life (in years).

- **Book Value:** Cost — Accumulated Depreciation. Not market value.

- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS):** The cost of inventory that was sold during the period. Matched against sales revenue on the income statement.

- **Unadjusted Trial Balance:** Account balances before adjusting entries.

- **Adjusted Trial Balance:** Account balances after adjusting entries. Used to make financial statements.

- **Closing Entries:** Entries that reset temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, dividends) to zero by moving their balances to Retained Earnings. Done at the end of each period.

- **Temporary Accounts:** Revenue, expense, and dividend accounts. Start each period at zero.

- **Permanent Accounts:** Asset, liability, and equity accounts. Balances carry over from period to period.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Net income is not cash flow — and that is the whole point.** Accrual accounting is different from cash accounting on purpose. A company could get $1 million in cash today for work it will do next year (good cash flow, but no income yet). Or it could do $1 million of work today and get the cash next year (good income, but no cash yet). Both situations matter, and you need both the income statement and the cash flow statement to understand them.

2. **Revenue recognition needs judgment and is often manipulated.** The two rules (work done and payment likely) sound simple but require a lot of judgment. For a software company, when is the revenue "earned"? When the contract is signed? When the software is delivered? When the customer accepts it? Companies have reasons to record revenue early (to look better now) or late (to save it for a bad quarter). Reading the footnotes about revenue rules is very important.

3. **Depreciation is an estimate, not a fact.** Management picks the useful life, the salvage value, and the method. The same building could be depreciated over 20 years or 40 years, which changes net income by a lot. Neither number is "wrong" if the estimate is reasonable. But when comparing two companies, you need to check if they use similar estimates.

4. **The matching rule explains why some costs are assets.** When Relic Spotter buys inventory, it records an asset, not an expense. The expense (COGS) happens when the inventory sells. This matches the cost of the goods against the revenue from selling them. The same goes for prepaid advertising — it is an asset until the ads actually run.

5. **Closing entries are just housekeeping, but they matter.** Zeroing out temporary accounts each period makes sure that only this period's revenues and expenses appear on this period's income statement. Without this step, income would pile up forever and you could not measure performance by period. The transfer to Retained Earnings is what connects the income statement to the balance sheet.

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Straight-Line Depreciation:** Annual Expense = (Cost — Salvage Value) ÷ Useful Life (years)

- **Gross Profit:** Net Sales — Cost of Goods Sold

- **Operating Income:** Gross Profit — Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D, Depreciation, etc.)

- **Net Income (Full):** Operating Income + Non-Operating Items (Interest, Gains/Losses) — Income Tax Expense

- **Retained Earnings Update:** Ending Retained Earnings = Beginning Retained Earnings + Net Income — Dividends

- **Book Value of an Asset:** Historical Cost — Accumulated Depreciation

---

## F. Practical Implications

- When looking at an income statement, always separate operating from non-operating items. A company might have strong operating income but get dragged down by interest (too much debt) or a one-time loss. Or it might look good because of a one-time gain that will not happen again next year.

- Compare depreciation methods across companies in the same industry. One using faster depreciation will look less profitable at first but more profitable later. To really compare, you sometimes need to adjust for these differences.

- Big changes in working capital (receivables, inventory, payables) should make you curious. If receivables are growing much faster than sales, the company might be having trouble collecting cash — or it might be recording revenue that should not be recorded yet.

- Prepaid expenses are assets, but they are not very helpful in a crisis. They represent services already paid for but not yet received — nice for matching, but you cannot sell them to pay bills.

# Module 3 — Cash Flows

## A. Core Idea of the Module

The income statement tells you about profit using accrual rules. The balance sheet tells you what a company owns and owes. But cash is what pays the bills, and neither of those statements fully explains cash. The statement of cash flows fills this gap. It sorts all cash transactions into three buckets: operating (core business), investing (long-term assets), and financing (owners and lenders). This module teaches how to sort cash flows, the difference between the direct and indirect methods, and how to prepare and read a cash flow statement. It also covers EBITDA and free cash flow — two numbers people use a lot (and misuse a lot).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Statement of Cash Flows:** A report showing cash coming in and going out, sorted into operating, investing, and financing activities.

- **Operating Activities:** Cash from the core business. Examples: cash from customers, cash paid to suppliers and workers, cash paid for taxes and interest.

- **Investing Activities:** Cash from buying and selling long-term assets. Examples: buying or selling buildings, equipment, trucks; buying or selling other companies; lending money to others (the principal part).

- **Financing Activities:** Cash from owners and lenders. Examples: issuing stock, buying back stock, borrowing money, paying back debt principal, paying dividends.

- **Direct Method (Operating):** Lists actual cash receipts and payments. Example: "Cash from customers: $X; Cash paid to suppliers: $Y; Cash paid to workers: $Z." The FASB likes this method, but almost no one uses it.

- **Indirect Method (Operating):** Starts with net income, then adds back non-cash items and adjusts for changes in working capital. Almost every public company uses this method.

- **Non-Cash Expenses:** Expenses that reduce net income but do not use cash. Examples: depreciation, amortization, stock-based compensation. Added back to net income in the indirect method.

- **Changes in Working Capital:** Adjustments to net income for changes in current assets and current liabilities:
  - Current asset goes up (more receivables or inventory) → cash flow goes down (cash was used to get that asset)
  - Current liability goes up (more payables) → cash flow goes up (cash was not paid yet for that expense)

- **EBITDA:** Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. Often used as a stand-in for cash flow, but it leaves out working capital changes and cash taxes and interest.

- **Free Cash Flow:** Usually means Operating Cash Flow — Capital Spending. But people define it in many ways. Always check how someone is defining it.

- **Cash Flow Lifecycle Patterns:**
  - **Startup:** Operating cash flow is negative (spending to start up), investing is negative (buying stuff), financing is positive (raising money)
  - **Growing:** Operating might turn positive, investing is still negative (expanding), financing is positive but smaller
  - **Mature:** Operating is positive, investing is negative but smaller, financing is negative (giving cash back to owners)
  - **Declining:** Operating is positive or shrinking, investing is small, financing is negative or flat

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Operating cash flow is the most important number on the statement.** Investing and financing matter, but a company that cannot generate positive operating cash flow over time will not survive. Positive operating cash flow means the core business pays for itself. Negative operating cash flow (especially with negative investing cash flow too) means the company is burning cash and needs to raise more from lenders or owners — which cannot go on forever.

2. **The indirect method shows why profit and cash are different.** By starting with net income and then adjusting for non-cash items and working capital changes, the indirect method explains exactly why accrual profit and cash flow do not match. Large adjustments often tell an important story: growing receivables (cash not collected yet) or growing inventory (cash tied up in unsold goods) reduce cash flow even when net income looks good.

3. **EBITDA is not cash flow.** The course is rightly skeptical of EBITDA. Adding back depreciation and amortization moves you toward cash flow — but EBITDA still leaves out interest (a cash expense for most companies), taxes (a cash expense), and changes in working capital (which can be huge). Two companies with the same EBITDA can have very different cash flows depending on their working capital, their spending on new equipment, and their tax situation. EBITDA is useful sometimes (like comparing companies with different debt levels), but it is not cash flow.

4. **Working capital changes tell you what management is doing.** More receivables compared to sales could mean (a) sales are growing (good), (b) customers are paying slower (bad), or (c) the company is recording revenue that should not be recorded yet (very bad). More inventory could mean (a) getting ready for more sales (good) or (b) products are not selling (bad). The cash flow statement does not tell you which is true — but it points you to the question.

5. **Free cash flow is useful but people define it differently.** Some people say free cash flow is operating cash flow minus capital spending (cash available to give back to owners). Others also subtract required debt payments, dividends, or other must-pay items. When you see "free cash flow," always ask how it is defined.

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Indirect Method Operating Cash Flow:** Net Income
  + Non-Cash Expenses (Depreciation, Amortization, Stock Compensation, etc.)
  — Gains on Sales / + Losses on Sales (to move them to investing)
  — Increases in Non-Cash Current Assets (Receivables, Inventory, Prepaids)
  + Decreases in Non-Cash Current Assets
  + Increases in Current Liabilities (Payables, Accrued Expenses, Unearned Revenue)
  — Decreases in Current Liabilities
  = Net Cash from Operating Activities

- **Free Cash Flow (Common Definition):** Operating Cash Flow — Capital Expenditures

- **EBITDA:** Net Income + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Always look at the cash flow statement along with the income statement. A company with growing net income but shrinking operating cash flow is a warning sign — it might be recording revenue too early or building up too much inventory.

- Use working capital changes as a checkup tool. If receivables are growing faster than sales, something is off — either customers are not paying or revenue is being recorded wrong. If inventory is growing faster than sales, products might not be selling.

- Do not trust EBITDA too much. When someone shows you EBITDA, ask for the cash flow statement too. In some industries (like cable or cell towers), EBITDA is a real operating measure because spending on equipment is large and predictable. But even there, EBITDA cannot replace actual cash flow.

- Match cash flow patterns to the company's age. A mature company like 3M should have positive operating cash flow, negative investing cash flow (reinvesting in the business), and negative financing cash flow (giving cash back). A startup with negative operating cash flow and positive financing cash flow is not in trouble — that is what a startup looks like.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The difference between the direct and indirect methods is interesting in theory but not very important in practice, since almost everyone uses the indirect method. The direct method would be more clear — showing actual cash from customers, actual cash paid to suppliers — but companies say it costs too much to prepare and would give too much information to competitors. The course handles this well by teaching both but focusing on the indirect method for real life.

The Purple and Green Company example is the best teaching tool in this module. Two companies with very different net incomes ($150,000 vs. $50,000) but the same operating cash flow ($50,000). Purple's higher net income is eaten up by working capital growth (investing in receivables and inventory for growth); Green's lower net income is helped by working capital shrinking (selling off assets, collecting receivables, not buying more inventory). This shows perfectly that high net income with negative working capital changes might mean growth, while low net income with positive working capital changes might mean decline. Cash flow analysis sees what net income hides.

The EBITDA warning is strong but could be even stronger. EBITDA is misused all the time — especially by companies with a lot of debt, who present EBITDA as "cash available to pay debt" while ignoring that they have to pay cash interest on that same debt. The course says "true cash flow requires adjusting EBITDA for working capital changes and cash interest and taxes" — this is right, but the warning could be louder.

# Module 4 — Ratio Analysis and Final Exam

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Financial statements alone are not enough. Ratios — numbers that compare one financial item to another — give you a way to measure performance, find problems, and compare companies. This module teaches ratio analysis, with special focus on the DuPont framework for breaking down Return on Equity (ROE) into three parts: profitability (net margin), efficiency (asset turnover), and leverage (how much debt is used). The Plainview Technology case walks through a full ratio analysis from start to finish. The module ends by showing how to calculate ratios for 3M Company using real public financial statements and how to get ratios from financial websites.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Ratio Analysis:** Looking at relationships between numbers on financial statements to understand performance, health, and risk.

- **Benchmarking:** Comparing ratios to something useful: (a) the same company over time, or (b) other companies in the same industry. Ratios by themselves do not mean much — you need a comparison.

- **Return on Equity (ROE):** Net Income ÷ Average Stockholders' Equity. Measures how much return owners get on their investment. The most complete single measure of performance.

- **Return on Assets (ROA):** (Net Income + After-Tax Interest) ÷ Average Total Assets. Measures operating performance without including how the company is financed. We add back interest to remove the effect of debt.

- **Return on Sales (Net Margin):** Net Income ÷ Net Sales. Measures profit per dollar of sales.

- **Asset Turnover:** Net Sales ÷ Average Total Assets. Measures how well a company uses its assets to create sales. Higher is better (more sales from the same assets).

- **Financial Leverage (Equity Multiplier):** Average Total Assets ÷ Average Stockholders' Equity. Measures how much debt the company uses. Higher number = more debt = more risk (but also more potential return).

- **DuPont Analysis (3 Parts):** ROE = (Net Income ÷ Sales) × (Sales ÷ Assets) × (Assets ÷ Equity) = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier. Breaks ROE into profit, efficiency, and leverage.

- **Gross Margin:** (Net Sales — Cost of Goods Sold) ÷ Net Sales. Profit after paying for the product itself. Better gross margin can come from higher prices, lower input costs, or selling more high-margin products.

- **Operating Margin:** Operating Income ÷ Net Sales. Profit after all operating costs (COGS, SG&A, R&D, depreciation) but before interest and taxes.

- **Accounts Receivable Turnover:** Net Sales ÷ Average Accounts Receivable. How fast the company collects cash from sales. Higher = faster collection.

- **Days Receivable (Days Sales Outstanding, DSO):** 365 ÷ Accounts Receivable Turnover. Average days between sale and cash collection.

- **Inventory Turnover:** Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Average Inventory. How fast inventory sells. Higher = less cash tied up in inventory.

- **Days Inventory:** 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover. Average days inventory sits before selling.

- **Accounts Payable Turnover:** Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Average Accounts Payable. How fast the company pays its suppliers.

- **Days Payable:** 365 ÷ Accounts Payable Turnover. Average days the company takes to pay suppliers.

- **Net Trade Cycle (Cash Conversion Cycle):** Days Receivable + Days Inventory — Days Payable. How long cash is tied up between paying for materials and getting cash from customers. Shorter is better.

- **Current Ratio:** Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities. Measures ability to pay short-term debts. Higher = more safety (but too high can mean inefficient use of cash).

- **Quick Ratio (Acid Test):** (Current Assets — Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities. Like current ratio but removes inventory (which might be hard to sell fast). A stricter test.

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Ratios only mean something with a comparison.** "ROE of 15%" — is that good? It depends. Compared to 10% last year? Compared to 20% for competitors? Compared to 5% for the whole market? Without a benchmark, a ratio is just a number. The course emphasizes time-series (looking at the same company over time) and cross-sectional (comparing to peers) as the two main ways to get meaning from ratios.

2. **DuPont analysis tells you where ROE really comes from.** A company can have high ROE because (a) it has high profit margins (good), (b) it uses assets very efficiently (good), or (c) it uses a lot of debt (risky). DuPont breaks ROE into these three pieces so you can see the source. A company with high ROE from high profit margins is different from a company with high ROE from high debt. Both numbers might be the same, but the story is very different.

3. **Profit margin is not the only path to good returns.** Some companies (like Walmart) compete on low margins but very high asset turnover. Others (like luxury brands) compete on high margins but lower turnover. Both can have excellent ROE. The DuPont framework shows that there is more than one way to win. Comparing a high-margin company to a high-turnover company using only ROE misses the point — they have different strategies, and both can work.

4. **Working capital ratios reveal the company's cash flow engine.** Days Receivable, Days Inventory, and Days Payable together tell you how much cash is tied up in daily operations. A company that collects cash fast (low days receivable), sells inventory fast (low days inventory), and pays suppliers slow (high days payable) has a very efficient cash cycle — or even negative cash cycle (getting cash from customers before paying suppliers). This is a huge advantage.

5. **Liquidity ratios must be read carefully.** A high current ratio might mean a company is very safe and can pay its bills easily. Or it might mean the company has a lot of cash sitting around doing nothing (inefficient). A low current ratio might mean a company is risky — or it might mean the company is so efficient at turning inventory into cash that it does not need much safety cushion. The number alone does not tell you which is true.

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| DuPont Analysis (3-Factor) | ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier | To figure out why a company's ROE is high or low |
| Benchmarking Framework | Compare over time (trend) or to peers (cross-section) | To give ratios meaning |
| Profitability Ratios | Gross margin, operating margin, net margin, ROE, ROA | To measure how well a company turns sales into profit |
| Efficiency (Turnover) Ratios | Asset turnover, receivables turnover, inventory turnover, payables turnover | To measure how well a company uses its assets |
| Working Capital Ratios | Days receivable, days inventory, days payable, net trade cycle | To understand the cash flow from daily operations |
| Liquidity Ratios | Current ratio, quick ratio | To measure ability to pay short-term debts |
| Common Size Statements | Every number as a percentage of sales (income statement) or total assets (balance sheet) | To compare companies of different sizes |

## E. Formulas & Equations

**Profitability Ratios:**
- Gross Margin = (Sales — COGS) ÷ Sales
- Operating Margin = Operating Income ÷ Sales
- Net Margin = Net Income ÷ Sales
- ROA = (Net Income + After-Tax Interest) ÷ Average Assets
- ROE = Net Income ÷ Average Equity

**Efficiency (Turnover) Ratios:**
- Asset Turnover = Sales ÷ Average Assets
- Receivables Turnover = Sales ÷ Average Receivables
- Inventory Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
- Payables Turnover = COGS ÷ Average Payables

**Working Capital Timing Ratios:**
- Days Receivable (DSO) = 365 ÷ Receivables Turnover
- Days Inventory = 365 ÷ Inventory Turnover
- Days Payable = 365 ÷ Payables Turnover
- Net Trade Cycle = Days Receivable + Days Inventory — Days Payable

**Liquidity Ratios:**
- Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
- Quick Ratio = (Current Assets — Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities

**DuPont ROE Decomposition:**
- ROE = (Net Income ÷ Sales) × (Sales ÷ Assets) × (Assets ÷ Equity)

## F. Practical Implications

- Always compare ratios to something. A 10% net margin might be great for a grocery store (typical margins are 1-3%) but terrible for a software company (typical margins are 20-30%). Know the industry benchmarks.

- Use DuPont analysis to understand the story behind ROE. Is ROE going up because margins are improving (good) or because debt is increasing (risky)? The three pieces tell you.

- Calculate the net trade cycle (days receivable + days inventory — days payable) to understand how much cash is tied up in operations. A negative cycle (collecting cash before paying suppliers) is a superpower — it means the company grows without needing its own cash.

- Be careful with liquidity ratios. A very high current ratio might mean the company is safe — or it might mean the company is holding too much cash instead of investing it. A very low current ratio might mean the company is risky — or it might mean the company is so efficient that it does not need much cash. Look at the industry.

- Common size statements are very helpful for comparing companies of different sizes. Making every number a percentage of sales (income statement) or total assets (balance sheet) lets you see the structure, not just the size.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The DuPont framework is the most useful tool in this module. It takes a single number (ROE) and breaks it into three pieces that tell a real story about the business. A company with high ROE from high margins is doing something different from a company with high ROE from high leverage. DuPont shows the difference. This framework is used all the time in professional financial analysis, and learning it early is very valuable.

The Plainview Technology case is a good way to practice all the ratios together. The case shows a company that improved its ROE from 11% to 16% mainly by improving profit margins, not by using more debt. That is a healthy improvement. The case also shows that the company's net trade cycle got longer (more cash tied up) because inventory days increased — which might be a cost of the higher margins (custom products take longer to make). This is the kind of trade-off that ratio analysis reveals.

One small issue: the module introduces a lot of ratios but does not spend much time on which ratios matter most for different industries. For a bank, liquidity ratios are critical. For a manufacturer, inventory turnover matters a lot. For a software company, neither of those is very important. A table matching industries to key ratios would be helpful.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course's most lasting contribution is a practical, repeatable framework for reading and understanding financial statements. The balance sheet equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) is simple but powerful — once you really understand it, you see how every transaction affects the financial position of a company. The accounting cycle (transactions → journal entries → trial balance → adjusting entries → financial statements → closing entries) demystifies how numbers get from a sale or a purchase to the final reports. The indirect method for cash flows explains exactly why net income and cash flow are different — and why both matter. The DuPont framework takes a single number (ROE) and breaks it into a story about profit, efficiency, and leverage.

The course's weakness is that it covers a lot of ground quickly. Four modules covering the balance sheet, income statement, cash flows, and ratio analysis — with a full accounting cycle case and real company examples — means nothing gets very deep. Accrual accounting adjustments alone could be a full course. But for an introduction, the trade-off between depth and breadth is reasonable. The course gives you the tools you need to start reading financial statements and asking good questions. The deeper answers come from practice and from more advanced courses.
