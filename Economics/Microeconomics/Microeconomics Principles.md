This course teaches the fundamental principles of microeconomics — the study of how individual decision-makers (consumers and firms) allocate scarce resources. The core idea is that almost everything is a scarce resource, from oil to time to romantic partners, so economics applies to nearly every aspect of life. # Module 1 — Introduction and Basic Principles

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Economics is the study of how society allocates scarce resources. Since almost everything is scarce — from fossil fuels to time to nice guys — economics applies to nearly everything. This module introduces four foundational principles: (1) when people maximize their own interest, society often wins (the invisible hand), (2) the cost of doing something is the opportunity given up, (3) trade makes people better off, and (4) rational people take an action only when the marginal benefits exceed the marginal costs.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Economics:** The study of how society allocates scarce resources among competing uses. Not just about money — it applies to any situation with limited resources.

- **Scarcity:** The fundamental economic problem. Resources (time, money, relationships, fossil fuels) are limited, so choices must be made.

- **Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative foregone when making a choice. The true cost of something is what you give up to get it.

- **Marginal Benefit:** The additional benefit from taking one more unit of an action.

- **Marginal Cost:** The additional cost from taking one more unit of an action.

- **Net Marginal Benefit Principle (Marginal Analysis):** A rational person takes an action only when the marginal benefits exceed the marginal costs. Decisions are made on the next unit, not averages.

- **Invisible Hand Principle:** When individuals act to maximize their own net benefits, society as a whole often benefits as a result. First described by Adam Smith.

- **Trade:** Voluntary exchange of goods or services. Trade benefits both parties because each values what they receive more than what they give up.

- **Specialization:** Focusing on tasks one does best. Increases total output and efficiency.

- **Ceteris Paribus:** "All else equal." The assumption that only the factor being studied changes while everything else stays constant.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Economics is a science of everything, not just money.** The course challenges the narrow view that economics is about finance and banking. Economics applies to love, marriage, education, pollution, and daily decisions like where to park or how much to eat. This broad definition makes economics relevant to everyone.

2. **Opportunity cost is the true cost — and most people get it wrong.** The lecture shows that even economics students struggle to calculate opportunity cost correctly. Interestingly, students without formal economics training sometimes perform better, suggesting the concept is not obvious. The key is to identify the next best alternative, not just any alternative.

3. **Marginal analysis, not averages, drives good decisions.** In the example of allocating study time between two courses, the decision depends on the marginal cost of the next problem, not the average time per problem. This applies to football (pass vs. run based on next play's expected gain) and countless other situations.

4. **The invisible hand shows how self-interest can produce social good.** Adam Smith's insight is that individuals pursuing their own gain — without central coordination — often end up benefiting society. The car rental example shows how higher gas prices lead individuals to choose fuel-efficient cars, reducing fossil fuel use without government mandates.

5. **Trade creates value out of nothing.** The classroom gift exchange example is powerful: students started with random gifts (total value X). After trading, total value increased — without any new goods being produced. Trade reallocated goods to people who valued them more, creating value.

## F. Practical Implications

- Before making any decision, ask: "What am I giving up?" The true cost is not just money — it is the value of the best alternative you are not choosing.
- When deciding "how much" of something to do (study, work, exercise), focus on the next unit's costs and benefits, not the average. The decision to study one more hour depends on what that hour gives up.
- Trust that voluntary exchange benefits both parties. If someone agrees to trade, they must value what they get more than what they give up. This is true for buying a car, accepting a job, or even asking someone on a date.

# Module 2 — Supply and Demand

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Supply and demand is the hallmark framework of microeconomics. It is simple yet powerful. The demand curve shows how much buyers want at each price (downward sloping). The supply curve shows how much sellers want to sell at each price (upward sloping). Where they cross is equilibrium — the price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. This module teaches how to derive both curves, what causes them to shift, and how to use the model to predict changes in market prices and quantities.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Market:** Any place or system where buyers and sellers interact to exchange goods or services.

- **Demand:** The relationship between price and quantity demanded (how much buyers want at each price).

- **Law of Demand:** All else equal, as price increases, quantity demanded decreases (downward sloping curve).

- **Determinants of Demand:** (1) Price of the good itself, (2) Prices of related goods (complements and substitutes), (3) Consumer income, (4) Consumer preferences/tastes.

- **Change in Quantity Demanded:** Movement along the demand curve caused by a change in the good's own price.

- **Change in Demand:** A shift of the entire demand curve caused by a change in any determinant other than the good's own price.

- **Complementary Goods:** Goods consumed together (e.g., bread and tomatoes). If the price of a complement rises, demand for the other good falls.

- **Substitute Goods:** Goods consumed instead of each other (e.g., tomatoes and red bell peppers). If the price of a substitute falls, demand for the other good falls.

- **Normal Goods:** Goods for which demand increases when income increases.

- **Inferior Goods:** Goods for which demand decreases when income increases (e.g., ramen noodles, public transportation).

- **Supply:** The relationship between price and quantity supplied (how much sellers want to sell at each price).

- **Law of Supply:** All else equal, as price increases, quantity supplied increases (upward sloping curve).

- **Determinants of Supply:** (1) Price of the good itself, (2) Prices of inputs (costs), (3) Technology.

- **Change in Quantity Supplied:** Movement along the supply curve caused by a change in the good's own price.

- **Change in Supply:** A shift of the entire supply curve caused by a change in input prices or technology.

- **Equilibrium:** The price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. The market settles here naturally.

- **Surplus:** When price is above equilibrium, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. Sellers lower prices to sell excess.

- **Shortage:** When price is below equilibrium, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. Sellers raise prices to ration demand.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Demand reflects value; supply reflects cost.** The demand curve shows how much buyers value each unit (the highest price they would pay). The supply curve shows the minimum price sellers need to cover costs. Equilibrium balances these two.

2. **A change in price changes quantity demanded (movement along the curve), not demand (no shift).** This is the most common mistake in using supply and demand. When the price of tomatoes rises, people buy fewer tomatoes — but the demand curve does not move. The demand schedule remains the same; we are just at a different point on it.

3. **Only four things shift demand: price of related goods, income, preferences, and number of buyers.** That is it. Everything else (including the good's own price) causes movement along the curve, not a shift.

4. **Only three things shift supply: input prices, technology, and number of sellers.** When input costs rise, supply shifts left (less at every price). When technology improves, supply shifts right (more at every price).

5. **Markets naturally move toward equilibrium.** If price is too high, surplus pushes it down. If too low, shortage pushes it up. No central planner needed — the invisible hand works through prices.

6. **When both supply and demand shift, price or quantity may be ambiguous.** If supply shifts left (price up, quantity down) and demand shifts left (price down, quantity down), quantity definitely goes down, but price could go either way depending on which shift is larger. The model's predictive power is limited without knowing the magnitude of shifts.

---

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Demand Curve | Downward sloping graph of price vs. quantity demanded | To show how much buyers want at each price |
| Supply Curve | Upward sloping graph of price vs. quantity supplied | To show how much sellers want to sell at each price |
| Equilibrium | Where supply and demand cross | To find market price and quantity |
| Comparative Statics | Analyze how equilibrium changes with shifts | To predict effects of market changes |
| Shift vs. Movement | Shift = curve moves; Movement = along curve | To avoid the most common mistake |

- Supply equation (linear): Qs = c + dP
- Equilibrium: Qd = Qs

---

## F. Practical Implications

- To predict what happens to price and quantity when something changes, first identify whether it affects demand or supply. Then determine which direction the curve shifts. Then trace the effect on equilibrium.

- If a change affects both supply and demand (e.g., bad weather and higher gas prices), you can sometimes determine quantity but not price — the direction of price change depends on which shift is larger.

- The farmers' market example shows the power of the model. Higher consumer value (demand right) and higher small-farm costs (supply left) both push prices up. This explains why farmer's market tomatoes cost more than grocery store tomatoes.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The romantic relationships example is clever but also a bit gimmicky. The underlying point is solid: any market with buyers and sellers (or "seekers" and "providers") has supply and demand dynamics. The "price" is not always money — it can be effort, time, or social capital.

Shifting the curve is different from moving along it. Yet in casual conversation, people say "demand went up" when they mean "quantity demanded went up" — which is fine in casual talk but wrong in economic modeling.

The farmers' market example is excellent because it shows both sides of the market. Consumers value the experience more (demand right). Small farmers have higher costs (supply left). Both push prices up. The model organizes this intuition clearly.

# Module 3 — Market Efficiency and Government Policies

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Free markets are not just about prices — they also create value for consumers and producers. This value is called consumer surplus and producer surplus. Under perfect competition, the market maximizes total surplus (consumer + producer). This is a powerful argument for markets. But governments sometimes intervene with price controls (ceilings and floors). These interventions create deadweight loss — value that is lost to both buyers and sellers. This module teaches how to measure surplus, how to see the efficiency of markets, and how to analyze the costs of government policies.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Consumer Surplus (CS):** The difference between what a consumer is willing to pay and what they actually pay. Measures the benefit consumers get from the market.

- **Producer Surplus (PS):** The difference between what a producer is paid and the minimum they would accept to produce. Measures the benefit producers get from the market.

- **Total Surplus (Social Welfare):** Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus. Under perfect competition, total surplus is maximized at equilibrium.

- **Deadweight Loss (DWL):** The loss of total surplus that occurs when a market is not at equilibrium (due to price controls, taxes, monopoly, etc.). Value that is created by the market but lost due to intervention.

- **Price Ceiling:** A maximum price set below equilibrium. Creates a shortage (quantity demanded > quantity supplied). Examples: rent control, price gouging laws.

- **Price Floor:** A minimum price set above equilibrium. Creates a surplus (quantity supplied > quantity demanded). Examples: minimum wage, agricultural price supports.

- **Binding Constraint:** A price ceiling below equilibrium or price floor above equilibrium actually affects the market. Non-binding constraints (ceiling above equilibrium or floor below) have no effect.

- **Tax Incidence (Burden):** How the burden of a tax is divided between buyers and sellers. Does not depend on who legally pays the tax — depends on elasticities.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Free markets maximize total surplus under perfect competition.** At equilibrium, the market produces every unit where the buyer's willingness to pay exceeds the seller's cost. No other allocation can produce higher total surplus. This is the efficiency argument for markets.

2. **Price ceilings below equilibrium create shortages and deadweight loss.** Rent control is the classic example. At the controlled price, more people want apartments (high demand) but fewer landlords want to supply them (low supply). The result is a shortage, waiting lists, and lost surplus from units that are not produced but should be.

3. **Price floors above equilibrium create surpluses and deadweight loss.** The minimum wage is the classic example. At a wage above equilibrium, more workers want jobs (high supply) but fewer employers want to hire (low demand). The result is unemployment (surplus of workers).

4. **Price ceilings also create non-price rationing.** When price cannot allocate goods, something else must. Under rent control, apartments go to the first applicants, friends of landlords, or those who pay bribes (key money). These allocation methods are often less efficient and less fair than price.

5. **The legal incidence of a tax (who writes the check) is not the economic incidence (who bears the burden).** A tax on sellers raises their costs, shifting supply left. Price rises, so buyers pay more. A tax on buyers reduces their willingness to pay, shifting demand left. Price falls, so sellers receive less. In both cases, the tax burden is shared between buyers and sellers based on elasticities, not the law.

---

| Consumer Surplus | Area below demand curve and above price | To measure consumer benefits from a market |
| Producer Surplus | Area above supply curve and below price | To measure producer benefits from a market |
| Total Surplus | CS + PS | To measure total value created |
| Deadweight Loss | Surplus lost due to market interference | To measure the cost of price controls, taxes, monopoly |
| Binding vs. Non-Binding | Ceiling below equilibrium binds; floor above binds | To know if a policy actually affects the market |
| Tax Incidence | Who actually pays the tax | To analyze tax policy |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Consumer Surplus (triangle area):** CS = 0.5 × (Base) × (Height) when demand is linear

- **Producer Surplus (triangle area):** PS = 0.5 × (Base) × (Height) when supply is linear

- **Total Surplus:** TS = CS + PS

- **Deadweight Loss:** DWL = Surplus lost from equilibrium

- **Tax Revenue:** Tax × Quantity sold after tax

---

## F. Practical Implications

- When evaluating a price control, first check if it binds. A minimum wage of $7.25 when the equilibrium wage is $10 is binding. A minimum wage of $15 when equilibrium is $10 is binding. A minimum wage of $8 when equilibrium is $10 is not binding — it has no effect.

- Price ceilings create shortages. If you are considering rent control, you must accept that people will have trouble finding apartments. The price is lower, but availability is lower too.

- Price floors create surpluses. If you support a higher minimum wage, you must accept that some low-skill workers may lose jobs or hours.

- When a tax is imposed, do not assume the group that writes the check bears the whole burden. Companies often say "we will not raise prices" after a tax — but if demand is inelastic, they may have to. The market decides.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The efficiency of markets is a powerful insight, but it depends on strong assumptions: perfect competition, no externalities, no public goods, perfect information, no transaction costs. When these assumptions fail, markets may not maximize total surplus. This is not a reason to reject markets — it is a reason to understand when they work and when they need help.

Rent control is the classic example of well-intentioned policy with bad consequences. The goal is affordable housing. The result is often fewer apartments (landlords convert to condos), deteriorating quality (no incentive to maintain), and misallocation (apartments go to people who do not value them most). Economists almost universally oppose rent control — not because they do not care about poor people, but because they know the policy backfires.

The minimum wage is more controversial among economists. The simple supply-demand model predicts job loss for low-skill workers. But empirical evidence is mixed — small increases may have little or no negative employment effect because labor markets are not perfectly competitive (monopsony). The course does not go into this nuance, but it is worth knowing.

# Module 4 — Elasticities

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Supply and demand tell us the direction of change — prices go up or down, quantity goes up or down. But how much? That is what elasticity measures. Elasticity is a measure of responsiveness. Price elasticity of demand tells us how much quantity demanded changes when price changes. This matters for businesses (total revenue) and for government policy (tax incidence). This module covers own-price elasticity of demand, cross-price elasticity, income elasticity, and price elasticity of supply.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Own-Price Elasticity of Demand (Ed):** The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. Measures how responsive demand is to price changes.

- **Elastic Demand:** |Ed| > 1. Quantity demanded changes more than price. Example: luxury goods, goods with many substitutes.

- **Inelastic Demand:** |Ed| < 1. Quantity demanded changes less than price. Example: necessities, goods with few substitutes (insulin, gasoline).

- **Unit Elastic Demand:** |Ed| = 1. Quantity demanded changes exactly as much as price. Total revenue is maximized at this point.

- **Perfectly Inelastic Demand:** Ed = 0. Quantity does not change at all when price changes. Vertical demand curve.

- **Perfectly Elastic Demand:** Ed = infinity. Any price increase causes quantity to drop to zero. Horizontal demand curve.

- **Total Revenue Test:** If demand is elastic, a price decrease increases total revenue. If demand is inelastic, a price increase increases total revenue.

- **Determinants of Elasticity of Demand:** (1) Availability of substitutes, (2) Necessity vs. luxury, (3) Definition of the market (narrow vs. broad), (4) Time horizon (more elastic in long run).

- **Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand:** Percentage change in quantity demanded of good A divided by percentage change in price of good B. Positive for substitutes, negative for complements.

- **Income Elasticity of Demand:** Percentage change in quantity demanded divided by percentage change in income. Positive for normal goods, negative for inferior goods.

- **Price Elasticity of Supply:** Percentage change in quantity supplied divided by percentage change in price. Depends on how easily firms can increase production.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Elasticity determines whether a price increase raises or lowers total revenue.** This is critical for business pricing decisions. If demand is inelastic (|Ed| < 1), raising price increases total revenue (quantity falls by less than price rises). If demand is elastic (|Ed| > 1), raising price decreases total revenue (quantity falls by more than price rises). Many managers raise prices without knowing elasticity — they are flying blind.

2. **The more substitutes, the more elastic demand.** Gasoline has few substitutes in the short run (inelastic). A specific brand of soda has many substitutes (elastic). This explains why luxury brands have more pricing power — they have created perceived uniqueness (fewer substitutes).

3. **Elasticity is higher in the long run than the short run.** When gas prices rise, people cannot change their cars immediately (inelastic). Over time, they buy more fuel-efficient cars, move closer to work, or take public transit (more elastic). Time matters.

4. **Cross-price elasticity tells you if goods are substitutes or complements.** Positive cross-price elasticity (price of A up, demand for B up) means substitutes. Negative cross-price elasticity (price of A up, demand for B down) means complements. This is how antitrust authorities define markets.

5. **Elasticity of supply depends on flexibility.** Perishable goods (fresh tomatoes) have inelastic supply in the short run — farmers cannot produce more overnight. Manufactured goods (cars) have more elastic supply — factories can increase shifts.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Price Elasticity of Demand | (%ΔQd) / (%ΔP) | To measure responsiveness to price changes |
| Total Revenue Test | If elastic → lower price raises revenue; if inelastic → higher price raises revenue | For business pricing decisions |
| Cross-Price Elasticity | (%ΔQd of A) / (%ΔP of B) | To identify substitutes and complements |
| Income Elasticity | (%ΔQd) / (%ΔY) | To identify normal vs. inferior goods |
| Price Elasticity of Supply | (%ΔQs) / (%ΔP) | To measure producer responsiveness |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Price Elasticity of Demand (midpoint formula):** Ed = [(Q2 - Q1) / ((Q1 + Q2)/2)] / [(P2 - P1) / ((P1 + P2)/2)]

- **Total Revenue:** TR = P × Q

- **Cross-Price Elasticity:** Exy = (%ΔQx) / (%ΔPy)

- **Income Elasticity:** Ey = (%ΔQ) / (%ΔY)

- **Price Elasticity of Supply:** Es = (%ΔQs) / (%ΔP)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before changing your price, estimate your demand elasticity. If you do not know whether demand is elastic or inelastic, you cannot predict whether revenue will go up or down. Run a small price experiment.

- Luxury goods (elastic demand) should have lower prices to increase revenue. Necessities (inelastic demand) can have higher prices without losing much quantity.

- Taxes fall more heavily on the side of the market that is less elastic. If demand is inelastic and supply is elastic, consumers bear most of the tax burden. If demand is elastic and supply is inelastic, producers bear most of the burden.

- When analyzing a market, check cross-price elasticities to see what other products compete with yours. A high cross-price elasticity means customers will switch if your competitor changes price.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The total revenue test is one of the most practically useful ideas in the course. Many business owners think raising prices always increases revenue — but that is only true if demand is inelastic. If demand is elastic, raising prices lowers revenue. This is not obvious to non-economists, which is why companies sometimes make costly pricing mistakes.

The midpoint formula is important because elasticity between two points on a demand curve depends on which direction you are calculating (from high to low vs. low to high). The midpoint formula solves this by using averages. The course correctly uses this standard method.

The connection between elasticity and tax incidence is important for policy. If the government wants to tax a product without hurting consumers, it should choose products with inelastic demand (addictive goods, necessities). But that is also regressive — poor people spend a larger share of income on necessities. There is a trade-off between efficiency (minimize deadweight loss) and equity (fairness).

# Module 5 — Production and Costs

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Before a firm can decide how much to produce, it needs to understand its costs. This module covers the relationship between inputs (labor, capital) and output (the production function), and the relationship between output and costs (cost curves). The key insight is that costs are not fixed — they change with output in predictable ways. Short-run costs include fixed costs (do not change with output) and variable costs (change with output). Long-run costs have no fixed costs — all costs are variable. Understanding cost curves is essential for analyzing firm behavior in competitive and non-competitive markets.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Production Function:** The relationship between inputs (labor, capital, materials) and output. Q = f(L, K).

- **Short Run:** A period in which at least one input is fixed (usually capital — plant size, equipment). Firms cannot fully adjust.

- **Long Run:** A period in which all inputs are variable. Firms can adjust everything.

- **Fixed Costs (FC):** Costs that do not change with output. Even if Q = 0, FC remain. Examples: rent, insurance, loan payments.

- **Variable Costs (VC):** Costs that change with output. If Q = 0, VC = 0. Examples: labor, materials, electricity.

- **Total Costs (TC):** FC + VC.

- **Marginal Cost (MC):** The additional cost of producing one more unit. ΔTC / ΔQ. The most important cost concept for decision-making.

- **Average Fixed Cost (AFC):** FC / Q. Declines as output increases (spreading the fixed cost).

- **Average Variable Cost (AVC):** VC / Q.

- **Average Total Cost (ATC):** TC / Q = AFC + AVC. U-shaped curve.

- **Economic Profit:** Total Revenue — Total Costs (including opportunity costs). Different from accounting profit (which excludes opportunity costs).

- **Accounting Profit:** Total Revenue — Explicit Costs (actual cash outlays).

- **Normal Profit:** The minimum profit needed to keep a firm in business. Equal to the opportunity cost of the owner's time and capital. Part of economic costs.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Marginal cost is the most important cost for decisions.** Fixed costs are sunk in the short run — they should not affect decisions about how much to produce. The decision to produce one more unit depends only on whether the price exceeds marginal cost.

2. **Average total cost is U-shaped.** At low output, AFC is high (spreading fixed costs over few units), so ATC is high. As output increases, AFC falls (spreading) and AVC may fall (learning, specialization). But eventually, diminishing returns set in — AVC rises, pulling ATC up. The bottom of the ATC curve is the most efficient scale.

3. **Marginal cost crosses average cost at the minimum of average cost.** This is a mathematical identity. When MC < ATC, ATC is falling. When MC > ATC, ATC is rising. When MC = ATC, ATC is at its minimum. This relationship is true for both ATC and AVC.

4. **Economic profit includes opportunity costs.** A business that earns $100,000 accounting profit might have $80,000 in opportunity costs (the owner could earn $80,000 elsewhere). Economic profit is only $20,000. If economic profit is zero, the owner is earning exactly what they could earn elsewhere — this is "normal profit" and is fine.

5. **Short-run cost curves determine long-run cost curves.** In the long run, firms can choose their plant size. For each possible plant size, there is a short-run ATC curve. The long-run ATC curve is the envelope of all short-run ATC curves — the lowest possible cost for each output level.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Production Function | Q = f(L, K) | To understand how inputs become outputs |
| Short-Run vs. Long-Run | Fixed vs. variable inputs | To know what costs can change |
| Cost Curves (MC, ATC, AVC, AFC) | Graphs of costs vs. output | To analyze firm cost structure |
| MC-ATC Relationship | MC crosses ATC at minimum | To find efficient scale |
| Economic vs. Accounting Profit | Include opportunity costs or not | To evaluate true profitability |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Total Cost:** TC = FC + VC

- **Marginal Cost:** MC = ΔTC / ΔQ

- **Average Total Cost:** ATC = TC / Q = AFC + AVC

- **Average Fixed Cost:** AFC = FC / Q

- **Average Variable Cost:** AVC = VC / Q

- **Accounting Profit:** TR - Explicit Costs

- **Economic Profit:** TR - (Explicit Costs + Implicit Costs) = TR - Economic Costs

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Do not let fixed costs influence short-run production decisions. The factory rent is paid whether you produce or not. The question is: does the price you get cover the variable cost of producing one more unit? If yes, produce.

- The bottom of the ATC curve is your most efficient scale. If you are producing to the left of that point, you could lower average costs by expanding. If you are to the right, you are experiencing diseconomies of scale.

- Economic profit is the right measure for long-run decisions. If you are earning positive accounting profit but zero economic profit, you are doing fine — you are earning exactly your opportunity cost. If economic profit is negative, you should consider exiting the industry.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The relationship between marginal cost and average cost is not obvious to non-economists. Your average grade in a class and your next exam grade have the same relationship: if your next grade is above your average, your average goes up. If below, your average goes down. Marginal pulls average. This intuition is useful beyond economics.

The distinction between short-run and long-run is sometimes confusing. The key is what can change. In the short run, you cannot build a new factory. In the long run, you can. Most real-world decisions fall somewhere in between — you can change some things but not everything. The model simplifies this to two extremes for clarity.

Economic profit is the right measure for understanding whether a business should exist. If a firm earns zero economic profit, it is covering all costs, including opportunity costs. That is a fine outcome — it means resources are earning exactly what they would earn elsewhere. Persistent positive economic profit attracts entry.

# Module 6 — Competitive Output

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Perfect competition is a market structure with many small firms selling identical products, with no barriers to entry. No single firm can affect the market price — each firm is a price taker. This module covers how competitive firms decide how much to produce in the short run (shut down if price falls below AVC) and in the long run (exit if price falls below ATC, entry if price is above ATC). The key insight is that in long-run equilibrium, competitive firms earn zero economic profit — price equals minimum ATC.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Perfect Competition:** Market structure with (1) many small firms, (2) identical products, (3) no barriers to entry, (4) perfect information. Firms are price takers.

- **Price Taker:** A firm that cannot affect the market price. It can sell as much as it wants at the market price, but cannot charge more (customers will go elsewhere) and has no reason to charge less (it can sell all it wants at the market price).

- **Marginal Revenue (MR):** The additional revenue from selling one more unit. For a price taker, MR = Price.

- **Profit-Maximizing Rule:** Produce where MR = MC. For a price taker, produce where P = MC.

- **Shutdown Decision (Short Run):** Shut down if P < AVC (minimum). If P is between AVC and ATC, produce at a loss (cover variable costs and some fixed costs).

- **Exit Decision (Long Run):** Exit if P < ATC (minimum). If P > ATC, firms earn economic profit, attracting entry.

- **Short-Run Supply Curve of a Firm:** The portion of MC curve above AVC.

- **Long-Run Supply Curve of a Firm:** The portion of MC curve above ATC (but in long-run equilibrium, price returns to minimum ATC).

- **Entry and Exit:** Positive economic profit attracts new firms (entry), which increases supply, lowers price, and erodes profits. Negative economic profit causes firms to exit, decreasing supply, raising price, and eliminating losses.

- **Long-Run Equilibrium:** Price = Minimum ATC, and firms earn zero economic profit.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Competitive firms are price takers — they cannot set prices.** This is a strong assumption that fits some markets (agriculture, stock market) but not most. The value is as a benchmark. If a market is perfectly competitive, the firm's only decision is how much to produce.

2. **The profit-maximizing quantity is where MR = MC.** Since MR = P for price takers, this simplifies to P = MC. If P > MC, producing one more unit adds more revenue than cost — do it. If P < MC, the last unit cost more than it earned — reduce output.

3. **Fixed costs do not affect short-run production decisions.** In the short run, fixed costs are sunk. The decision to produce or shut down depends only on whether price covers AVC. If P > AVC, producing at a loss is better than shutting down (you cover some fixed costs). If P < AVC, shut down.

4. **Entry and exit drive long-run equilibrium to zero economic profit.** If firms earn positive economic profit, new firms enter, increasing supply, lowering price, and eliminating profits. If firms earn losses, firms exit, decreasing supply, raising price, and eliminating losses. In long-run equilibrium, P = minimum ATC.

5. **The long-run supply curve can be flat (constant cost industry) or upward sloping (increasing cost industry).** If input prices do not change as the industry expands (constant cost), the long-run supply curve is horizontal at minimum ATC. If input prices rise as the industry expands (increasing cost), the long-run supply curve slopes upward.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| MR = MC Rule | Produce where marginal revenue equals marginal cost | For any profit-maximizing firm |
| P = MC (for price takers) | Special case of MR = MC | For competitive firms |
| Shutdown Condition | Shut down if P < min AVC | Short-run decision |
| Exit Condition | Exit if P < min ATC | Long-run decision |
| Entry Condition | Entry if P > min ATC | Long-run decision |
| Long-Run Equilibrium | P = min ATC, zero economic profit | Long-run prediction |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Marginal Revenue (price taker):** MR = P

- **Profit Maximization (price taker):** P = MC

- **Profit (per unit):** (P - ATC) × Q

- **Shutdown Condition (short run):** Shut down if P < min AVC

- **Exit Condition (long run):** Exit if P < min ATC

- **Entry Condition (long run):** Entry if P > min ATC

- **Long-Run Equilibrium:** P = min ATC

---

## F. Practical Implications

- If you are in a competitive market, you have no pricing power. Focus on minimizing costs and finding the most efficient scale. Your only decision is how much to produce.

- In the short run, do not shut down just because you are losing money. If price covers variable costs, you are better off producing than shutting down (you lose less). Shut down only if price does not cover AVC.

- In the long run, if you cannot cover ATC, exit the industry. Staying in with losses is not sustainable.

- Watch for entry. If you are earning high profits in a competitive industry, new firms will enter. Your profits will be competed away. The only way to sustain profits is to create barriers to entry (brand, patents, scale).

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

Perfect competition is an idealized benchmark, not a description of most real markets. Very few products are truly identical (even wheat has different grades). But the model is useful as a starting point. If a market is close to competitive, the predictions hold approximately.

The shutdown condition (P < AVC) is important for understanding business decisions. During a downturn, many firms operate at a loss. This is not irrational — as long as price covers variable costs, they lose less than shutting down. The moment price falls below AVC, they shut down.

The entry/exit dynamic is the market's self-correcting mechanism. High profits attract entry, which lowers prices and erodes profits. Losses cause exit, which raises prices and eliminates losses. This is why competitive markets tend toward zero economic profit. It is not that firms are unlucky — it is that competition eliminates excess returns.

# Module 7 — Firms with Market Power

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Perfect competition is one extreme. The opposite extreme is monopoly — a single firm selling a unique product with high barriers to entry. Between these extremes is monopolistic competition (many firms, differentiated products) and oligopoly (few firms, strategic behavior). This module covers how firms with market power set prices above marginal cost, earn positive economic profit (even in long run), and create deadweight loss. The key insight is that market power allows firms to charge more than competitive price, reducing quantity and creating inefficiency.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Market Power:** The ability to charge a price above marginal cost. Comes from barriers to entry and product differentiation.

- **Monopoly:** A market with a single seller, no close substitutes, and high barriers to entry. The firm is the market.

- **Natural Monopoly:** A monopoly that arises because one firm can produce the entire market output at lower cost than multiple firms. Example: utilities (water, electricity).

- **Barriers to Entry:** Things that prevent new firms from entering a market. Examples: patents, licenses, economies of scale, control of key resources.

- **Marginal Revenue for a Monopolist:** Unlike a competitive firm, a monopolist faces a downward-sloping demand curve. To sell more, it must lower price on all units. So MR < P.

- **Monopolist's Profit-Maximizing Rule:** Produce where MR = MC, then charge the highest price consumers will pay for that quantity (from the demand curve).

- **Deadweight Loss (DWL) from Monopoly:** The loss of consumer surplus that is not captured by producer surplus. Value that would be created under competition but is lost because monopoly restricts quantity.

- **Price Discrimination:** Charging different prices to different customers for the same product. Requires (1) market power, (2) ability to identify different customer groups, (3) ability to prevent resale.

- **Perfect Price Discrimination (First Degree):** Charging each customer their maximum willingness to pay. Eliminates consumer surplus (all becomes producer surplus) and eliminates deadweight loss.

- **Monopolistic Competition:** Many firms selling differentiated products, with low barriers to entry. Each firm has some market power (differentiation) but entry erodes profits.

- **Oligopoly:** A market with a few firms, where each firm's actions affect the others. Strategic behavior (game theory) is required.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Monopolists restrict quantity to raise price.** Unlike a competitive firm that takes price as given, a monopolist chooses both price and quantity. It reduces quantity below the competitive level, which raises price above marginal cost. This is how it earns economic profit.

2. **Monopolist's marginal revenue is less than price.** To sell an additional unit, the monopolist must lower price on all units. So the revenue from the last unit (MR) is less than the price of that unit. This is why the MR curve is below the demand curve.

3. **Monopoly creates deadweight loss.** Compared to perfect competition, monopoly produces less output at a higher price. The lost consumer surplus is partly captured as producer surplus, but some is lost entirely — deadweight loss. This is the inefficiency of monopoly.

4. **Price discrimination can reduce deadweight loss.** If a monopolist can charge different prices to different customers (e.g., student discounts, senior discounts, airline tickets), it can capture more consumer surplus and increase output. Perfect price discrimination eliminates deadweight loss entirely (though consumer surplus becomes zero).

5. **Monopolistic competition leads to excess capacity.** In long-run equilibrium, each firm produces at a point where ATC is still falling (left of minimum ATC). This is called excess capacity — firms could lower average costs by producing more, but they cannot because demand is limited.

6. **Oligopoly requires strategic thinking.** With only a few firms, each firm's decision affects the others. Game theory (prisoner's dilemma, Nash equilibrium) is needed to analyze outcomes. Collusion (acting like a monopoly) is profitable but unstable because each firm has an incentive to cheat.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Monopoly Pricing | MR = MC, then price from demand curve | To find monopoly price and quantity |
| Deadweight Loss from Monopoly | Area between demand and MC from Qm to Qc | To measure inefficiency of market power |
| Price Discrimination | Different prices to different customers | To capture more consumer surplus |
| Monopolistic Competition Long Run | P = ATC, but P > MC, excess capacity | To analyze differentiated product markets |
| Oligopoly / Game Theory | Strategic interaction between few firms | To analyze markets with few competitors |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Monopolist's MR (linear demand):** If demand is P = a - bQ, then MR = a - 2bQ

- **Profit Maximization (monopoly):** MR = MC

- **Lerner Index (measure of market power):** (P - MC) / P = 1 / |Ed| (more market power when demand is less elastic)

- **Deadweight Loss (monopoly):** 0.5 × (Pm - Pc) × (Qc - Qm)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- If you have market power, your price should be higher than MC. How much higher depends on elasticity. The Lerner Index says (P - MC)/P = 1/|Ed|. More inelastic demand means more pricing power.

- Price discrimination is not always illegal. Senior discounts, student discounts, coupons, and airline ticket pricing are all price discrimination. The key legal restriction is that you cannot discriminate based on race, gender, religion, etc.

- If you are in monopolistic competition (many competitors but differentiated products), your long-run profits will be zero. Focus on differentiation to keep demand high and costs low. Innovate constantly because your advantage will be eroded by entry.

- In oligopoly, be careful about price wars. If you cut price, competitors will follow. You may end up at competitive prices (P = MC) even with only a few firms. Collusion is illegal in most countries.

---

- [x] Find the monopoly price and quantity (MR = MC)

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The MR < P relationship is the key to monopoly. Many people assume a monopolist can just charge whatever it wants — but that is wrong. The monopolist faces the same demand curve as everyone else. To sell more, it must lower price. The trade-off between selling more units (good) and getting less revenue on existing units (bad) is captured by MR.

Price discrimination is more common than most people realize. Every coupon, student discount, and airline ticket is price discrimination. The airline example is especially clear: business travelers pay more (inelastic demand), leisure travelers pay less (elastic demand). Same flight, same cost, different prices. This is legal and efficient — it increases output compared to uniform pricing.

The Lerner Index is a neat formula. It connects market power to elasticity. If demand is perfectly elastic (|Ed| = infinity), then (P - MC)/P = 0, so P = MC — no market power. If demand is very inelastic (|Ed| close to 0), then (P - MC)/P is large — high market power. This matches intuition.

# Module 8 — Public Goods, Common Resources, and Externalities

## A. Core Idea of the Module

The invisible hand works well for private goods — goods that are excludable (sellers can prevent use) and rival (one person's use reduces availability). But not all goods have these properties. Public goods (non-excludable, non-rival) lead to free riding and underprovision. Common resources (non-excludable but rival) lead to overuse (tragedy of the commons). Externalities occur when one person's actions affect others without going through markets (pollution, education). This module covers these market failures and possible government solutions: taxes, subsidies, regulation, and cap-and-trade.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Excludable:** Sellers can prevent people from using the good if they do not pay.

- **Rival:** One person's use reduces availability for others.

- **Private Goods:** Excludable and rival. Examples: food, clothing, cars. Markets work well.

- **Public Goods:** Non-excludable and non-rival. Examples: national defense, street lighting, basic research. Markets underprovide because of free riding.

- **Free Rider Problem:** People can enjoy the benefits of a public good without paying. No one has an incentive to pay, so the good is not provided at all or underprovided.

- **Common Resources:** Non-excludable but rival. Examples: fish in the ocean, clean air, public grazing land. Markets overuse because no one has an incentive to conserve.

- **Tragedy of the Commons:** Overuse of a common resource because each user ignores the cost they impose on others.

- **Externalities:** Costs or benefits of an action that affect people not directly involved in the transaction. Can be positive (benefits to others) or negative (costs to others).

- **Negative Externality:** The social cost (private + external) is greater than the private cost. Example: pollution.

- **Positive Externality:** The social benefit (private + external) is greater than the private benefit. Example: education, vaccination.

- **Internalizing the Externality:** Changing incentives so that decision-makers face the full social costs or benefits. Example: pollution tax.

- **Pigouvian Tax:** A tax on a negative externality equal to the external cost. Brings private cost up to social cost.

- **Cap and Trade:** A system that sets a total limit on pollution (cap) and allows firms to buy and sell permits to pollute (trade). Creates a market for pollution.

- **Coase Theorem:** If property rights are clearly defined and transaction costs are low, private bargaining can solve externalities without government intervention.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Public goods are underprovided by markets.** Because of free riding, no one has an incentive to pay for a public good. The government provides national defense, street lights, and basic research because markets would not provide enough (or anything at all).

2. **Common resources are overused.** The tragedy of the commons occurs because each user gets the full benefit of using the resource but shares only a fraction of the cost. Without regulation, the resource is depleted.

3. **Negative externalities lead to overproduction.** A factory that pollutes imposes costs on people who are not involved in the transaction. The private cost (to the factory) is less than the social cost (to everyone). The market produces too much.

4. **Positive externalities lead to underproduction.** Education benefits not just the student but also society (lower crime, higher productivity). The private benefit is less than the social benefit. The market produces too little.

5. **Pigouvian taxes correct negative externalities.** A tax equal to the external cost forces the producer to face the full social cost. This reduces quantity to the socially optimal level.

6. **Cap-and-trade is an alternative to taxes.** Setting a total cap on pollution and allowing firms to trade permits creates a market for pollution. Firms with low abatement costs reduce pollution and sell permits; firms with high abatement costs buy permits. The cap ensures the total pollution target is met.

7. **The Coase Theorem shows that private solutions are possible.** If property rights are clear and transaction costs are low, people can bargain to solve externalities. Example: a farmer and a rancher can agree on fencing regardless of who has the legal right. But in many real cases (air pollution, climate change), transaction costs are too high.

---

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Social Cost:** SMC = PMC + External Cost

- **Social Benefit:** SMB = PMB + External Benefit

- **Optimal Quantity (negative externality):** SMB = SMC (where SMC includes external cost)

- **Pigouvian Tax:** T = External Cost at optimal quantity

- **Deadweight Loss from Externality:** Area between SMC and PMC from market quantity to optimal quantity

---

## F. Practical Implications

- If you are a policymaker, do not assume markets always work. For public goods, provide them directly or subsidize private provision. For common resources, regulate use or create property rights.

- For negative externalities, taxes and cap-and-trade are more efficient than command-and-control regulation. Both create incentives to reduce pollution at the lowest cost. Command-and-control (requiring specific technology) does not.

- For positive externalities, subsidies or direct provision can increase quantity to the social optimum. Education, vaccines, and research are examples.

- The Coase Theorem suggests that sometimes the best solution is to do nothing — let people bargain. But transaction costs must be low. For air pollution and climate change, they are not.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The public goods problem is one of the strongest arguments for government intervention. No private company will provide national defense because they cannot exclude non-payers. Basic research (scientific knowledge) is similar — once discovered, it is hard to keep others from using it. This is why government funding for science is important.

The tragedy of the commons is a powerful metaphor for many environmental problems. Overfishing, air pollution, climate change — all are tragedies of the commons. Each individual has an incentive to take more (fish more, pollute more) because the cost is shared. The solution is to create property rights (fishing quotas, pollution permits) or regulate use.

The Coase Theorem is a beautiful idea but often not practical. For two neighbors with a dispute over noise, bargaining can work. For thousands of firms emitting carbon into the global atmosphere, bargaining cannot work. Transaction costs are too high, and property rights are not clearly defined. This is why we need government policy for climate change.
