# Module 1 — Overview of Risk Management

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Risk is defined as the potential for deviation from an expected result — the probability that outcomes differ from expectations. Risk management is divided into three main disciplines: market risk (asset prices, interest rates), credit risk (counterparty default), and operational risk (people, processes, systems, external events). Enterprise risk management (ERM) integrates these disciplines for a holistic view aligned with corporate strategy. Risks are interconnected — COVID-19 began as operational risk (health and safety), led to market risk (asset price corrections), and then credit risk (contagion, counterparty default).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Risk:** Potential for deviation from expected result. Probability that outcomes differ from expectations.

- **Market Risk:** Risk related to asset prices, interest rates, foreign exchange, and commodity prices. Primarily quantitative.

- **Credit Risk:** Risk of counterparty default. The probability that a counterparty will fail to honor its financial obligations.

- **Operational Risk:** Risk related to people, processes, systems, and external events (e.g., COVID-19, cyberattacks, fraud).

- **Enterprise Risk Management (ERM):** Process that aligns risk management with corporate strategy. Integrates market, credit, and operational risks for a holistic view.

- **Risk Classification (Known vs. Unknown):**
  - **Known knowns:** known probabilities and impacts
  - **Known unknowns:** risks that exist but have uncertain timing
  - **Unknown unknowns:** completely random events

- **Risk Management Process:** Identify risks → assess probability and impact → decide to accept or avoid → continuously monitor.

- **Liquidity Risk:** Risk related to speed and cost of converting assets to cash.
  - **Asset liquidity risk:** difficulty selling assets without affecting price
  - **Funding liquidity risk:** inability to meet liabilities without losses

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Risk management is not about minimizing risk** — it is about achieving a good risk-adjusted return for the risk taken.

2. **Risks are interconnected, not isolated.** COVID-19 started as operational risk (health), led to market risk (asset corrections), then credit risk (counterparty default).

3. **Risk management is dynamic and forward-looking.** It aims not only to avoid risks but also to anticipate and sometimes pursue them for good risk-adjusted returns.

4. **Liquidity risk is critical but hard to measure.** The Basel Committee has not set formal capital charges due to measurement challenges.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Three Risk Disciplines | Market, Credit, Operational | Categorizing risks |
| ERM (Enterprise Risk Management) | Integrates risks with corporate strategy | Holistic risk view |
| Known/Unknown Classification | Known knowns, known unknowns, unknown unknowns | Risk identification |
| Liquidity Risk Management | Lines of credit, securitization, contingency funding | Managing liquidity |

## F. Practical Implications

- Risk management is not just about avoiding loss — it is about taking smart risks for good returns.

- Do not treat risks in isolation. A single event can trigger multiple risk types (operational → market → credit).

- Liquidity risk requires forecasting daily cash flows, analyzing funding sources, and preparing contingency plans.

# Module 2 — Types of Business and Financial Risks

## A. Core Idea of the Module

This module covers market risk (quantitative, using statistical measures like standard deviation and Value at Risk), credit risk (probability of default, exposure at default, loss given default, maturity), operational risk (qualitative, firm-specific, affecting people, processes, systems), and enterprise risk (integrating all disciplines with corporate strategy). Key concepts include portfolio construction, diversification (systematic vs. unsystematic risk), the efficient frontier, and the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Value at Risk (VaR):** Estimates potential loss at a given confidence level under a normal distribution.

- **Normal Distribution:** Bell-shaped curve centered at mean. About 68% of observations within one standard deviation.

- **Log Normal Distribution:** Skewed right because asset prices cannot go negative (though exceptions like negative oil futures occurred in 2020).

- **Sharpe Ratio:** Measures total risk-adjusted return.

- **Correlation Coefficient:** Ranges from +1 (perfect positive) to -1 (perfect negative). Measures how assets relate.

- **Systematic Risk:** Affects all companies. Cannot be diversified away. Examples: monetary policy, economic growth.

- **Unsystematic Risk:** Specific to individual companies. Can be reduced by adding more assets to portfolio.

- **Efficient Frontier:** Set of portfolios offering best return for given level of risk. Parabolic shape.

- **Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM):** Expected return = Risk-free rate + Beta × (Market return - Risk-free rate)

- **Beta:** Measures company's volatility relative to market. Beta > 1 = higher volatility; Beta < 1 = lower volatility.

- **Capital Market Line (CML):** Linear line starting at risk-free rate, intersecting efficient frontier at market portfolio.

- **Credit Risk Components:**
  - **Probability of Default (PD):** Likelihood counterparty will default
  - **Exposure at Default (EAD):** Total potential loss amount
  - **Loss Given Default (LGD):** Actual expected loss (usually less than exposure)
  - **Maturity:** Length of credit exposure (longer = higher risk)

- **Expected Loss:** Anticipated loss, represented by loan loss reserve.

- **Unexpected Loss:** Loss beyond expected, absorbed by capital (economic capital).

- **Operational Risk Classification:** Transaction processing, technology, external dependencies, human capital.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Market risk is primarily quantitative** (data, statistics). Credit and operational risk involve more qualitative judgment.

2. **Diversification reduces unsystematic risk** but cannot eliminate systematic risk.

3. **CAPM and the efficient frontier help identify optimal portfolios.** The Capital Market Line represents the most efficient portfolio.

4. **Credit risk is more complex than market risk** — involves default probability, recovery rates, legal issues, and longer time horizons.

5. **Operational risk is firm-specific and hard to model.** It requires strong qualitative analysis and governance structures.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Value at Risk (VaR) | Estimates potential loss at confidence level | Market risk measurement |
| CAPM | Expected return = Rf + β(Rm - Rf) | Calculating required return |
| Efficient Frontier | Best return for given risk level | Portfolio optimization |
| Capital Market Line (CML) | Most efficient portfolio | Portfolio selection |
| Credit Risk Components | PD, EAD, LGD, Maturity | Credit risk analysis |
| Expected/Unexpected Loss | Loan loss reserve vs. economic capital | Credit risk capital allocation |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **CAPM:** E(Ri) = Rf + βi × [E(Rm) - Rf]

- **Sharpe Ratio:** (Rp - Rf) / σp

- **Correlation Coefficient:** r = Cov(X,Y) / (σx × σy)

- **Expected Loss:** PD × EAD × LGD

---

## F. Practical Implications

- For portfolio construction, consider correlation between assets. Perfectly correlated assets provide no diversification benefit.

- Systematic risk cannot be diversified away. Investors are compensated for bearing systematic risk (CAPM).

- Credit risk requires assessing four components: PD, EAD, LGD, and maturity.

- Operational risk requires both quantitative (loss data) and qualitative (internal controls, risk assessments) analysis.

- [x] Understand portfolio construction, diversification, and the efficient frontier

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The efficient frontier is a beautiful concept. It shows the optimal trade-off between risk and return. Any portfolio below the frontier is inefficient — you can get higher return for the same risk or lower risk for the same return.

CAPM assumptions are unrealistic (no transaction costs, no taxes, everyone can borrow at risk-free rate), but the model still provides a useful framework for thinking about systematic risk.

# Module 3 — Money & Capital Markets and Regulatory

## A. Core Idea of the Module

The financial system ensures transactions are completed and channels savings into investments — crucial for economic growth. Money markets handle short-term instruments (less than one year) for liquidity management; capital markets handle long-term instruments (debt and equity) for raising funds. Regulatory structures vary by jurisdiction: the US has a decentralized system with multiple federal and state regulators; the UK has a centralized system (FCA, PRA, FPC); the EU balances national and EU-level supervision (ESRB, ESAs).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Money Markets:** Short-term instruments (< 1 year). Used by companies to manage liquidity.

- **Capital Markets:** Long-term instruments (> 1 year). Debt and equity. Non-financial companies raise funds here.

- **US Regulatory Structure:** Dual banking system (federal and state charters). Multiple federal regulators (prudential, securities). Self-regulatory organizations (FINRA). State regulators (especially for insurance).

- **UK Regulatory Structure:** Centralized. Three main bodies:
  - **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)**
  - **Prudential Regulatory Authority (PRA)**
  - **Financial Policy Committee (FPC)**

- **EU Regulatory Structure:** Balances national and EU-level supervision.
  - **European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB)**
  - **European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs)**
  - Joint committee of ESAs ensures alignment

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The financial system is heavily regulated** because it channels savings into investments, which is essential for economic growth.

2. **Money and capital markets are closely interconnected** but serve different purposes: liquidity vs. long-term funding.

3. **Regulatory structures differ by jurisdiction** but all aim to protect depositors and consumers.

4. **The US has a decentralized, layered system** (federal, state, self-regulatory). The UK has a centralized system. The EU balances both.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Money Markets | Short-term instruments (< 1 year) | Liquidity management |
| Capital Markets | Long-term instruments (debt/equity) | Raising funds |
| US Regulatory (Decentralized) | Multiple federal + state regulators | US financial institutions |
| UK Regulatory (Centralized) | FCA, PRA, FPC | UK financial institutions |
| EU Regulatory | ESRB, ESAs, national supervisors | EU financial institutions |

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Companies use money markets for short-term liquidity (commercial paper, Treasury bills) and capital markets for long-term funding (bonds, equity).

- Financial institutions must comply with multiple regulators depending on jurisdiction.

- The trend is toward more centralized regulation (post-2008), but the US remains more decentralized.

The UK moved toward centralization after the 2008 crisis (creating the FPC, PRA, FCA). The EU balances national and EU-level supervision — a reflection of its political structure.

# Module 4 — Introduction to Risk Modeling

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Scenario analysis and stress testing are tools to prepare organizations for future uncertainties, especially extreme but plausible events. Scenario analysis uses multiple risk factors for complex situations. Stress testing uses fewer risk factors, closely tied to market data, focusing on extreme but plausible market events. Both tools help firms maintain sufficient liquidity and capital to avoid failure under adverse conditions. The case study on negative interest rates and the COVID-19 pandemic shows that unconventional events require risk models to incorporate qualitative factors and Bayesian networks.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Scenario Analysis:** Uses multiple risk factors. Better for complex situations with many variables. Identifies emerging and strategic risks.

- **Stress Testing:** Uses one or a few risk factors. Closely tied to market data. Focuses on extreme but plausible market events.

- **Reverse Stress Testing:** Identifies scenarios that would cause the firm to fail.

- **Hypothetical Scenarios:** Example: trade war between major economies, volatility shocks without historical precedent.

- **Historical Scenarios:** Example: 2008 financial crisis, 1990 S&P 500 decline (28.7% in two weeks).

- **VIX:** "Fear gauge" — measures market volatility expectations.

- **Circuit Breakers:** Market mechanisms that halt trading after significant declines.

- **Bayesian Networks:** Integrate quantitative and qualitative data for improved scenario analysis and stress testing.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Scenario analysis and stress testing are complementary.** Scenario analysis is better for complex, multi-factor situations; stress testing is more focused.

2. **Risk management must prepare for extreme but plausible events.** Not just historical events, but hypothetical scenarios (trade wars, negative rates, pandemics).

3. **Negative interest rates became a formal monetary policy tool in 2014** (ECB), challenging traditional risk models and computer systems.

4. **The COVID-19 pandemic triggered all three risk types:** operational (health), market (asset corrections), credit (counterparty default).

5. **Risk models must incorporate qualitative factors** (behavioral changes, government interventions, external events) — not just quantitative data.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Scenario Analysis | Multiple risk factors | Complex, multi-variable situations |
| Stress Testing | One or few risk factors | Extreme but plausible market events |
| Reverse Stress Testing | Identify scenarios causing failure | Capital and liquidity planning |
| Bayesian Networks | Integrate quantitative + qualitative data | Improved scenario analysis |
| Historical Scenarios | Past events as reference | Benchmarking |
| Hypothetical Scenarios | Trade wars, volatility shocks | Emerging risks |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Use stress testing for portfolios of investments (market risk). Use scenario analysis for complex situations with many variables.

- Test for extreme but plausible events, not just historical events. History does not repeat perfectly.

- Include both quantitative and qualitative data. Bayesian networks can integrate both.

- Ensure the firm maintains sufficient liquidity and capital to survive adverse scenarios.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to risk management. The key insights: risk is the potential deviation from expected results — not just downside, but any deviation. The three main risk disciplines are market risk (quantitative, asset prices), credit risk (counterparty default), and operational risk (people, processes, systems, external events). Enterprise risk management (ERM) integrates these disciplines with corporate strategy. Portfolio construction uses diversification to reduce unsystematic risk, but systematic risk cannot be diversified away. The efficient frontier shows optimal risk-return trade-offs; CAPM calculates expected returns based on systematic risk (beta). Credit risk has four components: probability of default, exposure at default, loss given default, and maturity. Liquidity risk (asset and funding) is critical but hard to measure. Regulatory structures vary by jurisdiction (US decentralized, UK centralized, EU balanced). Stress testing and scenario analysis prepare firms for extreme but plausible events; they are complementary tools. The course emphasizes that risk management is not about eliminating risk but about achieving a good risk-adjusted return.

The course's strength is its breadth — covering market, credit, operational, and enterprise risk in one course. The weakness is that each topic could be a full course (and in the specialization, they are). But as an introduction to risk management, this course covers the essentials clearly.
