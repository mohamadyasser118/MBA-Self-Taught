## 🧭 Course Overview

This course provides practical guidance for improved decision-making in business situations involving critical ethical issues. It focuses on healthcare ethics but the principles apply broadly to management. The course covers four main areas: the foundation of ethics (distinguishing legal from ethical, moral from non-moral uses of "good," traditional ethical approaches), the triad of vision, mission, and strategy (how ethics enters organizational planning), conflicting interests (identifying and managing conflicts of interest), and patient safety as an ethical obligation. The core message is that ethics is not an impediment to progress but a signpost for attention — a tool for setting priorities and making better decisions.

# Module 1 — The Foundation of Ethics

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Ethics involves understanding what is good and right, and establishing rules for behavior to achieve these ideals. This module distinguishes legal from ethical questions (law tells you what you are allowed to do; ethics tells you what you should do). It introduces the specialized use of "right" and "good" in ethical theory, with "aretaic" referring to moral excellence or virtue. It covers four traditional ethical approaches (relativism, absolutism, consequentialism, virtue ethics), the evolution of healthcare ethics (Hippocratic Oath, Nuremberg Code, Belmont Report), and the four Georgetown principles (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Ethics (plural):** The set of moral principles or rules governing behavior. Refers to right and wrong behavior and the goodness or badness of human character.

- **Ethic (singular):** A specific principle or code, such as the "ethic of care" in nursing.

- **Legal vs. Ethical:** Law tells you what you are allowed to do without penalty. Ethics tells you what you should do. Something can be legal but ethically wrong.

- **Aretaic (moral) meaning:** From Greek "arete" (goodness, excellence). Refers to moral excellence or virtue. The aretaic sense of "good" means morally good.

- **Non-aretaic meaning:** Factual, technical, or aesthetic qualities. A good surgeon can be technically excellent (non-aretaic) but morally questionable (aretaic).

- **Four Traditional Ethical Approaches:**
  - **Moral Relativism:** Moral right and wrong are determined by cultural norms. No absolute moral truths.
  - **Moral Absolutism:** Universal moral principles apply to all people regardless of culture (e.g., Golden Rule, deontology).
  - **Consequentialism:** The right action is the one that produces the best overall consequences (e.g., utilitarianism).
  - **Virtue Ethics:** Focuses on character and virtues rather than rules or consequences. Becoming a virtuous person (honesty, courage) to achieve eudaimonia (flourishing).

- **Four Branches of Ethics:**
  - **Meta-ethics:** Nature and meaning of ethical terms like "right" and "good."
  - **Normative ethics:** Rules and guidelines for behavior (what people ought to do).
  - **Descriptive ethics:** How people actually behave in ethical situations.
  - **Applied ethics:** Applying ethical principles to specific professions or settings.

- **Georgetown Mantra (Four Principles of Biomedical Ethics — Beauchamp & Childress):**
  - **Beneficence:** Doing good, kindness, generosity. Both a character virtue and a professional obligation.
  - **Non-maleficence:** The moral duty to avoid causing harm intentionally. "First, do no harm."
  - **Autonomy:** The right of individuals to self-determination and independent decision-making, including the right to refuse treatment.
  - **Justice:** Distributing society's benefits and burdens based on morally justifiable principles. Includes distributive and procedural justice.

- **Paternalism:** Physician overrides patient's wishes for their perceived benefit. Historically dominant approach; now largely discredited in favor of autonomy.

- **Phases of Healthcare Ethics:**
  - Early: Hippocratic Oath, Thomas Percival's code (1803)
  - Post-WWII: Nuremberg Code, Declaration of Helsinki (consent, oversight)
  - 1979: Belmont Report (respect for persons, beneficence, justice)
  - Georgetown principles expanded to autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice

- **Supererogation:** Going above and beyond one's moral duty. Acts that are praiseworthy but not obligatory.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Legal and ethical are not the same.** Something can be legally permitted but ethically wrong. Many business failures stem from ethical lapses, not legal violations.

2. **The word "good" has both moral (aretaic) and non-moral meanings.** A good surgeon can be technically excellent but morally corrupt. Distinguishing these meanings is crucial for ethical analysis.

3. **The four Georgetown principles are widely cited but have limitations.** They are often criticized as imperialist (North American perspective), inapplicable (too abstract), inconsistent (principles conflict), and inadequate (not sufficient for complete ethical systems).

4. **Autonomy generally takes priority over beneficence and non-maleficence,** except in life-threatening emergencies or when the patient lacks competence. This is a major shift from historical paternalism.

5. **The Nuremberg Code and Belmont Report were responses to atrocities.** The Nuremberg Code (1947) established informed consent for human experimentation. The Belmont Report (1979) established respect for persons, beneficence, and justice.

## F. Practical Implications

- When facing an ethical problem, first distinguish: is it a legal question, a moral question, or both? Different tools apply.

- Avoid conflating technical excellence with moral goodness. A highly skilled employee may still act unethically.

- In healthcare, patient autonomy generally overrides physician judgment. Informed consent is required.

- When ethical principles conflict (e.g., beneficence vs. autonomy), document the deliberative process and justification.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The distinction between legal and ethical is crucial. Many people assume that if something is legal, it is ethical — but this is false. The law sets a minimum standard; ethics sets a higher standard. Corporate scandals often involve legal but unethical behavior.

The Georgetown principles are a useful starting point but not a complete ethical system. They can conflict (beneficence vs. autonomy), and they reflect a North American perspective. Applying them uncritically is dangerous.

The shift from paternalism to autonomy is one of the most important changes in healthcare ethics. Patients have the right to make decisions about their own care — even decisions that physicians disagree with. This is uncomfortable for some clinicians but ethically correct.

# Module 2 — The Triad of Vision, Mission, and Strategy

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Healthcare organizations have a moral purpose beyond financial sustainability. This module distinguishes vision (the "why" — aspirational, future-oriented), mission (the "what" — what the organization does and whom it serves), and strategy (the "how" — the plan for achieving goals). Ethics enters all three. Organizations must choose goals and objectives that serve their moral purpose and can be measured against it. The module also covers tactics (concrete actions supporting objectives) and the importance of integrating ethical analysis into corporate strategy.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Vision:** The ability to think about or plan the future with imagination and wisdom. Answers "why bother?" Aspirational. The desired future endpoint.

- **Mission:** Defines what an entity does or intends to do, including its moral purpose. Clarifies whom the entity serves. Focused on the present.

- **Strategy:** A detailed plan for achieving a goal. Answers "what plans?" Defines clear goals and objectives that serve as guideposts.

- **Goals:** Broad, long-term outcomes guided by strategy. Progress assessed over extended periods.

- **Objectives:** Specific, measurable actions that contribute to achieving the overall goal. Result from detailed planning.

- **Tactics:** Concrete, measurable actions designed to support goals and objectives. Short-term. Answer "how?" The "blocking and tackling."

- **Corporate Strategy Framework:**
  - Why? (Vision)
  - What? (Strategy)
  - How? (Tactics)

- **Moral Purpose in Healthcare:** The fundamental responsibility to promote health and treat disease. Should not be overshadowed by population health or financial goals.

- **Utilitarian Approach:** Aiming for the greatest good for the greatest number. Common in healthcare resource allocation but complex when ethical (aretaic) considerations are involved.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Healthcare organizations have a moral purpose beyond profit.** Promoting health and treating disease is inherently ethical. Organizations must choose goals that serve this purpose.

2. **Vision, mission, and strategy must be aligned.** If these are misaligned, the organization will send mixed signals and fail to achieve its moral purpose.

3. **Ethical analysis is a strategic and tactical tool.** It should be integrated into corporate strategy, not added as an afterthought. Asking "why, what, how" repeatedly uncovers ethical dimensions.

4. **Transparency is crucial when healthcare options are limited.** Patients should know how an institution's mission impacts the delivery of care.

5. **Utilitarian approaches (greatest good for greatest number) can conflict with care for individual patients.** Healthcare organizations must balance population health with obligations to individual patients, including those with rare diseases.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Vision-Mission-Strategy Triad | Why, what, how | Organizational planning |
| Goals vs. Objectives | Broad outcomes vs. specific measurable actions | Strategic planning |
| Tactics | Concrete actions supporting objectives | Execution |
| Utilitarian Analysis | Greatest good for greatest number | Resource allocation |
| Ethical Analysis as Audit | Questioning ethics of strategy | Strategic review |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Ensure your organization's vision, mission, and strategy are explicitly articulated and aligned. Misalignment causes ethical confusion.

- Do not let population health goals (utilitarian) completely override care for individual patients, including those with rare diseases.

- Use ethical analysis as a strategic tool, not just a compliance exercise. Ask "why, what, how" repeatedly.

- Be transparent with patients about how the institution's mission affects care delivery, especially when options are limited.

---

## G. Learning Outcomes

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Distinguish vision (why) from mission (what) from strategy (how)
- [x] Distinguish goals (broad outcomes) from objectives (specific actions) from tactics (concrete steps)
- [x] Explain the moral purpose of healthcare organizations
- [x] Apply utilitarian analysis to resource allocation
- [x] Identify when population health goals conflict with individual patient care

---

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The vision-mission-strategy triad is a useful framework. Vision is the "why" — it should be aspirational and inspiring. Mission is the "what" — what the organization actually does. Strategy is the "how" — the plan. Tactics are the concrete actions. Many organizations confuse these terms, leading to misalignment.

The tension between utilitarian approaches (greatest good for greatest number) and care for individual patients is real. Healthcare organizations cannot ignore population health, but they also cannot abandon patients with rare or expensive-to-treat conditions. This is a genuine ethical dilemma, not a calculation problem.

# Module 3 — Conflicting Interests

## A. Core Idea of the Module

A conflict of interest arises when professional judgment or actions related to a primary role are influenced by a secondary interest. Conflicts of interest are situations (sets of circumstances) that may risk influencing judgment — not accusations of wrongdoing. This module covers four types of conflicts (financial, non-financial, roles/commitments, predetermination), the distinction between legal and ethical conflicts, the importance of disclosure and transparency, and how to manage conflicts through clear policies.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Conflict of Interest (COI):** A set of circumstances where professional judgment or actions related to a primary role may be influenced by a secondary interest. Not an accusation of wrongdoing.

- **Primary Interest:** The core obligation — patient welfare, integrity of clinical and scientific judgment.

- **Secondary Interest:** Financial gain, professional advancement, recognition, favors to friends or colleagues. Legitimate but must remain subordinate to primary interests.

- **Four Types of Conflicts of Interest:**
  - Financial conflicts
  - Non-financial conflicts (personal relationships, intellectual commitments)
  - Conflicts of roles or commitments
  - Predetermination (decisions made in advance rather than through open competition)

- **Undue Influence:** When secondary interests improperly sway decision-making, compromising voluntary consent or professional judgment.

- **Sunshine Laws:** Laws requiring public disclosure of business relationships and institutional events.

- **Disclosure vs. Resolution:** Disclosure helps weigh potential conflicts but does not automatically resolve them. Some conflicts require forfeiting the secondary interest.

- **Conflicts Among Primary Interests:** Not all conflicts are between primary and secondary interests. Some involve competing primary obligations (e.g., patient confidentiality vs. public safety).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **A conflict of interest is a situation, not an accusation.** The existence of a conflict does not imply improper motivation. COI policies aim to minimize conditions that could lead to suspicion of undue influence.

2. **Financial conflicts are the main focus of COI policies** because they are more objective, quantifiable, and easier to regulate than non-financial conflicts.

3. **Disclosure is necessary but not sufficient.** Transparency helps identify potential conflicts, but some conflicts are so serious that disclosure alone is inadequate — the secondary interest must be forfeited.

4. **Conflicts of obligation (primary vs. primary) are different from conflicts of interest.** When two legitimate primary interests conflict (e.g., patient confidentiality vs. public safety), this is an ethical dilemma, not a COI.

5. **Clear, transparent policies are essential.** COI policies should be established in advance, clearly articulated, and consistently applied.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Four Types of COI | Financial, non-financial, roles, predetermination | Identifying conflicts |
| Primary vs. Secondary Interest | Patient welfare vs. financial gain | Analyzing COI |
| Undue Influence | Improper swaying of judgment | Assessing COI severity |
| Disclosure | Transparency about relationships | First step in COI management |
| Sunshine Laws | Public disclosure requirements | Regulatory compliance |
| COI Disclosure Forms | Written declarations | Institutional policies |

---

## F. Practical Implications

- If you have a conflict of interest, disclose it immediately. Non-disclosure creates suspicion even if no actual bias occurred.

- Understand your organization's COI policy. Compliance is not optional.

- Financial relationships with industry (pharmaceutical, device) are the most regulated. Assume they will be scrutinized.

- Some conflicts cannot be resolved by disclosure — you may need to forfeit the secondary interest (e.g., sell stock, step away from decision-making).

- Do not confuse conflicts of interest (primary vs. secondary) with conflicts of obligation (primary vs. primary). The latter requires different analysis.

---

- [x] Define conflict of interest as a situation, not an accusation
- [x] Distinguish primary from secondary interests
- [x] Identify four types of COI (financial, non-financial, roles, predetermination)
- [x] Explain why financial conflicts are the main focus of COI policies
- [x] Distinguish disclosure (necessary) from resolution (sometimes requires forfeiture)

---

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The definition of COI as a "situation" rather than an "accusation" is important. People resist disclosing conflicts because they fear being accused of wrongdoing. But a conflict of interest is a set of circumstances — not a judgment. Policies should focus on managing circumstances, not blaming individuals.

The birthday cake example in the lecture is memorable: to fairly divide cake, one person cuts and the other chooses. This creates a natural check on self-interest. Good COI policies create similar structural checks.

Disclosure alone is not enough. Some conflicts are so severe that the person should be removed from the decision-making process entirely. Knowing when disclosure suffices and when forfeiture is required is a key managerial skill.

# Module 4 — Ethics and Patient Safety

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Patient safety is an ethical obligation, not just a technical or operational concern. This module covers the types of errors in healthcare (omission vs. commission), the ethics of using the word "error" (blame vs. system improvement), the delivery of safe patient care (briefing, oversight, debriefing framework), the distinction between preventable and unavoidable harm, whistleblowing, and the costs of patient safety. The core message is that patient safety requires a culture of safety, not a culture of blame.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Patient Safety:** A complex public health issue relevant to all healthcare institutions. Includes diagnostic errors, quality of life, and dignity — not just mortality.

- **Errors of Omission:** Necessary actions not taken (e.g., not securing a patient properly).

- **Errors of Commission:** Incorrect actions taken (e.g., administering medication to which a patient is allergic).

- **Iatrogenic Harm:** Harm caused by medical care, not the underlying illness.

- **Adverse Events:** Unintended injuries related to medical care. May be avoidable or unavoidable.

- **Near Misses:** Events that could have caused harm but did not. Resemble adverse events except in outcome.

- **Preventable Harm:** Harm that could have been avoided through appropriate care or interventions.

- **Never Events:** Adverse events that should never occur (e.g., surgery on wrong patient). The Joint Commission provides standards.

- **To Err Is Human (1999 IOM report):** Found that approximately 98,000 patients die annually from preventable medical errors in US hospitals. Foundational to modern patient safety movement.

- **Before-During-After Framework (from military operations):**
  - Briefing before (detailed plans)
  - Constant communication and oversight during
  - Debriefing after (identify improvements)

- **Whistleblowing:** Exposing confidential or restricted information about unethical or wrongful activities. Requires protection mechanisms (anonymous reporting, ombudsmen).

- **Culture of Safety vs. Culture of Blame:** Current view reluctant to affix blame to individuals except in special circumstances. Focus on system failures and fail-safe measures.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Patient safety is an absolute ethical obligation,** but reasonable arguments can be made that excessive costs to prevent extremely remote risks are unwarranted. "Preventable risk" is a moving target.

2. **The term "error" should be used with circumspection.** Using it carelessly can damage professional confidence and morale, leading to career departure. The focus should be on understanding causes, not assigning blame.

3. **Most errors result from multiple converging factors.** The purpose of error investigation is not to affix blame but to suggest ways to reduce the likelihood of harm even should an error occur.

4. **Patient safety has both aretaic (moral) and non-aretaic (technical) elements.** Healthcare professionals care deeply about patient safety (aretaic), but implementing technical changes may be easier.

5. **Whistleblowing requires protection mechanisms.** Despite legal protections, whistleblowers often face retaliation. Organizations should provide anonymous reporting channels and ombudsmen.

6. **The cost of patient safety is ethically challenging.** The Ford Pinto case (cost-benefit analysis of safety) is widely condemned. Public opinion opposes purely cost-based decisions in patient safety.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Errors of Omission vs. Commission | Failure to act vs. incorrect action | Classifying adverse events |
| Before-During-After Framework | Briefing, communication, debriefing | Surgical teams, high-risk procedures |
| Culture of Safety | Blame-free, system-focused error analysis | Patient safety programs |
| Whistleblowing Protection | Anonymous reporting, ombudsmen | Addressing observed wrongdoing |
| Preventable vs. Unavoidable Harm | Avoidable vs. not avoidable with current standards | Root cause analysis |
| Never Events | Adverse events that should never occur | Quality measurement |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Create a culture of safety, not blame. When errors occur, focus on system failures, not individual fault.

- Separate error investigation from discipline. The purpose of investigation is learning, not punishment.

- Use the before-during-after framework (briefing, communication, debriefing) for high-risk procedures.

- Provide anonymous reporting channels for whistleblowers. Protect them from retaliation.

- Document adverse events thoroughly. Admission of error is different from assuming total responsibility.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The 1999 IOM report "To Err Is Human" was a watershed moment. 98,000 preventable deaths annually — equivalent to a jumbo jet crashing every day. The healthcare industry had been ignoring patient safety as a systemic issue. The report shifted the focus from individual blame to system design.

The before-during-after framework (briefing, communication, debriefing) is borrowed from military aviation. Aviation has much lower error rates than medicine because they systematized safety. Medicine is still catching up.

The tension between patient safety and financial costs is real. The Ford Pinto case (where Ford calculated it was cheaper to pay lawsuits than fix a known defect) is the classic cautionary tale. Public opinion rejects purely cost-based safety decisions. Healthcare organizations should be very careful about making safety trade-offs explicit.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course provides a practical framework for ethical decision-making in healthcare and management. The key insights: legal and ethical are not the same — ethics tells you what you should do, not just what you are allowed to do. The word "good" has both moral (aretaic) and technical (non-aretaic) meanings; distinguishing them is crucial. The four Georgetown principles (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) are a useful starting point but have limitations. Vision (why), mission (what), and strategy (how) must be aligned and infused with ethical analysis. Conflicts of interest are situations, not accusations — disclosure is necessary but not sufficient. Patient safety is an ethical obligation requiring a culture of safety, not blame.

The course's strength is its practical, case-based approach (Ford Pinto, Johnson & Johnson Tylenol, handwashing history). The weakness is that it focuses on healthcare; managers in other industries must translate the principles. But the core ethical frameworks apply broadly.
