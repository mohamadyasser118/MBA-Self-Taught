# Module 1 — Introduction to Consumer Behavior

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Consumer behavior involves the psychological and behavioral processes consumers go through when searching, selecting, purchasing, using, and evaluating products or services. Understanding consumer behavior is essential for identifying market opportunities and setting effective marketing strategies. This module covers internal factors (personality, lifestyle, age, gender, socioeconomic status) and external factors (reference groups, family, culture, economic environment) that influence consumer behavior. It also covers psychological theories (Freud's laddering, Maslow's hierarchy of needs, Herzberg's satisfiers/dissatisfiers) and prospect theory (reference pricing, framing, loss aversion, mental accounting).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Consumer Behavior:** The psychological and behavioral processes consumers go through when searching, selecting, purchasing, using, and evaluating products or services.

- **Internal Factors:** Personality traits, lifestyle, age, gender, socioeconomic status.

- **External Factors:** Reference groups (friends, experts), family influence, cultural differences, economic environment.

- **Marketing Stimuli:** Products, prices, availability channels (online/offline), company communications.

- **Consumer Decision Process Stages:** Problem recognition, information search, evaluation of alternatives, purchase, post-purchase behavior.

- **Psychological Theories in Consumer Behavior:**
  - **Freud's Laddering Technique:** Explains decision stages leading to purchase.
  - **Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs:** Physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualization.
  - **Herzberg's Theory:** Distinguishes satisfiers (motivators) from dissatisfiers (hygiene factors).

- **Three Approaches to Consumer Thought Process:**
  - **Rational Approach (Standard Economic Theory):** Consumers maximize utility within budget constraints.
  - **Motivational Approach:** Consumers driven by unconscious and emotional motivations (emotional appeal, sex appeal, fear appeal).
  - **Bounded Rationality (Behavioral Economics):** Consumers are neither fully rational nor completely irrational.

- **Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky):**
  - **Reference Pricing:** Consumers use baseline reference prices (past prices, prices paid by others).
  - **Framing:** Consumers weigh losses more heavily than gains (loss aversion).
  - **Value Function:** Defined relative to reference point; concave for gains, convex for losses.
  - **Risk Function:** Risk-averse in gain domain; risk-seeking in loss domain.
  - **Diminishing Sensitivity:** Reduced sensitivity to price changes as base price increases.
  - **Mental Accounting:** Consumers mentally code money as gains or losses, influencing payment methods and satisfaction.
  - **Disposition Effect:** Consumers hold on to losses longer and sell gains faster (status quo bias).

- **Three Types of Buying Decision Behavior:**
  - **Routine Response Behavior:** Low-cost, frequent purchases (milk). Minimal time, low involvement.
  - **Limited Problem Solving:** Moderate research and involvement (clothing).
  - **Extensive Problem Solving:** Expensive, infrequent purchases (computer). Significant research, high involvement.

- **Psychological Components of Consumer Behavior:**
  - **Cognitive (Mental Processes):** Gathering information about product features, prices, options.
  - **Affective (Emotional):** Developing preferences by liking certain features and prices.
  - **Behavioral:** Actual purchase, usage, satisfaction, repeat buying, brand loyalty.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Consumers are not fully rational.** Prospect theory shows they use reference pricing, frame gains and losses differently, and exhibit loss aversion (losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good).

2. **Different products require different marketing approaches.** Routine purchases (milk) need brand reinforcement; extensive problem-solving purchases (computers) need detailed information and comparison tools.

3. **Maslow's hierarchy explains why different consumers buy different products.** Someone seeking safety buys differently than someone seeking self-actualization.

4. **Herzberg's satisfiers/dissatisfiers map to marketing.** Dissatisfiers (poor quality, bad service) cause complaints; satisfiers (features, style) drive purchase.

5. **The value function is concave for gains and convex for losses.** A $1 gain feels smaller than a $1 loss. This explains why consumers prefer price decreases framed as gains and resist price increases framed as losses.

---

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|

| Herzberg's Two-Factor | Satisfiers (motivators) vs. Dissatisfiers (hygiene) | Product design, service quality |
| Prospect Theory | Reference pricing, framing, loss aversion, mental accounting | Pricing strategy, promotions |
| Value Function | Concave for gains, convex for losses, reference-dependent | Price framing |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

- **Value Function (conceptual):** V(x) is concave for gains (x > 0), convex for losses (x < 0), and steeper for losses.

---

## F. Practical Implications

- For routine purchases (milk, bread), focus on brand reinforcement and availability. Consumers do not research extensively.

- For extensive problem-solving purchases (computers, cars), provide detailed specifications, comparison tools, and expert reviews.

- Use reference pricing to anchor consumer expectations (e.g., "was $100, now $75").

- Frame price decreases as gains; avoid framing price increases as losses.

- Understand that consumers are loss-averse. A price increase will hurt more than an equivalent price decrease will please.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

Prospect theory is one of the most important insights in behavioral economics. Loss aversion explains why "free trial" offers work (switching feels like a loss) and why price increases are so painful. Marketers who understand loss aversion frame their offers accordingly.

The continuum of buying decision behavior (routine → limited → extensive) is a useful segmentation tool. The same consumer uses routine behavior for milk and extensive behavior for a computer. Marketing strategy must match the behavior type.

# Module 2 — Consumer Decision-Making Process

## A. Core Idea of the Module

The consumer decision-making process has five stages: need recognition, information search, evaluation of alternatives, purchase, and post-purchase behavior. These stages differ in importance depending on the product category. Consumers face constraints including information overload, limited attention, and personal relevance. Perceptions and attitudes also influence decisions. The module covers decision rules (non-compensatory vs. compensatory), the elaboration likelihood model (central vs. peripheral routes), and the foot-in-the-door technique for attitude change.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Five Stages of Consumer Decision-Making:**
  1. **Need Recognition:** Gap between ideal and actual state (problem or opportunity).
  2. **Information Search:** Gathering data from friends, family, media, internet, memory.
  3. **Evaluation of Alternatives:** Using decision rules to choose among options.
  4. **Purchase:** Actual buying decision (influenced by habits, preferences).
  5. **Post-Purchase Behavior:** Satisfaction = experience - expectations. Experience > expectations = satisfied/delighted.

- **Information Search Sources:** Friends, family, print/electronic media, internet, memory. Marketers target early adopters and opinion leaders.

- **Decision Rules (Evaluation of Alternatives):**
  - **Non-Compensatory:** Eliminate options not meeting criteria.
    - Lexicographic: Choose highest on most important attribute.
    - Elimination-by-aspects: Eliminate options below threshold on any attribute.
    - Conjunctive: Meet minimum cutoffs on all attributes.
  - **Compensatory:** Allow trade-offs among attributes.
    - Simple additive: Sum scores.
    - Weighted additive: Weighted sum scores.

- **Constraints on Consumer Decision-Making:**
  - **Information Overload:** Too many options cause confusion.
  - **Attention:** Marketers capture attention through unexpected or unusual advertising.
  - **Personal Relevance:** Consumers more influenced by messages relating to personal experience.

- **Perceptions and Attitudes:**
  - **Perception:** Brand names and packaging shift preferences.
  - **Attitudes:** Lasting evaluations linked to status quo bias. Marketers maintain attitudes through foot-in-the-door technique and dissonance reduction.

- **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM):** High-involvement products → central route (detailed arguments about features). Low-involvement products → peripheral route (cues like attractiveness, celebrity).

- **Foot-in-the-Door Technique:** Start with small request, then larger request. Builds consistency.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The importance of each decision stage varies by product.** Groceries (routine) skip evaluation; electronics (extensive) require extensive evaluation; clothing (limited) moderate evaluation.

2. **Consumers use different decision rules for different products.** Non-compensatory rules (elimination) for complex choices; compensatory rules (trade-offs) when attributes are comparable.

3. **Satisfaction is a comparison of experience to expectations.** Marketers should not overpromise. Underpromise and overdeliver.

4. **Information overload is real.** Too many options cause choice paralysis. Marketers should simplify.

5. **Attitudes are sticky.** Status quo bias means consumers prefer familiar brands. Changing attitudes requires dissonance reduction or foot-in-the-door.

6. **The elaboration likelihood model guides message strategy.** Central route for high-involvement products (detailed arguments); peripheral route for low-involvement products (attractive cues).

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Five Stages of Decision-Making | Need recognition → information search → evaluation → purchase → post-purchase | Any consumer product |
| Non-Compensatory Rules | Lexicographic, elimination-by-aspects, conjunctive | Complex, high-stakes choices |
| Compensatory Rules | Simple additive, weighted additive | Comparable attributes |
| Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) | Central route (high involvement) vs. peripheral route (low involvement) | Advertising strategy |
| Foot-in-the-Door | Small request → larger request | Attitude change |
| Status Quo Bias | Preference for familiar brands | Loyalty programs |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Satisfaction:** Satisfaction = Experience - Expectations

---

## F. Practical Implications

- For extensive problem-solving products, provide detailed information and comparison tools. Consumers are in central route processing.

- For routine products, focus on brand reinforcement and availability. Consumers are in peripheral route processing.

- Do not overpromise. Underpromise and overdeliver to exceed expectations.

- Simplify choices. Too many options cause decision paralysis.

- Use foot-in-the-door for attitude change. Start with a small request (free sample), then larger request (purchase).

---

## G. Learning Outcomes

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Describe the five stages of consumer decision-making
- [x] Distinguish non-compensatory from compensatory decision rules
- [x] Apply the elaboration likelihood model to advertising strategy
- [x] Identify constraints on consumer decision-making (information overload, attention, personal relevance)
- [x] Explain the foot-in-the-door technique
- [x] Calculate satisfaction as experience minus expectations

---

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The elaboration likelihood model is a practical framework. For laptops (high involvement), consumers want detailed specifications. For soda (low involvement), they respond to attractive packaging and celebrity endorsements. Matching message to involvement level is essential.

The satisfaction formula (experience - expectations) explains why overpromising is dangerous. Exceed expectations by delivering more than promised, not by promising more than you can deliver.

# Module 3 — Marketing Research & Experimental Design

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Marketing research supports marketing decision-making by providing relevant information. This module covers the three types of marketing research (exploratory, descriptive, causal), the steps to conducting research (problem formulation, research design, data collection, sampling, analysis, reporting), experimental design fundamentals (validity, reliability, types of designs), and the difference between primary and secondary data.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Marketing Research:** Addresses marketing problems and opportunities through data collection, analysis, information specification, and interpretation.

- **Three Types of Marketing Research:**
  - **Exploratory Research:** Discovers new ideas, generates hypotheses. Methods: literature research, experience surveys, observation, focus groups, in-depth interviews.
  - **Descriptive Research:** Measures frequencies and relationships. Methods: surveys, longitudinal/cross-sectional studies, case studies, correlational research.
  - **Causal Research:** Establishes cause-and-effect through controlled experiments.

- **Steps to Conducting Marketing Research:**
  1. Formulate the problem (manager's decision problem → research problem)
  2. Determine research design (exploratory, descriptive, or causal)
  3. Design data collection method (observational vs. survey, open-ended vs. closed-ended)
  4. Design sampling (representative sample, reliability, validity)
  5. Analyze data (basic: descriptive/correlation; advanced: statistical/econometric models)
  6. Report results (findings, conclusions, recommendations)

- **Components of Marketing Research Environment:** Economic environment, internal corporate structure, competition, pricing, product, distribution, promotion, buyer behavior.

- **Marketing Research Organization:** Centralized (economies of scale, better control) vs. decentralized (focus on specific market divisions).

- **Types of Information Delivery:**
  - Projects: specific, timely questions
  - Marketing Information Systems (MIS): continuous monitoring of predictable issues
  - Decision Support Systems (DSS): broader, complex, ongoing challenges

- **Marketing Dashboards:** Coherent set of metrics (sales, customer satisfaction, market share, ROI). Benefits: efficiency, clearer actions. Downsides: difficulty measuring long-term impact, may promote inefficient strategic behavior.

- **Experimental Design Fundamentals:**
  - **Dependent Variable:** Outcome measured (effect)
  - **Independent Variable (Treatment):** Variable manipulated by researcher
  - **Control Variable:** Held constant to avoid confounding
  - **Extraneous Variable:** Uncontrolled, may bias results
  - **Within-Subject Variation:** Same group measured before and after treatment
  - **Between-Subject Variation:** Different groups compared after treatment

- **Validity:**
  - **Internal Validity:** Accuracy of causal relationships in the study
  - **External Validity:** Generalizability to target population
  - **Construct Validity:** Variables properly identified

- **Reliability:** Consistency of results if experiment repeated.

- **Three Types of Experimental Designs:**
  - **Pre-Experimental Design:** No control over subjects, exposure, or measurement. No randomization.
  - **True Experimental Design:** Full control over exposure and measurement. Random assignment possible.
  - **Quasi-Experimental Design:** No control over subjects or exposure. Control over measurement.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Exploratory research asks "what"; descriptive research asks "how many"; causal research asks "why."** Different questions require different methods.

2. **True experiments are the gold standard for causality** but are often impractical or unethical. Quasi-experiments are common in marketing.

3. **Internal validity (accuracy of causal claims) and external validity (generalizability) often trade off.** Highly controlled lab experiments have high internal validity but low external validity.

4. **Marketing dashboards are useful but have downsides.** They may incentivize short-term behavior and miss long-term brand effects.

5. **The depth of consumer research depends on the product category.** For milk (routine), consumers need minimal research. For computers (extensive), consumers need detailed causal research.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Exploratory Research | Focus groups, interviews, observation | Generating hypotheses |
| Descriptive Research | Surveys, case studies, correlation | Measuring frequencies and relationships |
| Causal Research | Controlled experiments | Establishing cause-and-effect |
| Pre-Experimental Design | No control over subjects or measurement | Early-stage, low-budget research |
| True Experimental Design | Random assignment, full control | High-validity causal inference |
| Quasi-Experimental Design | Control over measurement only | When randomization impossible |
| Internal Validity | Accuracy of causal claims | Evaluating research quality |
| External Validity | Generalizability | Applying findings to real world |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Match research design to the question. Exploratory for "what"; descriptive for "how many"; causal for "why."

- If you need to establish causality (e.g., does this ad increase sales?), use a true experiment with random assignment.

- Marketing dashboards are useful for tracking, but supplement with long-term brand health metrics.

- For consumer goods, understand how much research consumers actually do. For routine products, focus on availability and brand reinforcement.

---

## G. Learning Outcomes

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Distinguish exploratory, descriptive, and causal research
- [x] List the six steps to conducting marketing research
- [x] Distinguish pre-experimental, true experimental, and quasi-experimental designs
- [x] Distinguish internal from external validity
- [x] Distinguish dependent, independent, control, and extraneous variables
- [x] Distinguish within-subject from between-subject variation

---

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The trade-off between internal and external validity is important. Lab experiments have high internal validity (you know the cause) but low external validity (will it work in the real world?). Field experiments (e.g., A/B testing) balance both.

Many marketing decisions are made without causal evidence. Correlational data (descriptive research) is easier and cheaper, but it does not prove causation. Managers should be skeptical of causal claims based on correlational data.

# Module 4 — Data Collection and Analysis

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Data collection and analysis are the operational core of market research. This module covers primary data (collected for current study) vs. secondary data (collected for other purposes), scales of measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio), questionnaire design, attitude measurement scales (Likert, rank-ordered, constant sum, paired comparison, graphical), sampling (target population, sampling frame), and hypothesis testing (null hypothesis, p-value, Type I and Type II errors).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Primary Data:** Collected specifically for the current study. Types: demographics/socio-economics, psychographics/lifestyle, personal values, awareness/knowledge, intentions, attitudes/motivations, actual behavior.

- **Secondary Data:** Collected for a different study but used to support current research.

- **Scales of Measurement (increasing information content):**
  - **Nominal:** Categories with no order (e.g., brand preference).
  - **Ordinal:** Categories with order, no meaningful distance (e.g., preference levels).
  - **Interval:** Ordered categories with meaningful distance, no true zero (e.g., 7-point scale).
  - **Ratio:** Interval data with true zero (e.g., sales, revenue).

- **Questionnaire Design Essentials:**
  - Specify information needed based on research objectives.
  - Decide question type (open-ended vs. closed).
  - Keep questions concise and precise.
  - Use simple, clear wording; avoid ambiguous, leading, or double-barreled questions.
  - Sequence: easy, interesting first; funnel approach (broad to specific); difficult/sensitive near end.
  - Pretest on small sample.

- **Attitude Measurement Scales:**
  - **Single-Item:** Itemized category, rank-ordered, constant sum, paired comparison, graphical.
  - **Multi-Item:** Likert scale (most popular). Combines multiple items, sometimes weighted.

- **Likert Scale:** Respondents indicate agreement with statements (e.g., strongly agree to strongly disagree).

- **Constant Sum Scale:** Respondents allocate points summing to fixed total across items.

- **Paired Comparison Scale:** Compare two brands at a time. Suitable for limited number of brands.

- **Sampling:**
  - **Target Population:** Population relevant to research question.
  - **Sampling Frame:** Subset of target population from which sample is drawn.
  - **Representative Sample:** Sample characteristics match target population.

- **Data Analysis Fundamentals:**
  - **Categorical Data:** Nominal, ordinal.
  - **Metric (Interval) Data:** Discrete (countable), continuous (measurable).

- **Hypothesis Testing:**
  - **Null Hypothesis (H₀):** No effect or no difference.
  - **Significance Level (α):** Commonly 0.05.
  - **p-value:** Probability of observing sample effect assuming null hypothesis is true.
  - **p < 0.05:** Reject null hypothesis (statistically significant).
  - **Type I Error (False Positive):** Reject null hypothesis when it is true (e.g., selecting ineffective ad).
  - **Type II Error (False Negative):** Accept null hypothesis when it is false (e.g., not selecting effective ad).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Different scales provide different information.** Ratio data (sales, revenue) is most informative. Nominal data (brand preference) is least informative.

2. **Question wording shapes answers.** Ambiguous, leading, or double-barreled questions produce biased data.

3. **Pretesting is essential.** Even well-designed questionnaires have problems. Pretest on a small sample before full deployment.

4. **Likert scales are the most popular multi-item scale** because they capture intensity of agreement.

5. **Statistical significance (p < 0.05) does not mean practical significance.** A statistically significant effect may be too small to matter.

6. **Type I and Type II errors trade off.** Reducing Type I error (lower α) increases Type II error. Choose based on consequences.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Nominal Scale | Categories, no order | Brand preference, gender |
| Ordinal Scale | Order, no meaningful distance | Preference rankings |
| Interval Scale | Meaningful distance, no true zero | 7-point satisfaction scales |
| Ratio Scale | Meaningful distance, true zero | Sales, revenue, units sold |
| Likert Scale | Multi-item agreement scale | Attitudes, opinions |
| Constant Sum Scale | Allocate fixed points | Importance weights |
| Paired Comparison | Compare two brands | Small number of brands |
| Hypothesis Testing | Null vs. alternative, p-value | Statistical inference |
| Type I Error | False positive (α) | When false positive costly |
| Type II Error | False negative (β) | When false negative costly |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **p-value interpretation:** p < α → reject null hypothesis

- **Type I Error probability:** α (commonly 0.05)

- **Type II Error probability:** β

- **Power:** 1 - β (probability of detecting true effect)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Use ratio data (sales, revenue) whenever possible. It contains the most information.

- For questionnaire design: keep it short, use simple language, pretest.

- Likert scales are versatile and widely understood. Use 5- or 7-point scales.

- Statistical significance (p < 0.05) is the minimum standard. Look for effect size and practical significance.

- Understand the trade-off between Type I and Type II errors. In marketing, false positives (Type I) waste money on ineffective campaigns; false negatives (Type II) miss opportunities.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The distinction between nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio data is foundational. Many statistical techniques require interval or ratio data. Using nominal data with parametric tests is invalid.

Likert scales are everywhere because they work. But they are ordinal, not interval. Strictly speaking, means of Likert scales are not mathematically justified, but in practice they are used.

Hypothesis testing is often misused. p < 0.05 is not a magic number. Effect size matters. Practical significance matters. Statistical significance alone is insufficient.

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to market research and consumer behavior. The course's strength is its practical, applied approach. The weakness is that it does not teach statistical analysis in depth. But as an introduction to market research and consumer behavior, this course covers the essential concepts clearly.
