## 🧭 Course Overview

This course builds a practical framework for one of management's most persistent challenges: getting people to do what the organization needs, while respecting who they are and what they want. It moves across four connected topics — motivation and reward systems, job and work system design, decision-making under pressure, and organizational architecture — using real cases (Costco vs. Walmart, Toyota vs. GM, Nordstrom, Charles Schwab) and research throughout. The two professors bring different lenses: Cappelli focuses on HR economics and evidence-based management; Useem brings organizational behavior and leadership in crises. Together they show that managing people is not a soft add-on to business strategy — it is business strategy.

# Module 1 — Motivation and Reward

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Managing people starts with a problem: employees are free people with legal rights, social needs, and their own goals — but the organization needs specific behaviors and results from them. The module opens this tension by showing how human capital is different from every other thing a company uses. Unlike machines or money, people cannot be owned, cannot be directed without their agreement, and respond to management through psychology and social context as much as through money.

The module then builds two different ways of thinking about motivation. The economic model (agency theory) treats employees as self-interested and responsive to incentives. It explains pay-for-performance, efficiency wages, and tournament pay. The psychological model adds what economics misses: the power of expectations, social norms, random rewards, and the times when financial incentives actually make motivation worse.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Human Capital:** The productive power inside employees — their skills, knowledge, experience, judgment, and relationships. Unlike machines, it cannot be owned by the company. It walks out the door every night.

- **Agency Model (Principal-Agent Theory):** A way of thinking about the relationship between an employer (principal) and employee (agent). The agent has private information and possibly different goals. The principal must design incentives to align behavior without watching everything they do.

- **Efficiency Wages:** Paying more than the market rate to attract better workers, reduce turnover, and make getting fired costly — which motivates better performance through fear of losing a good job.

- **Tournament Model:** A pay system where employees compete for a limited number of top positions (promotions, bonuses), with big pay gaps between levels designed to motivate effort throughout the company.

- **Rewarding A While Hoping for B:** The classic incentive design failure — the company measures and rewards one behavior but actually wants a different one, so employees do exactly what gets rewarded, not what the company really needs.

- **Expectancy Theory:** Employees are only motivated when they believe three things: (1) effort leads to performance, (2) performance leads to reward, and (3) the reward is something they want. If any link breaks, motivation breaks.

- **Path-Goal Theory:** Motivation needs not just clear goals but also a clear path. Employees who lack the skills, knowledge, or tools to reach a goal will not be motivated by the goal alone.

- **Behavior Modification:** Using rewards (including random rewards) to shape behavior over time. Random rewards are often more powerful than predictable ones because they are harder to get used to and stop responding to.

- **Managing Performance:** The ongoing cycle of setting expectations, watching performance, giving feedback, and adjusting rewards — made harder by biases like overconfidence (thinking you are better than you are) and the fundamental attribution error (blaming the person instead of the situation).

- **Attraction-Selection-Attrition (ASA) Framework:** Organizations naturally attract, hire, and keep people who fit their culture. Over time this creates sameness, so building diversity requires active, intentional effort.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Incentives are needed but not enough — and can backfire badly.** The most important claim in this module is that financial incentives are not a cure-all. The Australian prisoner example and the Mir space station example both show "rewarding A while hoping for B": when you reward captains for each prisoner loaded on the ship (A) instead of each prisoner arriving alive (B), you get exactly what you reward, not what you want. Good incentive design starts with the real desired behavior, not the easy-to-measure substitute.

2. **Agency theory predicts behavior well but misses half the story.** The economic model is right that employees respond to incentives and that watching them closely is expensive. But it treats people as purely selfish robots, ignoring the proven power of doing work for its own sake, social norms, and identity. A manager who only uses economic tools will solve some problems while creating new ones.

3. **Efficiency wages work — but only when losing the job hurts.** The logic of efficiency wages depends on employees having something to lose. If the pay is much better than what they could get elsewhere, getting fired is costly, so they work harder to keep the job. This is why Costco's high pay leads to low turnover and high performance — employees are not loyal for emotional reasons, but because the alternative (another retail job with lower pay) is genuinely worse.

4. **Tournaments motivate individual performance but hurt teamwork.** Big pay gaps between levels create strong motivation to beat coworkers — which works well for sales or other individual contributor roles. But in team-based work, tournaments create bad incentives: hiding information, hurting coworkers, and optimizing personal numbers at the expense of the team's results.

5. **Bias in performance reviews is built-in, not just bad management.** Overconfidence (employees always rate themselves above average), the fundamental attribution error (bosses blame the person, not the situation), and the recency effect (recent events dominate annual reviews) are predictable, well-documented patterns. Any performance management system that ignores these biases will produce unfair and demotivating reviews.

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Principal-Agent Framework | Looks at incentive design when the agent has private information and the principal cannot watch everything | When designing pay, monitoring systems, or figuring out why desired behaviors are not happening |
| Expectancy Theory (VIE) | Motivation = Valence × Instrumentality × Expectancy — all three links must work | When figuring out why an incentive is not changing behavior |
| Tournament Model | Big pay gaps between levels to encourage competition | When designing pay for roles where individual performance is measurable and teamwork risk is low |
| Behavior Modification | Using rewards (especially random ones) to shape behavior over time | When trying to create or stop a specific behavior pattern |
| ASA Framework | Organizations become more similar over time through attraction, selection, and attrition; diversity needs active work | When designing hiring processes or figuring out why a company lacks diversity |

## F. Practical Implications

- Before designing any incentive system, write down clearly: "What behavior do I actually want?" Then check if your proposed incentive rewards that behavior directly or just something related to it. If it is related but not the same, think about how people might game the system to get the reward without doing what you really need.

- When someone on your team seems unmotivated, figure out which link in the expectancy chain is broken: do they think effort will lead to performance (a skill or resource gap)? Do they think performance will lead to reward (a trust gap)? Do they even want the reward (a preference gap)? Different problems need different fixes.

- Use tournament pay carefully — it works for sales and other individual roles but damages teamwork, information sharing, and collaboration. Know which setting you are in before using it.

- Separate performance from potential when doing reviews. The fundamental attribution error makes managers mix them up, leading to bad promotion decisions and feedback that demotivates good people who are just in a tough spot right now.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Australian prisoner example is one of the best illustrations of incentive misalignment I have seen. It works because the result is so clear and shocking: a large, documented change in death rates just from changing how captains were paid. Most incentive problems are slower and harder to see — salespeople padding their numbers, engineers padding time estimates, managers sandbagging forecasts — but the mechanism is the same. Behavior follows the incentive, not the good intention.

One small critique: the module suggests that expectancy theory gives you a complete way to diagnose motivation problems. But expectancy theory is still an economic model at heart — it treats motivation as a rational calculation of expected outcomes. It does not capture times when people are motivated by identity, meaning, or the work itself. The HR course (Preparing to Manage Human Resources) covers this more deeply. These two courses work well together.

# Module 2 — Tasks, Jobs, and Systems of Work

## A. Core Idea of the Module

How work is organized — broken into pieces, grouped into jobs, assigned to people, and coordinated across teams — is itself a design choice with huge effects on performance, motivation, and competitive advantage. This module traces job design from Frederick Taylor's scientific management (maximum specialization, minimum worker choice) through the pushback from the human relations movement (boredom, quality failures, resistance) to today's high-performance work systems (broad roles, team-based work, real involvement in improvement). The Toyota/NUMMI case is the centerpiece: the same workers, same factory, same union — completely different results under a different management system.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Task:** A single, specific activity done as part of a job — the smallest piece of work design. Tasks are grouped into jobs based on how they fit together.

- **Job:** A set of related tasks assigned to one person or role, with defined duties, needed skills, and reporting lines.

- **Organizational Chart:** A picture of jobs, who reports to whom, and the hierarchy — it shows who gets to decide what.

- **Decision Rights:** The authority to make specific decisions. Job design decides who gets to decide what, which strongly shapes behavior and accountability.

- **Scientific Management (Taylorism):** The approach from Frederick Taylor: break work into small, standard tasks; use time-and-motion studies to find the best way; train workers to follow it perfectly; pay by the piece. Maximum efficiency through deskilling and standardization.

- **Deskilling:** Making tasks simpler on purpose so lower-skilled (and lower-paid) workers can do them — a feature of Taylorism that cuts costs but also cuts engagement and flexibility.

- **Hawthorne Effect:** The finding that workers change their behavior when they know they are being watched — discovered at Western Electric's Hawthorne plant, which helped start the human relations movement.

- **Job Enrichment:** Redesigning jobs to have more skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback — the psychological fix for Taylorist boredom.

- **High-Performance Work System (HPWS):** A management system that combines many practices that work together — broad job design, team-based work, employee participation, information sharing, performance-based pay, and training — to create a self-reinforcing system of engagement and performance.

- **Systems of Work:** The idea that individual HR practices (pay, job design, training, participation) affect each other. The effect of any one practice depends on what other practices are in place.

- **NUMMI (New United Motor Manufacturing, Inc.):** The joint Toyota-GM plant in Fremont, California, where Toyota applied its production system to the same workers who had been GM's worst performers — and got dramatically better quality and productivity.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The same workers, under a different management system, produce very different results.** The NUMMI case is one of the most powerful natural experiments in management history. The Fremont plant's workers had a confrontational union, had sabotaged production under GM, and were the company's worst. Under Toyota's system — with more autonomy, team-based problem-solving, and the power to stop the line — the same workers made cars with quality as good as Japan's. The system was what changed, not the people.

2. **Taylor was right about efficiency but wrong about people.** Scientific management really did create huge productivity gains in early 20th-century factories. But the evidence from Western Electric, GM in the 1970s, and many other cases shows the limits: when work is so simple it is meaningless, when workers have no choice, and when pay is purely by the piece, the result is boredom, sabotage, and falling quality. Taylor optimized for the single task in isolation. He missed the social and psychological context where work actually happens.

3. **Job enrichment must improve all five job traits to work.** Research on job design consistently finds that skill variety, task identity (doing a whole piece of work), task significance, autonomy, and feedback all add to motivation. Improving one or two without the others gives limited results. Giving workers autonomy over meaningless tasks, or feedback on things they cannot change, does not help much.

4. **Isolated HR practices do not work — systems of practices do.** Profit sharing alone has almost no effect on performance. Add employee participation and information sharing, and the effect becomes large and positive. Practices interact. Hiring enthusiastic people into jobs with no freedom wastes the hiring effort. Designing rich jobs for workers with no training creates anxiety, not engagement. The whole system must fit together.

5. **Some management systems create advantages that cannot be copied piece by piece.** Nordstrom's service model depends on hiring enthusiastic people, paying good commissions, and giving huge freedom over customer interactions. Holt Chemical's customer service model depended on hiring engineers as salespeople, which worked because their deep knowledge created real customer trust — until they added sales incentives and that trust was destroyed. The advantage came from the whole system, and the system was fragile when individual pieces were changed.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Five Job Traits Model | Skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, feedback — each adds to motivation | When redesigning jobs to improve engagement and performance |
| Taylorist vs. High-Performance Design | A range from max specialization/control to broad roles/empowerment | When figuring out if a job design is right for the type of work |
| High-Performance Work System Framework | A set of practices that work together (job design, teamwork, pay, training, participation, information sharing) | When designing or checking a management system as a whole, not practice by practice |
| Profit Sharing + Participation Rule | Research shows profit sharing alone ≈ no effect; profit sharing + participation ≈ big positive effect | When deciding if a financial incentive will work without other changes |

## F. Practical Implications

- When redesigning a job to improve motivation, check it against all five job traits before making changes. Adding autonomy without adding feedback often increases anxiety, not motivation.

- Before blaming poor performance on individual workers, look at the work system: are workers given the information, authority, and tools to do well? GM Fremont's workers were not bad workers — they were bad workers in a system that made good work impossible.

- When adding a new HR practice (profit sharing, team incentives, 360-degree feedback), figure out which other practices it depends on — and make sure those are in place first.

- Be careful about changing single pieces of a high-performance system that works. Adding sales incentives to Holt Chemical's engineer-salespeople destroyed the trust-based customer relationships that made the system special. System fit is fragile.

The Holt Chemical case was the most useful warning in this module. A high-performance system built on engineer-salespeople's real expertise destroyed its own advantage by adding a commission structure — a change that made sense in isolation but broke the system's logic. This is the hard truth about high-performance systems: they are more fragile than they look, because their power comes from how the parts interact, and changing one part can break the whole.

The Southwest Airlines flight attendant video shows the opposite: job enrichment through creative freedom within required tasks. The attendant adding her own personality to the safety instructions is exactly what Taylor's scientific management would have removed — and exactly what makes Southwest's service different and makes the job satisfying. The contrast with a strict script makes the case for autonomy better than any research study could.

# Module 3 — Making Good and Timely Management Decisions

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Organizations are making decisions faster, with less information, in more uncertain conditions than ever before. This module looks at what good decision-making looks like in these conditions — using examples from trade talks, military leadership, space exploration, and firefighting. The main argument is that speed and quality are not opposites. The Marine Corps rule of deciding with 70% of the information is not recklessness — it is knowing that the cost of not deciding is often worse than the cost of a slightly wrong early decision. But speed without good process produces overconfidence and disaster — as the Wagner Dodge firefighting case shows at deadly cost.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Strategic Intent:** A clear, shared statement of what the organization or team is trying to achieve. The "commander's intent" lets people below make their own decisions that still fit the larger goal, even when they cannot check with the leader.

- **70% Rule (Marine Corps Rule):** The rule that officers should decide when they have about 70% of the information they would ideally want. Waiting for 100% causes indecision, which in fast-changing situations is itself a bad choice.

- **Overconfidence Bias:** The proven tendency for people (especially new managers and those who have just succeeded) to think their judgments are more accurate than they really are and to think things will go as planned more than they should.

- **After-Action Review (AAR):** A structured review after an event used in the military: what was supposed to happen, what actually happened, why was there a difference, and what should be done differently next time?

- **Team Cohesion:** How much team members trust each other and act together under pressure. High cohesion improves decision speed and follow-through — but it requires time spent building relationships and shared experience beforehand.

- **Collaborative Decision-Making:** Involving trusted advisors or team members in a decision before finalizing it — not to get everyone to agree, but to get information and views that the main decision-maker lacks.

- **Indecisiveness as a Risk:** The idea that failing to decide is itself a decision — often the worst one, because it gives control to outside forces or competitors.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Good decisions need trusted people who will give you honest pushback, not just agreement.** John Chambers at Cisco talks about the value of a trusted internal advisor who gives honest feedback before big decisions. This is not about committees. It is about building the social infrastructure to challenge the leader's assumptions before they commit, not after.

2. **Speed is a competitive necessity, but it requires preparation.** The Marine Corps rule of deciding at 70% information only works because Marine officers train extensively in tactics, so they have internalized the patterns that let them judge quickly in new situations. "Decide fast" without that preparation leads to overconfident errors. The lesson for companies: invest in scenario training and decision frameworks ahead of time, so when speed is needed, decision quality does not collapse.

3. **Overconfidence is the most dangerous bias for successful managers.** Research shows that managers who just had a success are more overconfident than those who have not — exactly when their confidence is most rewarded socially. The fix is to deliberately seek out information that might be wrong, build in devil's advocate processes, and truly listen to advisors who will tell you what you do not want to hear.

4. **Trust cannot be built in a crisis — it must be built before.** The Wagner Dodge firefighting case is the module's most powerful lesson: Dodge invented a life-saving technique (starting a fire to burn a safe area) on the spot, but his team did not trust him enough to follow his unusual instruction. Thirteen died. The lesson is not that Dodge failed as a leader in the moment — he made the right call. The lesson is that trust had not been built enough during normal times to support a new idea in a crisis. Trust is an investment that pays off exactly when things go wrong.

5. **Clear strategic intent enables fast, distributed decision-making.** In complex, fast-moving situations — military operations, market disruptions, service crises — waiting for centralized decisions creates deadly delays. When every team member understands the intent clearly enough to make local decisions that fit the big picture, the organization acts as one without needing real-time coordination. This requires leaders to explain purpose, not just tasks.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| 70% Rule | Decide when you have about 70% of the information you would like — do not wait for certainty | When speed matters and delay has compounding costs |
| Strategic Intent Communication | State what you are trying to achieve and why, not just what to do — lets people below act on their own | When delegating in fast-moving or uncertain situations |
| After-Action Review (AAR) | Structured review after an event: planned vs. actual, gap analysis, lessons | After any important decision or operation, especially failures |
| Collaborative Pre-Decision Process | Bring in trusted advisors before committing — not for agreement but for challenge and information | Before big, hard-to-reverse decisions |
| Anti-Overconfidence Practices | Devil's advocate, pre-mortem analysis, forcing yourself to look at disconfirming data | When a team or leader shows signs of overconfidence or recent-success bias |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No math. The tools in this module are about process and behavior.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Build trust with your team during calm, normal times — the investment pays off when you need them to follow an unusual call under pressure. Trust cannot be built in a crisis.

- When giving direction to your team, always state the intent (why this matters and what we are trying to achieve) along with the task (what to do). Intent lets people act on their own, in the right direction, even when they cannot reach you for guidance.

- After any significant failure or close call, do a structured after-action review — not to blame, but to learn. Frame it clearly: "What was supposed to happen, what actually happened, and what do we do differently next time?"

- Watch for overconfidence, especially after a string of wins. Find people who will give you honest disagreement, and listen to them more when you feel most sure of yourself.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Wagner Dodge case stays with you. Dodge did everything right in the crisis moment. He read the situation correctly, invented a smart response on the spot, and explained it clearly. The failure was not his crisis leadership — it was the lack of trust needed for his team to follow a never-before-seen instruction from someone they had not fully bonded with. This changes the lesson completely: the time to build trust that saves lives in a crisis is not during the crisis. It is during every normal operation that came before.

One small pushback: the 70% rule could be used to justify acting without enough information in situations where the cost of being wrong is huge and you cannot reverse it. In military operations, speed often matters more than perfect information because not deciding is immediately bad. In many business decisions — big investments, key hires, major strategy changes — waiting another week for better information is often cheaper than making a big mistake. The 70% rule depends on context, and the module could say more clearly when it applies and when it does not.

# Module 4 — Designing and Changing Organizational Architecture

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Organizational architecture — the structure of roles, reporting lines, decision rights, information flows, and coordination methods — is the most powerful and least visible influence on employee behavior. This module argues that organizational design is not just drawing boxes and lines. It is the main way management shapes what decisions get made, by whom, with what information, and with what incentives. The module also tackles the hardest problem in management: changing an architecture that no longer works, against the resistance of people whose careers and comfort depend on the existing structure.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Organizational Architecture:** The overall design of an organization — including formal structure (hierarchy, reporting lines), decision rights (who decides what), information systems (what data goes where), and performance evaluation and reward systems.

- **Functional Organization:** A structure where people are grouped by specialty (all engineers together, all marketers together). Efficient for deep expertise. Bad for coordination across different functions.

- **Integrated (Product/Customer) Structure:** A structure where people are grouped around products, customers, or projects — crossing functional lines. Better for coordination. May sacrifice deep expertise.

- **Decision Rights:** The formal and informal authority to make specific types of decisions. When decision rights do not match the information and incentives needed to decide well, the organization fails.

- **Inertial Guidance:** The pattern where an organization keeps doing what it was set up to do, even after conditions change — like a ship's gyroscope that keeps the same course even when a new direction is needed.

- **Forces Against Change (Kotter's 8 Errors):** Eight common reasons change fails: not enough urgency, weak coalition, unclear vision, under-communication, not removing obstacles, not enough short-term wins, declaring victory too early, and not anchoring changes in culture.

- **Organizational Equity:** Employees' sense of fairness in how rewards (pay, recognition, office space, titles) are given out compared to what they contribute. When people feel things are unfair — in either direction — it affects performance.

- **Perceived Over-Reward:** The less-discussed fairness condition where an employee feels they are being paid more than they deserve. This actually leads to higher effort in the short term (working harder to feel deserving), but it is less stable than true fairness.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Structure shapes behavior more than management speeches do.** The Rose Company case shows this: plant managers who are given profit-and-loss responsibility behave differently from those who are just cost center managers — not because they are different people, but because the structure signals different expectations, gives different information, and rewards different outcomes. Change the structure, change the behavior. Telling people to behave differently within an unchanged structure rarely works.

2. **Organizational design always involves trade-offs — the question is which trade-off you can live with.** Functional structures optimize for depth and efficiency within specialties, but coordination across functions is hard. Integrated structures optimize for coordination, but depth in each specialty suffers. Neither is always better. The Charles Schwab case shows a specific version: organizing around the old brokerage model made sense for the old business. The internet needed a different structure, and the resistance to changing it was not irrational — it was the functional organization protecting its existing value.

3. **Organizations build structures to solve today's problems using yesterday's thinking.** Inertial guidance is real. Once an organizational structure is in place, it becomes self-reinforcing. People hire others who fit it. Systems are built around it. Careers depend on it. Changing it requires not just a new design but actively taking apart the social and political machinery that keeps the old one going.

4. **Organizational change fails in predictable ways, for identifiable reasons.** Kotter's research on change failures lists eight errors that explain most failed change efforts. The most dangerous is not enough urgency. When people do not truly believe the current situation is unsustainable, they will always prefer the comfort of what they know over the disruption of something new. Creating real urgency — not fake drama, but honest talk about what is at stake — is the precondition for successful change.

5. **Unfair rewards distort behavior in measurable ways.** The NBA study and the insurance underwriter study make a clear point: feeling underpaid reduces effort; feeling overpaid temporarily increases it. This is not just a morale issue — it is a performance issue. Organizational designs that create visible pay gaps (even by accident) are creating performance problems that no pep talk can fix.

---

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |

| Organizational Architecture Design | Aligning structure, decision rights, information systems, and rewards to support desired behaviors | When designing a new organization or figuring out why current structure creates bad behaviors |

| Kotter's 8 Change Errors | Eight common reasons change initiatives fail — a checklist for diagnosis | When planning or evaluating a major organizational change |
| Equity Theory Applied to Architecture | People's sense of fairness shapes effort — unfairness in either direction is costly | When designing pay systems or figuring out performance problems related to perceived unfairness |
| Decision Rights Alignment | Match authority to decide with the information and incentives needed to decide well | When redesigning roles after a structural change or figuring out why good decisions are not being made |

> *No math. The tools are diagnostic frameworks and case analysis.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- When performance problems keep happening despite coaching, feedback, and incentives, look at the organizational architecture: does the structure give people the information, authority, and incentives they need to do well? If not, changing people will not fix a structural problem.

- When redesigning an organization, map out decision rights clearly: who decides what, and what information do they need to decide well? Decision rights that do not match information (authority without information, or information without authority) are a main cause of bad decisions.

- When starting an organizational change, begin with an honest check of urgency: do the people who need to change truly believe the current situation cannot continue? If not, work on that before announcing the new design.

- Be thoughtful about visible reward signals — office size, title, resources, and pay all communicate relative value to the organization. Unintended unfairness signals create performance consequences that no scorecard will show.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Charles Schwab case is the most business-relevant case in the course. It shows a problem that many organizations face but few diagnose clearly: a new competitive threat (online trading) that requires the company to hurt its own profitable existing business. Pottruck's challenge was not analytical — he could see clearly what needed to happen. It was political and organizational: how do you get stakeholders (board, branch managers, existing customers) to support a change that will hurt them in the short term? The answer the course implies — building the case carefully, showing data, managing the board relationship, creating urgency — is right, but it understates how rare and hard this kind of organizational courage actually is.

The equity finding that over-rewarded people initially work harder (to feel deserving) is something I had not seen before. It partly reframes the case for pay transparency. If people who are overpaid increase effort while people who are underpaid decrease it, the net effect of visible pay gaps on overall performance is not clear — which makes the argument for pay transparency more complex.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course's most lasting contribution is a unified way of thinking about managing people as system design, not just individual fixes. The insight running through all four modules is that behavior comes from the system — incentive structures, job designs, decision processes, and organizational architectures — not just from individual motivation or skill. Fixing people problems by focusing on people (counseling, coaching, pep talks) while leaving the system unchanged is treating symptoms. The system produces the behavior. Changing the behavior requires changing the system. This reframe is both the most important and the hardest thing for managers to truly accept, because it takes away the comfortable option of blaming poor performance on individuals.
