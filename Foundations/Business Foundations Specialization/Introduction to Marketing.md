## 🧭 Course Overview

This course builds a strategic framework for understanding and practicing marketing as something far more sophisticated than advertising or sales. It moves across five interconnected domains — branding and positioning, customer centricity, go-to-market strategies, brand communications, and applied cases — using real-world examples (Apple, Nike, Nordstrom, Crocs, Electronic Arts) and research evidence throughout. The two professors bring complementary lenses: Kahn focuses on brand strategy, consumer perception, and the psychology of marketing; Fader brings customer-centric analytics, lifetime value thinking, and data-driven business models. Together they make the case that marketing is not a support function — it is the discipline of creating, communicating, and delivering superior customer value, which is the essence of competitive strategy.

# Module 1 — Branding, Marketing Strategy and Brand Positioning

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Marketing is not advertising. It is not selling. It is the discipline of understanding markets — exchanges between buyers and sellers — and designing offerings that create superior value for targeted customers. This module opens by distinguishing product-focused marketing (build the best product, reduce costs, gain market share) from customer-focused marketing (understand what specific customers want, create value through premium pricing and loyalty). It then introduces the foundational frameworks of modern marketing: the STP framework (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) and the 4 P's (Product, Price, Promotion, Place). The module closes with the concept of the brand mantra — a three-word distillation of a brand's essence — and experiential branding as the evolution beyond rational product claims.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Marketing:** The study of markets — exchanges between buyers and sellers that do not always involve money. Marketing is about creating and delivering superior value to targeted customers.
- **Seller's Market vs. Buyer's Market:** In seller's markets, sellers have power and focus on products; in buyer's markets, buyers have power and focus on customer needs.
- **Product-Focused Marketing:** Emphasizes developing the best product, reducing costs, and increasing market share as the path to profitability.
- **Customer-Focused Marketing:** Prioritizes understanding what specific customers want and creating value through premium pricing, loyalty, and cross-selling.
- **STP Framework (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning):** The foundational strategic framework. Divide the market into segments (Segmentation), choose which segment to serve (Targeting), and design your offering to meet that segment's needs (Positioning).
- **Four P's of Marketing (Marketing Mix):** Product (what is offered), Price (what the buyer gives), Promotion (how benefits are communicated), Place (how the product is delivered).
- **Brand:** More than a trademark — a promise, consistency, and set of benefits that customers expect. Ultimately, a brand is defined by what customers think it is.
- **Positioning Statement:** Includes three components: the target segment, the point of difference, and the frame of reference.
- **Brand Mantra:** A short (often three-word) phrase that captures the heart and soul of a brand — the elevator speech for internal guidance. Examples: Nike ("Authentic, Athletic, Performance"), Disney ("Fun Family Entertainment").
- **Experiential Branding:** Branding that engages all senses and emotions — sensory, emotional, cognitive, behavioral, and social elements — not just rational product claims.
- **Mental Maps:** Graphical representations of consumer associations with a brand, showing connections and strength of associations — used to identify key brand values.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Marketing orientation has evolved through four distinct phases.** The course identifies a progression: Product orientation (focus on innovation and cost reduction) → Marketing orientation (focus on customer value and differentiation) → Experience orientation (co-creating value with customers, managing social buzz) → Trust orientation (long-term relationships, transparency, genuine value). Each phase responds to changing market conditions — the rise of globalization, internet, and social media has made trust orientation increasingly essential.

2. **You cannot serve everyone profitably — segmentation is not optional.** The STP framework's core insight is that attempting to appeal to everyone produces a "lukewarm" average offering that fully satisfies no one. Effective positioning requires choosing a defensible point of difference that is relevant, distinctive, and deliverable — and then having the discipline to say no to everything else.

3. **A brand is what customers think it is, not what you say it is.** This is the most important sentence in the module. Brand positioning statements and mantras are internal guidance tools; they are not the brand itself. The brand lives in customer perceptions, which can only be understood through market research. No amount of internal messaging can override what customers actually believe.

4. **Experiential branding differentiates beyond rational features.** When products become commoditized, the brand experience becomes the primary differentiator. Nike's co-creation (designing your own shoes), Apple's emotional advertising, and flagship retail stores all create experiential value that cannot be copied by simply matching product specifications. The four P's can all be made experiential: product (customization), promotion (emotional ads), price (innovative pricing like auctions), place (interactive retail environments).

5. **Strong brands make consistent promises and keep them.** The characteristics of strong brands cluster around consistency: clear promises, superior and distinctive products, strong emotional connections, internal/external alignment, and adaptability. The brand mantra serves as the internal compass that maintains this consistency across products, advertising, and platforms.

## F. Practical Implications

- Before designing any marketing campaign, complete the STP analysis: Who are we serving? Why them? What point of difference will we deliver to them? The answers guide every subsequent decision.
- Write a brand mantra of three words for your brand or product. If you cannot do it in three words, you do not understand your brand's essence clearly enough. If the mantra does not help you make decisions about what NOT to do, it is not specific enough.
- When evaluating a brand's health, start with customer perceptions — not internal metrics. Conduct mental mapping research to understand what associations customers actually have, then compare that to what you want them to have. The gap is your strategic priority.
- Recognize which marketing orientation your industry demands. In commoditized categories with transparent pricing, trust orientation may be the only sustainable advantage. In innovative categories, product orientation may still work — but know which game you are playing.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The distinction between what a brand says it is and what customers think it is is deceptively simple but operationally profound. Most organizations spend vastly more time on internal messaging (mission statements, brand guidelines, internal communications) than on measuring and shaping external perceptions. The course's emphasis on mental mapping as the starting point for any brand work is a corrective to this tendency — but it underspecifies how to conduct such research in practice. What methods yield reliable mental maps? How do you distinguish stable associations from transient ones? The answers exist in the market research literature, but the course does not go there.

The experiential branding framework is useful but potentially inflationary: if every element of the marketing mix can be "experiential," the term risks losing its distinctive meaning. The core insight — that rational product claims are insufficient when products are similar — stands. But not every brand needs or can afford flagship stores and co-creation platforms. The framework would benefit from clearer guidance on when experiential branding is worth the investment versus when traditional differentiation suffices.

# Module 2 — Customer Centricity: The Limits of Product-Centric Thinking

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Most companies are organized around products: product lines, product managers, product-level P&Ls. This product-centric approach worked when products had long life cycles, customers were passive, and data was scarce. But technology has accelerated product life cycles, empowered customers with information, and created unprecedented data about individual behavior. This module argues for customer centricity as an alternative: focusing on a select set of valuable customers and maximizing their long-term financial value (Customer Lifetime Value, or CLV), rather than maximizing volume or market share. The module is honest about the challenges — customer centricity is radical, requires organizational restructuring, and may not work for all companies — but makes a compelling case for why every company needs to understand it.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Product Centricity:** The traditional business model centered on product expertise, scaling production, efficiency, and metrics like market share and cost reduction. Growth comes from extending existing products to new customers or creating new products.
- **Customer Centricity (Customer Intimacy):** A business strategy focused on a select set of valuable customers, maximizing their long-term financial value rather than volume or share.
- **Customer Lifetime Value (CLV):** The projected future financial value of a customer relationship — the central metric in customer-centric management. Forward-looking, not just based on past purchases.
- **Cracks in Product-Centricity:** Five forces undermining the product-centric model: commoditization (technology accelerates life cycles), smarter customers (internet-empowered and demanding), instantaneous availability (global distribution), deregulation (increased competition), and demand for bundled solutions.
- **Data-Driven Business Models:** Business models built on customer data and insights rather than product features. Examples: Harrah's loyalty program tracking individual gambling behavior; Tesco's tailored coupons based on purchase history.
- **Direct Marketing:** The origins of customer centricity — building business around individual customers, collecting data on purchases, inquiries, returns, and interactions. Concepts like segmentation and CLV originated here.
- **Customer Portfolio Approach:** Managing customers like a financial portfolio — balancing high-value "high flyer" customers (high risk, high return) with stable, less valuable customers (bonds and cash equivalents) for overall stability.
- **Customer Acquisition, Retention, Development:** The three core tactics of customer-centric management. Must be prioritized and integrated strategically — not all customers deserve the same investment in each tactic.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The product-centric model is cracking under five pressures.** The module's diagnosis of why product-centricity is failing is precise: commoditization (technology accelerates product life cycles), smarter customers (internet-empowered and demanding), instantaneous availability (global distribution makes products accessible everywhere), deregulation (increased competition), and demand for bundled solutions (customers want integrated products and services, not standalone items). Any one of these would strain the product-centric model; together, they are transforming it.

2. **Not all customers are equally valuable — and acting as if they are destroys value.** This is customer centricity's most controversial claim. In product-centric thinking, all customers are treated roughly the same because the goal is volume. In customer-centric thinking, the goal is maximizing long-term value — which requires recognizing that some customers are inherently more profitable than others, and allocating resources accordingly. Treating all customers equally means under-serving valuable customers while over-serving unprofitable ones.

3. **Customer centricity is radical, not incremental.** The module is unusually honest about this. Customer centricity may require restructuring the organization around customer segments rather than product lines (as Procter & Gamble did targeting African American women). It may require rewarding salespeople based on future CLV rather than immediate sales. It may require investing in customers who are not yet valuable (like MBA students who will become valuable travelers later). These changes conflict with traditional short-term performance metrics and face cultural resistance.

4. **A balanced customer portfolio manages risk.** The financial portfolio metaphor is clarifying: high-value customers are like high-growth stocks — potentially very profitable but also volatile and risky. Stable, less valuable customers are like bonds — lower returns but reliable. Smart customer-centric companies maintain both. The paradox of customer centricity is that focusing too intensely on highly valuable customers makes the company vulnerable if market conditions change or those customers leave.

5. **True customer centricity is easier for some companies than others.** Companies with a small, highly valuable customer base (B2B, luxury, specialized services) can implement customer centricity more fully. Companies with millions of low-value customers (CPG, grocery retail) face a harder challenge: they cannot personally know each customer, so they must rely on data and segmentation to approximate customer centricity at scale. The course suggests a hybrid approach: focus R&D and growth efforts on valuable customers while maintaining operational efficiency for the rest.

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before investing in customer acquisition, calculate the CLV of the customers you are acquiring. Many companies spend more to acquire customers than those customers will ever generate in profit — a destructive cycle that product-centric volume thinking encourages and CLV thinking prevents.
- Do not abandon product-centric practices entirely. The most successful companies balance both: they use customer-centric insights to guide R&D and product development (Electronic Arts) while maintaining operational excellence for less valuable segments.
- When implementing customer centricity, start with a pilot program or targeted initiative. Do not attempt a full organizational transformation overnight. Test, learn, and scale what works.
- Recognize that customer centricity may not be feasible for all parts of your business. The module's honesty about this is valuable: some industries (regulated, low-margin, high-volume) face genuine barriers. The goal is to be as customer-centric as your context allows, not to achieve purity.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The module's honesty about the limits of customer centricity is its greatest strength. Many marketing texts present customer centricity as a universal solution; this course acknowledges that it is radical, difficult, and not equally feasible for all companies. The recognition that even Nordstrom — the exemplar of customer service — is not truly customer-centric because it treats all customers the same rather than recognizing differential future value is provocative and clarifying.

The Electronic Arts case in the optional module (appearing later in the course) is the best illustration of the balanced approach: EA did not abandon product-centric practices (blockbuster game launches still matter) but added customer-centric insights to guide development and acquisition. This hybrid model is likely the future for most companies: not either/or, but both/and, with the balance depending on industry context.

What the module underspecifies is how to calculate CLV in practice for companies with millions of customers and long purchase cycles. The startups mentioned (Two Six Capital, CLVmetrics.com) are attempting to solve this, but the course does not provide enough methodological guidance for managers to implement CLV on their own. This is a gap that more advanced courses would fill.

# Module 3 — Go to Market Strategies: Communications Strategy & Fundamentals of Pricing

## A. Core Idea of the Module

A great product with a strong brand and clear positioning still fails if customers do not know about it (communications failure), do not want to pay for it (pricing failure), or cannot get it (distribution failure). This module covers all three go-to-market functions. The communications section introduces the 7 M's framework for campaign design and distinguishes rational from emotional appeals. The pricing section is unusually comprehensive: price elasticity, psychological pricing effects, break-even analysis, and Economic Value to Customer (EVC). The distribution section covers channel design, channel conflict, and the shift to omni-channel retail. The module's core argument is that these three functions must be aligned with each other and with the overall positioning — but pricing has the largest single impact on profitability.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **7 M's of Communications Campaign Design:** The seven critical decisions: (1) Who to talk to (target audience), (2) What to say (message content), (3) What to achieve (objectives), (4) How to say it (creative strategy), (5) Media strategy (channels), (6) Money (budget), (7) Measurement (evaluation).
- **AIDA Model:** Stages of consumer response: Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. Mass media works best for Attention and Interest; personal selling and word-of-mouth work best for Desire and Action.
- **Paid, Earned, Owned Media:** Paid media (TV ads, search ads), earned media (publicity from events or PR), owned media (company websites, social accounts). Campaigns must decide the emphasis on each.
- **Price Elasticity:** Percentage change in demand divided by percentage change in price. Unitless, comparable across products. Elastic demand (>1): price increase reduces revenue. Inelastic demand (<1): price increase increases revenue.
- **Psychological Pricing Effects:** Prices ending in 9 increase sales even when higher than alternatives without the 9-ending. The Weber-Fechner law: consumers respond to percentage changes more than absolute changes.
- **Endowment Effect:** Sense of ownership increases willingness to pay — people value items more if they feel they own them, even hypothetically.
- **Reference Prices:** Consumers compare prices to past prices or list prices — the perceived "deal" depends on the reference point, not just the absolute price.
- **Economic Value to Customer (EVC):** The maximum price a customer is willing to pay for a new product compared to the cost of existing alternatives. Helps determine pricing, targeting, and go/no-go decisions.
- **Break-Even Analysis:** Calculating the additional sales volume needed to cover a fixed cost (like advertising) or the sales increase required to justify a price reduction.
- **Channel Conflict:** Vertical conflict (company vs. channel partners) and horizontal conflict (among channel partners, e.g., showrooming and webrooming).
- **Omni-Channel Retail:** Integrated online and offline channels providing a seamless customer experience — distinct from multi-channel (operating separate channels without integration).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Pricing has the largest impact on profitability of any marketing lever.** The statistic is striking: a 1% improvement in price increases operating profits by about 11%, compared to smaller impacts from improving variable costs, fixed costs, or sales volume. Despite this, managers spend more time on cost reduction than pricing strategy — a gap the course attributes to lack of training and psychological discomfort with pricing decisions.

2. **Price elasticity is not fixed — it is driven by identifiable factors.** The module lists drivers of price sensitivity: availability of substitutes, ease of price comparison, total expenditure (higher expenditure = higher sensitivity), cost sharing (when others pay, sensitivity drops), switching costs, price-quality inference, and ability to carry inventory. Understanding these drivers allows managers to predict elasticity before measuring it.

3. **Psychological pricing effects are real and measurable.** The 9-ending effect is not a myth — studies show 59-cent pricing increases sales more than larger price drops without the 9-ending. The Weber-Fechner law explains why a $5 saving on a $10 product feels significant while the same saving on a $1,000 product feels trivial. Context effects (offering good/better/best options) steer consumers toward the middle option. These effects are not irrational — they are predictable and exploitable.

4. **Break-even analysis is the essential tool for pricing experiments.** Before changing a price, calculate the break-even sales change needed to maintain profitability. This framework allows managers to test pricing strategies through market experiments: if actual sales change exceeds break-even, the price change is profitable; if not, revert. The same logic applies to advertising spending and new product launches.

5. **Economic Value to Customer (EVC) reveals who your true competitors are.** EVC analysis compares a new product to existing alternatives by calculating the total cost savings or performance improvements. This reveals not just the maximum price but also which customers value the product most (those with higher costs under existing solutions) and who the true competitors are (who loses value if the new product succeeds). EVC may identify competitors in adjacent industries, not just direct product competitors.

6. **Distribution channels capture 30-40% of price — yet receive less management attention than advertising (typically <10% of price).** This ratio is striking and suggests that distribution decisions are under-managed relative to their importance. The module argues that channel innovation (IKEA outsourcing assembly, Apple internalizing consultative selling) can be a source of competitive advantage as powerful as product innovation.

7. **Channel conflict is inevitable — the goal is healthy management, not elimination.** Showrooming (examine offline, buy online) and webrooming (research online, buy offline) are both forms of horizontal conflict. The solution is not to eliminate one channel but to design clear roles, responsibilities, and compensation structures upfront, with rules for handling conflict when it arises.

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Price Elasticity (E):** E = (% Change in Quantity Demanded) / (% Change in Price)
  - Elastic: \|E\| > 1 → Price increase reduces revenue
  - Inelastic: \|E\| < 1 → Price increase increases revenue
  - Unit elastic: \|E\| = 1 → Revenue unchanged

- **Break-Even Sales Change for Price Reduction:**
  - Required % Change in Sales = (Price Change %) / (New Margin %)

- **Break-Even Advertising:**
  - Additional units needed = Advertising Cost / Margin per Unit
  - Or: Additional customers needed = Advertising Cost / (Margin per Customer)

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before any price change, estimate the break-even sales change. If you believe actual sales will change more than break-even, the price change is worth testing. If not, do not proceed.
- When designing a communications campaign, start with the target segment and message — not the media. Media choice follows strategy, not the other way around. The 7 M's sequence matters: do not skip to "money" before defining objectives.
- Use the Objectives and Tasks method for budgeting, not percentage-of-sales. Percentage-of-sales is circular (sales determine budget, budget determines sales) and agencies love it because it increases their fees.
- For products with high non-digital attributes (feel, brightness, speed), consider strategies to reduce purchase anxiety: home try-on (Warby Parker), long return windows (Casper's 100 days), or physical showrooms.
- When launching a new product, calculate EVC for different customer segments. Some segments will have higher EVC because they face higher costs with existing solutions — target them first.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The module's emphasis on pricing as the highest-leverage marketing lever is empirically correct but psychologically uncomfortable for most managers. The discomfort is rational: pricing changes are visible to customers, can signal quality shifts, and may provoke competitive responses. The module acknowledges these complexities but could spend more time on competitive pricing dynamics — how to anticipate and respond to competitor price changes, and when price wars are inevitable versus avoidable.

The 7 M's framework is comprehensive but somewhat mechanical. The creative strategy and message content decisions (M's 2 and 4) are where most campaigns succeed or fail, and the framework provides less guidance there than on the more measurable elements (budget, media, measurement). The California Milk campaign example is useful but the module would benefit from more diverse cases across different product categories and budget levels.

The distribution section is the shortest but introduces the most counterintuitive insight: channels capture 30-40% of price, yet receive less management attention than advertising. This ratio suggests that many companies are under-investing in channel strategy relative to its profit impact. The omni-channel imperative — integrating online and offline seamlessly — is now table stakes, not differentiation. The module's examples (IKEA, Apple, Warby Parker) illustrate how distribution innovation can be a source of sustainable advantage.

# Module 4 — Branding: Effective Brand Communications Strategies and Repositioning Strategies

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Brands live in consumer perceptions, and perceptions are biased, selective, and constructive. This module opens with the psychology of perception — how consumers interpret marketing stimuli through prior beliefs, selective exposure, and cognitive biases. It then moves through the tactical elements of brand building: brand names, colors, slogans, packaging, and the persuasion process (including the Elaboration Likelihood Model and celebrity endorsements). The module closes with repositioning: when and how to change a brand's position without losing existing customers. The central argument is that brand consistency and brand evolution are not opposites — they must be managed together through deliberate, psychology-informed strategy.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Perception Process:** Exposure (sensory inputs) → Attention (voluntary or involuntary) → Interpretation (biased by prior expectations). Consumers selectively expose themselves to and attend to stimuli, then interpret them through existing beliefs.
- **Selective Exposure/Attention:** Consumers avoid stimuli that contradict their beliefs and pay attention to stimuli that confirm them — a major barrier to changing brand perceptions.
- **Proximity Bias:** Consumers associate products placed close together as related — store brands exploit this by placing private labels next to national brands.
- **Similarity Bias:** Consumers assume products that look alike have similar quality — another tactic used by store brands.
- **Brand Elements:** The building blocks of brand identity: brand name, logos, symbols, characters, packaging, slogans, colors. Must work together cohesively.
- **Brand Name Types:** Descriptive (explains what it is), metaphorical (implies meaning), legacy (people's names), ambiguous (open to interpretation), altered (misspelled), blended (combined words), invented (wholly new).
- **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM):** Two routes to persuasion: central route (systematic, cognitive processing when motivation and ability are high) and peripheral route (superficial cues when motivation or ability is low).
- **Peripheral Cues:** Heuristic shortcuts: classical conditioning, reciprocity, consistency, social proof, liking, authority, scarcity.
- **Transfer of Meaning Model:** Celebrities carry symbolic meanings that transfer to endorsed brands — enhancing brand image and differentiation.
- **Repositioning:** Changing a brand's position to stay fresh, relevant, and consistent over time. Reasons: flawed initial positioning, unprofitable target audience, outdated brand, or just feeling "tired."
- **Just Noticeable Difference (JND):** Gradual changes that keep a brand modern without losing recognition — the opposite of radical, noticeable change (Butterfly Effect).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Perception is not passive — it is biased and constructive.** The module's opening on perception psychology is foundational. Consumers do not see marketing stimuli objectively; they interpret them through prior beliefs, selectively attend to confirming information, and avoid disconfirming information. This means that changing brand perceptions is difficult — not because consumers are stubborn, but because the perceptual system is designed to maintain consistency. Marketing communications that contradict existing beliefs are likely to be ignored or reinterpreted, not accepted.

2. **Brands shape perception so strongly that they alter physical experience.** The Coca-Cola example is striking: the brand name alone increases perceived taste and quality, allowing premium pricing. Brand extensions (Diet Coke) benefit from existing brand equity, influencing consumer expectations and acceptance of new products. This is not "irrational" — it is how perception works. The implication is that brand building is not about providing information; it is about shaping the interpretive framework through which all information is processed.

3. **Brand names are the anchor — choose them carefully.** The module provides a taxonomy of brand name types (descriptive, metaphorical, legacy, ambiguous, altered, blended, invented) and criteria for selection: memorable, meaningful, appealing, protectable, adaptable, culturally appropriate. The examples of translation challenges (brand names in Chinese) illustrate the complexity of global branding. Changing an established brand name is extremely difficult and rarely successful.

4. **Color and slogans can be owned as brand assets.** Owning a distinctive color (Tiffany blue, Mary Kay pink) is a powerful differentiator. Colors convey meaning (red stimulates appetite, blue conveys trust, green suggests health). Effective slogans reinforce positioning: they can be imperative ("Just Do It"), descriptive, superlative, or provocative. The best slogans are short, unique, easy to remember, and culturally appropriate.

5. **Packaging is often the most overlooked brand element.** The historical shift to self-service supermarkets made packaging the primary sales tool. Experiments show packaging design influences not just choice but perceived product effectiveness. Iconic packaging (Coca-Cola bottle, Absolut vodka, Heinz ketchup) becomes inseparable from the brand identity. Innovations in packaging (refrigerator packs, easy-to-use ketchup containers) can increase market share by improving convenience.

6. **Persuasion requires matching the route to the consumer's motivation and ability.** The Elaboration Likelihood Model is a practical framework: when consumers are motivated and able to process information (e.g., buying a car), use central route persuasion (detailed arguments, evidence, comparative information). When consumers are not motivated or able (e.g., buying gum at checkout), use peripheral cues (celebrity endorsements, attractive packaging, social proof). Mismatching the route to the context reduces persuasion effectiveness.

7. **Celebrity endorsements work through both central and peripheral routes.** Celebrities can provide expertise and credibility (central route) as well as attractiveness and likability (peripheral route). The transfer of meaning model explains that the symbolic meanings associated with celebrities transfer to the brand. Choosing a celebrity involves Q-ratings (popularity and familiarity), brand alignment, cost, and social media influence. Celebrities can endorse explicitly ("I use this"), implicitly (shown using it), imperatively ("You should use this"), or through mere association (product placement).

8. **Repositioning requires balancing consistency and change.** The Oldsmobile case is the cautionary tale: attempting to reposition away from an "old dad's car" image failed because the change was too radical relative to existing associations. Successful repositioning is often gradual (Just Noticeable Difference) — subtle tweaks over time that keep the brand modern without losing recognition. Larger changes work in categories where freshness is a purchase driver (fashion, cosmetics). The brand's DNA must be preserved; radical changes confuse or alienate consumers.

## F. Practical Implications

- Before launching a brand campaign, audit the perceptual biases that will filter your message. If your message contradicts existing consumer beliefs, expect it to be ignored or reinterpreted. You may need to change the perceptual framework before delivering the message.
- Choose brand names for adaptability across product lines and geographies. A descriptive name that works for one product may restrict extension; an invented name (Google, Kodak) has no meaning to restrict but requires education.
- Evaluate your packaging as seriously as your product. In self-service environments, packaging is the sales force. If your packaging does not communicate your brand positioning at a glance, you are leaving money on the table.
- When selecting a celebrity endorser, calculate the Q-rating (familiarity × likability) but also assess meaning alignment. A popular celebrity with mismatched symbolic meaning will dilute your brand, not enhance it.
- Before repositioning, diagnose why the current position is failing. Flawed initial positioning, unprofitable target, market changes, or just tiredness require different repositioning strategies. Do not treat all repositioning problems the same.
- Use gradual change (JND) for established brands with strong existing associations. Use radical change (Butterfly Effect) only when the brand has weak associations or the category demands freshness. And never change the brand's DNA — preserve the core while updating the expression.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The perception psychology section is the module's hidden gem. Most marketing courses jump straight to the 4 P's without grounding them in how humans actually process information. The selective exposure bias — consumers avoid information that contradicts their beliefs — explains why competitive advertising often fails: your customers are not reading your competitor's claims, and your competitor's customers are not reading yours. This is uncomfortable for marketers who believe that better information always wins. It does not. Beliefs are sticky, and perceptions are self-reinforcing.

The Elaboration Likelihood Model is widely cited but often misapplied. The module correctly emphasizes that the same person may use the central route for some decisions (high involvement, high consequence) and the peripheral route for others (low involvement, low consequence). The mistake is assuming consumers are either "central route people" or "peripheral route people." They are both, depending on context. Good marketers match the route to the decision context.

The repositioning section is the weakest in the module, not because it is wrong but because it is underdeveloped. The Oldsmobile case is dramatic, but the module provides limited guidance on how to diagnose the appropriate degree of change. When is a brand "tired" versus "broken"? When does gradual change signal irrelevance rather than evolution? The Just Noticeable Difference framework from psychophysics (Weber-Fechner) is mentioned but not operationalized. A more detailed framework for repositioning strategy selection would strengthen this section significantly.

# Module 5 — Applied Marketing (Optional)

## A. Core Idea of the Module

This optional module applies the course's frameworks to real-world cases: Jennifer Lopez's brand empire, Awesomeness TV's pivot to millennial moms, Playboy's entry into China, Crocs' attempted revival, and several customer centricity cases (Electronic Arts, Merial, CLV startups). The cases are not meant to be comprehensive but illustrative — showing how the same frameworks (STP, CLV, brand positioning, repositioning) play out in practice. The module also addresses barriers to customer centricity and the "pay it forward" idea of teaching customers to be customer centric themselves.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions (New in This Module)

- **Q-Rating:** A measure of a celebrity's popularity and familiarity — used to evaluate endorsement potential. High familiarity + high likability = high Q-rating.
- **CLVmetrics.com:** A startup offering a web-based platform to calculate CLV and inject estimates back into CRM systems.
- **Two Six Capital:** A private equity fund that values companies based on customer lifetime value and customer equity rather than traditional top-down revenue multiples.
- **Pay It Forward Customer Centricity:** Teaching customers to be more customer centric with their own customers — positioning the company as a trusted advisor and deepening relationships.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights from Cases

1. **Jennifer Lopez (J Lo) — Start with authentic positioning, then expand.** J Lo's authenticity as a Puerto Rican from the Bronx gives her a strong, unique point of difference. She targets the growing Latino market — an attractive and underserved segment. She starts with a focused frame of reference (fragrances and fashion) closely tied to her persona, then expands into other areas (weight loss, TV) from a strong foundation. The lesson: successful brand expansion requires a clear core before extending.

2. **Awesomeness TV — Segmentation reveals new opportunities.** Originally targeting teens with short-form video, Awesomeness identified millennial moms as an attractive new segment — underserved by traditional media (soap operas on old-fashioned TV) and with distinct media consumption habits (short-form videos during busy days). The new product, Awestruck, was designed specifically for them. The lesson: segmentation is not static; new segments emerge as demographics and media habits change.

3. **Playboy in China — Weak positioning leads to uncertainty.** Playboy is relatively unknown in China, but its iconic bunny logo is recognized. The company is leveraging its legacy and logo as points of difference — but the target segment is unclear, the competitive frame of reference is uncertain, and the strategy is experimental. The lesson: brand elements (like a logo) are not sufficient for success without clear positioning.

4. **Crocs — Shape as a differentiator for revival.** Crocs, known for its unusual shape and customizable charms, initially targeted young kids and became a fashion statement. Now trying to revive, the brand emphasizes its distinctive shape as a key differentiator — following Absolut Vodka's successful shape-based branding. The slogan "Find Your Fun" encourages rediscovery. The lesson: unique product features (especially visual ones) can anchor a repositioning strategy.

5. **Electronic Arts — Balancing product and customer focus.** EA, a traditionally product-centric gaming company, shifted toward customer centricity by analyzing CLV to identify their most valuable players. This data informs acquisition strategies and game features. EA has not abandoned product-centric practices but integrates customer-centric insights to enhance decision-making. The lesson: most successful pivots are hybrid, not pure.

6. **Merial — CLV in B2B contexts.** Merial, a veterinary pharmaceutical company, transformed sales force incentives: rewarding salespeople based on future-looking CLV rather than immediate sales volume. The strategy led to over 10% revenue increase in a competitive market without additional costs. The lesson: customer centricity and CLV are not limited to B2C; B2B applications may be even more powerful because relationships are fewer and higher-value.

7. **Barriers to Customer Centricity — Organizational, cultural, and metric challenges.** The module lists: organizing around valuable customers (not products), finding convergent-thinking talent, developing customer-centric brands globally, managing channel partner incentives, changing performance metrics, and shifting corporate culture. The lesson: the barriers are real, difficult, and take time to overcome — but they are not insurmountable.

8. **Pay It Forward — Teaching customers to be customer centric.** Companies like Google can teach their customers (advertisers) to be more customer centric with their own customers — using more analytics tools, understanding CLV, etc. This positions the company as a trusted advisor and deepens relationships. The lesson: customer centricity can spread through the ecosystem, creating value for everyone.

## F. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The optional module is appropriately titled: it is optional, and its absence would not weaken the core course. The cases are illustrative rather than deep, and the analysis is sometimes superficial. The Playboy China case is particularly thin — the module describes the uncertainty but does not resolve it or provide a framework for deciding the right strategy. This may be intentional (real-world marketing involves genuine uncertainty), but it feels incomplete.

The Electronic Arts and Merial cases are the most valuable in this module because they show customer centricity working in product-centric industries (gaming and pharma). The EA case demonstrates that you do not need to abandon product focus to benefit from customer insights. The Merial case demonstrates that CLV works in B2B — and may work even better there because relationships are fewer and higher-value, making the data more tractable and the ROI of relationship investment clearer.

The "pay it forward" idea is provocative but underdeveloped. Teaching customers to be customer centric is a plausible strategy for deepening relationships, but the module provides no evidence of its effectiveness beyond assertion. Google's analytics tools are mentioned, but is Google teaching customer centricity or just selling more ads? The distinction matters. A more rigorous treatment of this concept would strengthen the module significantly.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course's most lasting contribution is a strategic framework for marketing that balances rigor with realism. The STP framework is simple to state but difficult to execute: the discipline of saying no to attractive but off-strategy opportunities is what separates great marketing from average marketing. The customer centricity module is the course's distinctive contribution — most introductory marketing courses treat segmentation and targeting as purely demographic exercises, not as a radical reorientation of the entire business around customer value. The pricing module provides the quantitative toolkit that many managers lack, and the psychological pricing effects are directly actionable. The brand communications module grounds branding in perception psychology, preventing the field from becoming purely aesthetic or intuitive.

The course's weakness is its breadth. Five modules covering positioning, customer centricity, communications, pricing, distribution, branding, and repositioning — with an optional applied module — means no single topic gets the depth it deserves. Pricing alone could be a full course (and is, at Wharton). Distribution receives the shortest treatment despite capturing 30-40% of price. The trade-off between breadth and depth is real, and the course chooses breadth — which is appropriate for an introduction, but leaves the serious student wanting more.
