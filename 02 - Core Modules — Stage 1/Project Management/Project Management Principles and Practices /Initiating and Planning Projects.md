# Module 1 — What is a Project?

## A. Core Idea of the Module

A project is a unique and temporary endeavor with a specific beginning and end, aimed at creating a product or service. This is different from routine operations (like running a factory or processing payroll). Projects are constrained by factors like time, budget, and requirements. The module also covers different organizational structures: functional (departments own resources), matrix (shared resources between departments and projects), and projectized (full-time project teams). The structure affects how much authority the project manager has.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Project:** A unique and temporary endeavor with a specific beginning and end, aimed at creating a product or service. Examples: planning an event, moving an office, implementing a new system.

- **Routine Operations:** Ongoing, repetitive work that sustains the organization. Examples: manufacturing, customer service, payroll.

- **Project Constraints:** Factors that limit how a project is executed. Common constraints: time (deadline), cost (budget), scope (requirements), quality (standards).

- **Functional Organization:** A structure where employees report to functional managers (e.g., Marketing, IT, Finance). Project managers have little or no authority. Resources belong to departments.

- **Matrix Organization:** A structure where employees report to both a functional manager and a project manager. Three types:
  - **Weak Matrix:** Functional manager has more power. Project manager is more of a coordinator.
  - **Balanced Matrix:** Functional manager and project manager share power equally.
  - **Strong Matrix:** Project manager has more power. Resources are dedicated to projects.

- **Projectized Organization:** A structure where employees report directly to a project manager. Full-time project teams. Project managers have high authority.

- **PMBOK Guide:** Project Management Body of Knowledge — guidelines from the Project Management Institute (PMI).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Projects are temporary, operations are ongoing.** This is the core distinction. A project has a start date and an end date. When the project is complete, the team disbands or moves to other projects. Operations continue indefinitely.

2. **Project constraints create trade-offs.** If scope increases, time or cost must increase (or quality must decrease). If time is cut, cost may increase or scope must be reduced. Understanding these trade-offs is essential for project planning.

3. **Organizational structure determines project manager authority.** In functional organizations, project managers have little authority — they must negotiate for resources. In projectized organizations, project managers have high authority — they control resources directly. The structure is often chosen based on how important projects are to the organization.

4. **The choice of structure impacts team dynamics.** In matrix organizations, employees have two bosses — functional and project. This can create confusion and conflict. Clear reporting lines and communication protocols are essential.

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before starting a project, clearly define its start and end. Without clear boundaries, it is not a project — it is ongoing work.

- Identify the key constraints early. What is fixed (must deliver by date, must stay within budget, must include specific features)? The fixed constraints drive all other decisions.

- Understand your authority as a project manager. In a functional organization, you will need to negotiate and influence, not command. In a projectized organization, you have more direct control.

- If you are in a matrix structure, clarify reporting lines. Who makes decisions about resource allocation? Who evaluates performance? Ambiguity causes conflict.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The distinction between projects and operations is simple but important. Many organizations struggle because they treat ongoing work as projects (too much planning, temporary mindset) or treat projects as operations (not enough planning, no clear end). Knowing which is which helps you choose the right management approach.

The triple constraint (time, cost, scope) is the foundation of project trade-offs. But the course adds quality as a fourth constraint. In reality, quality is often the variable that suffers when time or cost is cut — but it should not be. Good project planning makes trade-offs explicit so quality is not accidentally sacrificed.

The matrix structure is common in practice but hard to manage. Employees with two bosses get conflicting priorities. The course notes this but does not go deep into solutions. In practice, strong communication and clear decision rights are essential.

# Module 2 — Get to Know Your Stakeholder

## A. Core Idea of the Module

A stakeholder is anyone who may be positively or negatively impacted by the project. This includes team members, customers, sponsors, vendors, and even people who are not directly involved but are affected. The module teaches how to identify stakeholders (using key questions), categorize them using a power/interest grid, and manage them with different engagement strategies. A stakeholder register is used to capture key information and track relationships. The goal is to ensure stakeholders are engaged appropriately — not too much, not too little.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Stakeholder:** Anyone who may be positively or negatively impacted by the project. Includes team members, customers, sponsors, vendors, end users, regulators, and community members.

- **Key Questions to Identify Stakeholders:**
  - Who requested the project?
  - Who will use the product?
  - Who influences decisions?
  - Who provides resources?
  - Who might be affected (positively or negatively)?

- **Power/Interest Grid:** A 2x2 matrix used to categorize stakeholders based on their level of power (authority to influence the project) and interest (how much they care about the outcome).

- **Power/Interest Categories:**
  - **High Power, High Interest:** Key players. Manage closely. Engage frequently.
  - **High Power, Low Interest:** Keep satisfied. Do not overwhelm with details, but keep informed.
  - **Low Power, High Interest:** Keep informed. Provide regular updates.
  - **Low Power, Low Interest:** Monitor. Minimal effort.

- **Stakeholder Register:** A document that captures key information about each stakeholder: name, role, contact information, influence level, interest level, communication preferences, and engagement strategy.

- **Engagement Strategies:** Different approaches for different stakeholders based on their power and interest.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Stakeholders include anyone impacted — not just the project team.** This is often overlooked. A new software system might impact customers, suppliers, IT support, and even other departments who use the data. All are stakeholders.

2. **Not all stakeholders are equal.** The power/interest grid helps prioritize. High-power, high-interest stakeholders get the most attention. Low-power, low-interest stakeholders get minimal effort. This is not about being unfair — it is about using limited time effectively.

3. **Engagement strategies depend on power and interest.** High power, high interest stakeholders need frequent, detailed communication. High power, low interest stakeholders need to be kept satisfied but not overwhelmed. Low power, high interest stakeholders need to be kept informed. Low power, low interest stakeholders just need monitoring.

4. **The stakeholder register is a living document.** Stakeholders can change over time — someone's interest may increase, power may shift, new stakeholders may emerge. The register should be updated regularly.

5. **Regular assessment of engagement is necessary.** What worked last month may not work this month. Communication preferences change. Checking in with stakeholders ensures ongoing support.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Stakeholder Identification Questions | Who requested, who uses, who influences, who provides resources, who is affected | At project initiation |
| Power/Interest Grid | 2x2 matrix (Power: High/Low, Interest: High/Low) | To categorize and prioritize stakeholders |
| Stakeholder Register | Document with name, role, influence, interest, preferences, strategy | Throughout the project — updated regularly |
| Engagement Strategies | Manage closely, keep satisfied, keep informed, monitor | After categorizing stakeholders |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Do not skip stakeholder identification. Missing a key stakeholder early can cause major problems later (e.g., a regulator who must approve the project).

- Use the power/interest grid to prioritize. Spend 80% of your stakeholder management time on high-power, high-interest stakeholders.

- For high-power, low-interest stakeholders (like a busy executive), provide concise summaries. Do not send long reports.

- For low-power, high-interest stakeholders (like enthusiastic end users), keep them informed but do not let them drive decisions.

- Update the stakeholder register regularly. People change roles, interests shift, new stakeholders emerge.

---

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Define a stakeholder (anyone impacted by the project)
- [x] Use key questions to identify stakeholders
- [x] Categorize stakeholders using a power/interest grid
- [x] Determine appropriate engagement strategies for each category
- [x] Create and maintain a stakeholder register

---

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The power/interest grid is simple but very useful. It prevents the common mistake of treating all stakeholders the same. Some people need detailed updates; others need just a summary. Some need to be consulted on decisions; others just need to be informed. Matching effort to impact is smart management, not favoritism.

The stakeholder register is often neglected in practice. Teams identify stakeholders at the start of the project and then forget about them. But stakeholders change — a new executive joins, a customer's priorities shift, a regulator updates requirements. The register should be reviewed monthly.

The key question "Who might be negatively impacted?" is often missed. Projects almost always create losers as well as winners. People whose jobs are eliminated, departments that lose budget, customers who lose a product they liked. These stakeholders may resist the project. Identifying them early allows you to manage their concerns.

# Module 3 — Scope Matters 

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Planning is not optional — it is the foundation of project success. This module covers two essential planning documents: the Project Charter and the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS). The Project Charter is the first document created after project approval. It formally announces the project, grants authority to the project manager, and clarifies objectives, success criteria, deadlines, budget, and risks. The WBS breaks down project deliverables into manageable components, helping with scheduling and resource allocation. It should include all necessary work and nothing outside the defined scope.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Project Charter:** The first document created after project approval. Formally announces the project and grants authority to the project manager. Includes: project objectives, success criteria, deadlines, budget, identified risks, key stakeholders.

- **Purpose of Project Charter:**
  - Clarifies project objectives and success criteria
  - Provides authority to the project manager
  - Communicates project scope to stakeholders
  - Serves as a "Rules of Engagement"

- **Project Planning:** The process of understanding and documenting project scope, schedule, and cost management. Essential for project success.

- **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS):** A foundational tool that breaks down project deliverables into smaller, manageable components. Depicts all necessary work to meet project objectives.

- **WBS Principles:**
  - Includes all work required — nothing outside the defined scope
  - Breaks down deliverables, not activities
  - Each level is more detailed than the level above
  - The lowest level (work package) can be estimated, scheduled, and assigned

- **Scope Creep:** Uncontrolled changes or continuous growth in project scope without corresponding adjustments to time, cost, or resources. A well-defined WBS helps prevent scope creep.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The Project Charter is the project's constitution.** It gives the project manager authority and sets the rules. Without a charter, the project manager may not have the power to make decisions or allocate resources.

2. **The Project Charter should be created after approval, not before.** The approval to start the project comes first (from a sponsor or steering committee). Then the project manager creates the charter to document what was approved.

3. **The WBS is about deliverables, not activities.** Common mistake: putting activities (like "write report") instead of deliverables (like "final report"). The WBS answers "what" will be delivered, not "how" it will be done. Activities come later in the schedule.

4. **The WBS should include all work and only that work.** If a required deliverable is missing, the project will miss scope. If an unnecessary deliverable is included, the project will waste time and money. The WBS is the scope baseline.

5. **A well-structured WBS supports scheduling, budgeting, and resource allocation.** Each work package (lowest level) can be estimated for time and cost, assigned to a team member, and tracked.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Project Charter | Document announcing project, granting authority | At project initiation, after approval |
| WBS (Work Breakdown Structure) | Hierarchical breakdown of deliverables | During planning, before scheduling |
| Deliverable-Based WBS | Organized by deliverables (not phases or activities) | Most common and recommended approach |
| Work Package | Lowest level of WBS — can be estimated and assigned | For scheduling and resource allocation |
| Scope Baseline | WBS + WBS Dictionary | To manage scope and prevent scope creep |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Always create a Project Charter. Even for small projects, a one-page charter clarifies expectations and gives you authority.

- Get the charter approved by the sponsor before starting detailed planning. The charter sets the boundaries.

- Build the WBS before the schedule. You cannot schedule work until you know what needs to be done.

- Use deliverable-based WBS (nouns) not activity-based WBS (verbs). Example: "Training materials" not "create training materials."

- Include the project team in WBS creation. The people doing the work know what needs to be done.

- Use the WBS to prevent scope creep. When someone asks for new work, check if it is in the WBS. If not, it is out of scope and requires a change request.

- [x] Explain when the Project Charter is created (after approval)
- [x] Define Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Project Charter is often skipped in informal projects, but that is a mistake. Even a one-page document that states the objective, deadline, budget, and project manager authority prevents huge problems later. Without a charter, stakeholders can argue about what was agreed. The charter is the written record.

The WBS is one of the most valuable tools in project management. It forces you to think through all deliverables before you start scheduling. The discipline of breaking down deliverables until they are small enough to estimate (work packages) prevents the "unknown unknowns" that kill projects.

The distinction between deliverables (WBS) and activities (schedule) is subtle but important. Many beginners put activities in the WBS. The rule: if it is a verb, it probably belongs in the schedule, not the WBS. WBS uses nouns (products, deliverables). Schedules use verbs (actions, tasks).

# Module 4 — Authority vs Influence 

## A. Core Idea of the Module

A project manager needs two things: authority (the right to make decisions, allocate resources, and approve actions) and influence (the ability to persuade others without formal authority). In many organizations, especially functional and weak matrix structures, project managers have limited authority. They must rely on influence. The module also covers conflict management — conflict is natural in projects, and the project manager must know when to use different approaches: confronting (collaboration), compromising, smoothing (accommodating), forcing (competing), and avoiding (withdrawal). The right approach depends on the situation.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Authority:** The right to make decisions, allocate resources, and approve actions within a project. Comes from the Project Charter and organizational structure.

- **Influence:** The ability to persuade others to support project goals and resolve conflicts. Built through relationships, expertise, communication, and trust.

- **Influence without Authority:** The skill of getting things done when you do not have formal power. Essential in functional and weak matrix organizations.

- **Conflict:** A natural occurrence in projects, arising from differing needs, priorities, or perspectives among stakeholders.

- **Confronting (Collaboration):** Face the problem directly. Work together to find a solution that satisfies all parties. Best for important issues where both sides have valid concerns.

- **Compromising:** Each side gives up something to reach a middle ground. Useful when time is limited or when parties have equal power.

- **Smoothing (Accommodating):** One side gives in to preserve harmony. Useful when the issue is more important to the other party or when maintaining the relationship is critical.

- **Forcing (Competing):** Impose a solution. Use when safety is at risk, when a quick decision is needed, or when the issue is non-negotiable.

- **Avoiding (Withdrawal):** Postpone or ignore the conflict. Useful for trivial issues or when emotions are high and cooling off is needed. Not appropriate for serious issues.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Authority is granted; influence is earned.** The Project Charter gives you authority. But influence comes from your reputation, expertise, relationships, and communication skills. In many organizations, influence is more important than authority.

2. **Conflict is not bad — it is natural.** The goal is not to eliminate conflict but to manage it productively. Different conflicts require different approaches.

3. **Forcing is appropriate when safety is at risk.** The case study example: a stakeholder refuses to wear safety glasses in a test area. Compromise is not acceptable — safety is non-negotiable. The project manager must enforce the rule (forcing/competing).

4. **Avoiding is only for trivial issues.** Postponing a conflict can be useful when emotions are high and people need to cool down. But avoiding serious issues makes them worse.

5. **Confronting (collaboration) is usually best for important issues.** Facing the problem directly and working together to find a solution builds trust and leads to better outcomes. But it takes time and requires good faith from both sides.

6. **The appropriate approach depends on the situation.** There is no single "best" conflict resolution method. The project manager must assess the importance of the issue, the power balance, the time available, and the relationships involved.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Authority | Right to decide, allocate, approve | Comes from charter and structure |
| Influence | Ability to persuade without authority | Built through relationships and expertise |
| Confronting (Collaboration) | Face problem together, find win-win | Important issues, time available |
| Compromising | Each side gives something | Time limited, equal power |
| Smoothing (Accommodating) | One side gives in | Issue more important to other party, relationship critical |
| Forcing (Competing) | Impose a solution | Safety risk, quick decision needed, non-negotiable |
| Avoiding (Withdrawal) | Postpone or ignore | Trivial issues, emotions high (temporary) |

---

> *No formulas in this module — frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Build influence early. Before you need it. Establish relationships, demonstrate expertise, communicate clearly, deliver on promises.

- Know your authority. Read the Project Charter. What decisions can you make? What requires approval? Stay within your bounds.

- When conflict arises, assess the situation before choosing an approach. Is safety at risk? Is the issue important? How much time is available? What is the power balance?

- Use forcing (competing) for safety issues. Do not compromise on safety. Do not avoid safety issues. Enforce the rules.

- Use collaboration for important strategic decisions. It takes time but builds buy-in and leads to better solutions.

- Use compromising when you need a quick decision and both sides have valid points. Everyone gives something, everyone gets something.

- Use smoothing (accommodating) when the issue matters more to the other party or when preserving the relationship is critical.

- Use avoiding only temporarily — to let emotions cool down. Then address the issue.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The distinction between authority and influence is crucial for project managers. Many new project managers assume they will have authority over team members. In functional and matrix organizations, they often do not. The team members report to functional managers. The project manager must influence, not command. This is hard but learnable — through relationships, expertise, and clear communication.

The safety case study (safety glasses in test area) is a clear example of when forcing is correct. There is no compromise on safety. No accommodation. No avoidance. The project manager must enforce the rule, even if it creates discomfort. This is not about being aggressive — it is about protecting people.

The five conflict resolution approaches are from the Thomas-Kilmann model, a standard in project management. The key insight is that there is no single "best" approach. Forcing is bad for team morale but necessary for safety. Avoiding is bad for important issues but useful for trivial ones. The skill is matching the approach to the situation.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course provides the essential tools for initiating and planning projects. The key insights: a project is temporary and unique, not ongoing operations. Stakeholders are anyone impacted, and they must be managed differently based on their power and interest. The Project Charter gives you authority and sets the rules. The Work Breakdown Structure breaks deliverables into manageable pieces and prevents scope creep. As a project manager, you need both authority (granted) and influence (earned). Conflict is natural, and different situations require different approaches — forcing is correct for safety, collaboration is best for important issues, compromise works when time is short.

The course's strength is its practical, tool-based approach. The power/interest grid, stakeholder register, Project Charter, and WBS are immediately useful. The case studies (Project A vs. Project B, stakeholder inclusion, safety glasses) make the concepts concrete.

The weakness is that the course is short (4 modules) and some topics (risk management, communication planning, scheduling) are not covered. But as an introduction to project planning, this course covers the essentials clearly.
