## 🧭 Course Overview

This course teaches how to manage project communication, risks, and changes. Project managers spend most of their time communicating — so a clear communication plan is essential. Risk management is about preparing for potential threats and opportunities before they happen. And change management is about handling the inevitable requests for new features or adjustments without losing control of the project. The course covers communication planning (who gets what information, when, and how), risk identification and response (avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept), change control processes, and lessons learned. The core message is that planning for communication, risks, and changes is not optional — it is how you prevent problems before they happen.

# Module 1 — Project Communications

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Project managers spend most of their time communicating. A communication management plan is not optional — it is essential for keeping stakeholders informed, preventing missed opportunities, and ensuring everyone understands their roles and expectations. The plan should answer: who to communicate with, what information they need, when they need it, and what medium to use. Different stakeholders (senior management, peers, subordinates, different departments) need different information. Status reporting measures project performance against baselines and highlights key issues, risks, and accomplishments.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Communication Management Plan:** A document that outlines who to communicate with, what information they need, when they need it, and the best medium for communication.

- **Purpose of Communication Plan:**
  - Prevents missed opportunities
  - Ensures stakeholders understand roles and expectations
  - Keeps everyone informed of progress, risks, and accomplishments

- **Stakeholder Communication Needs:** Different stakeholders need different information:
  - **Senior Management:** High-level status, key risks, major decisions needed
  - **Peers:** Coordination information, resource needs
  - **Subordinates:** Task assignments, expectations, feedback
  - **Different Departments:** Tailored information relevant to their function

- **Communication Channels:** The medium used to deliver information (email, meeting, report, dashboard, presentation).

- **Status Reporting:** Measuring project performance against baselines (scope, schedule, cost). Includes progress, key issues, risks, and accomplishments.

- **Milestone Communication:** Announcing when major project milestones are completed. Should include not just the announcement but also what the milestone means for each stakeholder.

- **Tailored Communication:** Adapting message content, detail level, and frequency to each stakeholder's needs.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Project managers spend most of their time communicating.** This is not a distraction from "real work" — it is the real work. A project manager who is not communicating is not managing.

2. **A communication plan prevents missed opportunities.** When stakeholders do not know what is happening, they cannot help. When they are surprised by bad news, trust is lost. Good communication keeps everyone aligned.

3. **Different stakeholders need different information.** The case study shows that marketing and accounting care about different aspects of a milestone. Marketing needs to start campaigns; accounting needs to update budgets. One announcement does not fit all.

4. **Milestone announcements should educate, not just notify.** Simply saying "prototype approved" is not enough. Explain what this means for each department. "Prototype approved means marketing can begin campaign planning. Accounting should update capitalized development costs."

5. **Status reporting measures against baselines.** Without a baseline (approved scope, schedule, budget), you cannot measure status. "We are 50% done" means nothing unless you know what 100% looks like.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Communication Management Plan | Document with who, what, when, how | At project initiation |
| Stakeholder Communication Mapping | Matching information to stakeholder needs | When creating communication plan |
| Status Report | Progress against baselines, issues, risks | Regularly (weekly, monthly) |
| Milestone Announcement | Notification plus implications | When major milestones are completed |
| Tailored Communication | Adapting message to audience | For every communication |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Create a communication plan at the start of every project. Even a simple one-page plan is better than nothing.

- Ask each stakeholder: "What information do you need, how often, and in what format?" Do not guess.

- For milestone announcements, do not just say "X is complete." Explain what it means for each department.

- Use status reports to compare actual progress to baselines. If you are behind, say so early. Do not wait.

- Different channels for different purposes. Use meetings for discussion and decisions. Use email for documentation. Use dashboards for ongoing status.

- [x] Create a communication management plan
- [x] Identify what information different stakeholders need
- [x] Tailor communication to different departments

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The insight that milestone announcements should educate, not just notify, is important. Many project managers send a simple "Milestone X achieved" email and move on. But stakeholders in different departments need to know what this means for them. Marketing needs to start campaigns. Accounting needs to update capitalization. Operations needs to prepare for production. A good announcement includes these implications.

The communication plan is often the most neglected project document. Teams assume everyone will figure it out. They do not. Without a plan, some stakeholders get too much information (wasting time), others get too little (missing critical updates), and everyone is confused. A simple RACI-based communication plan solves this.

Status reporting is only useful if it is honest. Many project managers hide bad news because they are afraid. This is the worst thing you can do. Bad news does not age well. Report issues early, with a plan to address them.

# Module 2 — Managing Project Risks

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Risk management is about preparing for potential threats and opportunities before they happen. Risks should be identified early (starting in the project charter) and continuously throughout the project. A risk register captures each risk, its likelihood, impact, and response plan. Responses include: avoid (eliminate the threat), transfer (shift the risk to someone else, like insurance), mitigate (reduce likelihood or impact), and accept (acknowledge and budget for it). Risks are prioritized by likelihood and impact — high-likelihood, high-impact risks get the most attention.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Project Risk:** An uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives.

- **Risk Management:** The process of identifying, analyzing, responding to, and monitoring risks throughout the project.

- **Risk Register:** A document that captures identified risks, their likelihood, impact, response plan, and owner.

- **Risk Identification Methods:**
  - Brainstorming sessions with the team
  - Reviewing similar past projects
  - Analyzing the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
  - Assumptions analysis (what if an assumption is wrong?)

- **Risk Prioritization:** Ranking risks by likelihood (probability) and impact (effect on project objectives). High-high risks get the most attention.

- **Risk Responses:**
  - **Avoid:** Eliminate the threat entirely (e.g., change the approach)
  - **Transfer:** Shift the risk to someone else (e.g., insurance, fixed-price contract)
  - **Mitigate:** Reduce the likelihood or impact (e.g., more testing, backup plan)
  - **Accept:** Acknowledge the risk and budget for it (contingency reserve)

- **Opportunity Responses (for positive risks):**
  - **Exploit:** Make sure the opportunity happens
  - **Share:** Partner with someone to capture the opportunity
  - **Enhance:** Increase the likelihood or impact
  - **Accept:** Take advantage if it happens

- **Continuous Risk Management:** Risks are not identified once at the start. New risks emerge. Existing risks change. Review the risk register regularly.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Risk management starts in the project charter.** High-level risks and assumptions should be documented early. As the project progresses, expand the risk register with more detail.

2. **The team is the best source of risks.** Team members know the work. Ask them: "What keeps you up at night?" Their answers are your risk register.

3. **Prioritize by likelihood and impact.** A high-likelihood, high-impact risk gets immediate attention. A low-likelihood, low-impact risk goes on the watch list. Do not spend time on risks that are unlikely and unimportant.

4. **Risk responses should be developed collaboratively.** The team should help design responses. They know what is feasible.

5. **Risk management is continuous.** Do not identify risks once and forget them. Review the risk register at every team meeting. Add new risks. Re-evaluate existing risks. Close risks that are no longer relevant.

6. **Not all risks are bad.** Opportunities are positive risks. If there is a chance to finish early or under budget, plan to exploit it.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Risk Register | Document with risks, likelihood, impact, response, owner | Throughout the project |
| Likelihood/Impact Grid | 2x2 matrix (likelihood: high/low, impact: high/low) | To prioritize risks |
| Avoid | Eliminate the threat | When you can change the plan to remove the risk |
| Transfer | Shift risk to someone else | Insurance, fixed-price contracts |
| Mitigate | Reduce likelihood or impact | Most common response |
| Accept | Acknowledge and budget | Low-impact risks or unavoidable risks |
| Brainstorming | Team generates risk ideas | At project initiation and regularly |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Start risk identification early. Include risks in the project charter. Do not wait for detailed planning.

- Ask the team: "What could go wrong? What assumptions might be wrong? What keeps you up at night?"

- Use a likelihood/impact grid to prioritize. Do not spend time on risks that are unlikely and low-impact.

- For high-priority risks, assign an owner. One person is responsible for monitoring the risk and executing the response if needed.

- Review the risk register at every team meeting. Add new risks. Update old ones. Close completed ones.

- Do not forget opportunities. If there is a chance to finish early, plan how to make it happen.

---

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Define project risk and risk management
- [x] Identify risks using brainstorming, past projects, and WBS analysis
- [x] Create a risk register
- [x] Prioritize risks by likelihood and impact
- [x] Develop risk responses (avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept)
- [x] Explain why risk management is continuous, not one-time

---

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The likelihood/impact grid is simple but very useful. It prevents the common mistake of treating all risks the same. A risk that is 90% likely to cost $1,000 is more important than a risk that is 1% likely to cost $100,000. The grid makes this visible.

The case study shows the value of team risk identification. When team members share their concerns openly, you get a much better risk register than one person guessing. Creating a safe environment for this discussion is key.

The distinction between avoid, transfer, mitigate, and accept is important. Avoid is best but not always possible. Transfer shifts the risk (insurance) but costs money. Mitigate is the most common — reduce likelihood or impact. Accept is for risks that are not worth addressing. Each has its place.

# Module 3 — Change Happens

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Change is a natural part of project management. The problem is not change itself — it is uncontrolled change. Scope creep happens when project objectives are unclear or when there is no formal process for requesting changes. A change control process provides a clear, simple way to handle change requests. It includes a standardized form, a review board (Change Control Board) with defined approval thresholds, and a decision process. Capturing lessons learned throughout the project helps prevent repeating mistakes and improves future project performance.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Scope Creep:** Uncontrolled changes or continuous growth in project scope without corresponding adjustments to time, cost, or resources.

- **Causes of Scope Creep:**
  - Unclear project objectives
  - No formal process for requesting changes
  - Stakeholders adding features without approval
  - Project manager afraid to say no

- **Change Control Process:** A formal process for reviewing, approving, and tracking changes to project scope, schedule, or budget.

- **Change Control Board (CCB):** A group of stakeholders authorized to approve or reject change requests. May have different approval thresholds for different types of changes.

- **Change Request Form:** A standardized document for requesting changes. Includes: description of change, reason, impact on scope/schedule/cost, and recommendation.

- **Approval Thresholds:** Different levels of approval for different types of changes (e.g., project manager approves small changes, CCB approves large changes, executive approves major changes).

- **Lessons Learned:** Knowledge gained from a project that can be applied to future projects. Should be captured throughout the project, not just at the end.

- **Lessons Learned Register:** A document that captures what went well, what went wrong, and recommendations for future projects.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Change is inevitable — plan for it.** Do not assume the project will go exactly as planned. Build a change control process before you need it.

2. **Scope creep is the enemy of project success.** When features are added without adjusting time or budget, quality suffers. The project fails slowly, one small change at a time.

3. **A change control board provides a fair decision process.** When an executive asks for a new feature, it is hard to say no. A CCB takes the pressure off the project manager. "I cannot approve this alone — it must go to the CCB."

4. **Not all changes need the same approval level.** Small changes that do not affect the critical path or budget can be approved by the project manager. Large changes need the CCB. This prevents bureaucracy for small changes.

5. **Lessons learned should be captured continuously.** Waiting until the end of the project means you forget. Capture lessons in real time. Share them regularly.

6. **Lessons learned must be applied to be useful.** A lessons learned document that sits on a shelf is worthless. Review lessons before starting new projects. Build them into processes.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Change Control Process | Formal process for reviewing changes | Throughout the project |
| Change Control Board (CCB) | Group authorized to approve changes | When changes are proposed |
| Change Request Form | Standardized document for change requests | For every proposed change |
| Approval Thresholds | Different levels for different changes | To balance control and efficiency |
| Lessons Learned Register | Document capturing what worked and what did not | Throughout the project |
| Scope Creep Prevention | Clear objectives + change control + courage to say no | From project initiation onward |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Create a change control process before you need it. Define the form, the CCB members, and approval thresholds.

- Use a change request form for every proposed change. Require the requester to state the impact on scope, schedule, and budget.

- Establish approval thresholds: project manager approves changes under $X or under Y days; CCB approves larger changes; executive approves major changes.

- When an executive asks for a new feature, do not say no. Say "I will submit this to the CCB for approval." This is not avoidance — it is process.

- Capture lessons learned weekly. Ask the team: "What went well this week? What could be better? What should we do differently next week?"

- Share lessons learned with other project managers. Do not make the same mistakes twice.

---

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Define scope creep and explain its causes
- [x] Implement a change control process
- [x] Create a change request form
- [x] Establish a Change Control Board (CCB)
- [x] Define approval thresholds for different types of changes
- [x] Capture and use lessons learned

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The change control board is one of the most practical tools in project management. It takes the pressure off the project manager. When an executive asks for a new feature, the project manager does not have to say no. The CCB says no — or says yes with additional time and budget. This is not avoidance; it is accountability.

The case study about an executive demanding new features is realistic. Many project managers would just add the features and try to absorb the work. This is a mistake. It leads to burnout, quality problems, and missed deadlines. The correct response is to follow the change control process.

Lessons learned are often collected at the end of the project, when everyone has moved on. No one reads them. The course is right to emphasize capturing lessons continuously and applying them immediately. A weekly "what went well / what could be better" is much more useful than a post-mortem.

# Module 4 — Wrap Up (from People Analytics)

> **Note:** This module includes content from a separate Wharton course (People Analytics) on collaboration networks, measuring outcomes, and talent analytics challenges.

## A. Core Idea of the Module

People analytics uses data to understand and improve how people work together. Collaboration networks can be mapped using surveys or digital data (email, calls). Key metrics include network size, strength, range, centrality, and density. Evaluating collaboration helps identify overloaded employees, underutilized employees, and organizational silos. The module also covers challenges in talent analytics: context (performance depends on situation, not just individual), interdependence (performance depends on team), self-fulfilling prophecies (expectations shape performance), and reverse causality (correlation is not causation). Prescriptions include broadening samples, creating exogenous variation, rewarding in proportion to signals, and emphasizing development.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Organizational Network Analysis (ONA):** A method for mapping relationships and interactions among employees to understand collaboration patterns.

- **Network Map:** A visualization showing who collaborates with whom. Shows clusters (groups that work together) and bridges (connections between clusters).

- **Network Matrix:** A table capturing the frequency or intensity of interactions between every pair of individuals. Often asymmetric (A seeking information from B may differ from B seeking information from A).

- **Collaboration Metrics:**
  - **Network Size:** Number of connections an individual has (inbound and outbound)
  - **Centrality:** How central an individual is in the network
  - **Density:** How connected the network is overall
  - **Range:** Diversity of connections across different groups

- **Collaborative Overload:** When employees are overwhelmed by excessive collaboration demands, reducing productivity.

- **Measuring Outcomes:** Outcome measures must be reliable (consistent), valid (measure what they intend), comparable (across individuals), comprehensive (cover everyone), and cost-effective.

- **Fundamental Attribution Error:** The tendency to attribute performance to individual traits rather than situational factors.

- **Interdependence:** Individual performance is influenced by team and organizational context.

- **Self-Fulfilling Prophecy:** People perform in ways consistent with others' expectations. High expectations boost performance; low expectations hinder it.

- **Matthew Effect:** Early advantages accumulate over time, leading to increasing disparities.

- **Reverse Causality:** Two correlated factors are mistaken for cause-effect in the wrong direction (e.g., success causes charisma, not charisma causes success).

- **Algorithm Aversion:** People prefer human judgment over algorithms, especially after seeing algorithms make errors.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Collaboration networks reveal hidden patterns.** Who talks to whom? Who is overloaded? Who is isolated? Network analysis makes these patterns visible.

2. **More collaboration is not always better.** Collaborative overload is real. Some employees spend too much time helping others and not enough on their own work.

3. **Performance measurement is harder than it looks.** Many common measures fail on reliability, validity, or comparability. Bad measures lead to bad decisions.

4. **Context matters.** The fundamental attribution error leads managers to blame individuals for poor performance caused by bad situations. Always ask: "What else could explain this performance?"

5. **Interdependence means individual performance is often team performance.** Moving a star performer to a new team often reduces their performance because they lose their support system.

6. **Expectations shape performance.** If you expect someone to fail, you treat them differently, and they are more likely to fail. Self-fulfilling prophecies are real.

7. **Correlation is not causation.** Just because two things are related does not mean one causes the other. Be careful with reverse causality.

8. **Algorithm aversion is common.** People trust algorithms less than humans, even when algorithms are more accurate. Allowing users some control over algorithms increases acceptance.

---

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Map collaboration networks before intervening. You cannot fix what you cannot see.

- Look for overloaded employees (many inbound requests) and underutilized employees (few inbound requests). Redistribute collaboration demands.

- When evaluating performance, always ask about context. Could the situation explain the performance? Is the comparison fair?

- Be aware of self-fulfilling prophecies. Set high expectations. Give people the resources to meet them.

- Use longitudinal data to establish causation. Do not rely on one-time correlations.

- When implementing algorithms, give users some control. People accept algorithms more when they can adjust them.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The organizational network analysis content is fascinating. The idea that you can map who talks to whom — and see overloaded employees, isolated employees, and silos — is powerful. The case of the star investment analyst whose performance dropped after moving firms shows that individual performance is often team performance. The star was not a star alone; the team made them a star.

The fundamental attribution error is one of the most persistent biases in management. We blame individuals for failure and credit individuals for success, ignoring the situation. Good people analytics forces you to ask: "What else could explain this?"

The Matthew Effect (the rich get richer) explains why early advantages compound. This has implications for talent management: give early opportunities to many people, not just the usual stars. You might discover hidden talent.

Algorithm aversion is an important insight for anyone implementing analytics tools. People trust algorithms less than humans, even when algorithms are more accurate. Allowing users some control (even just the ability to adjust the algorithm) increases acceptance.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course provides essential tools for managing project communication, risks, and changes. The key insights: communicate proactively with a plan, not reactively. Identify risks early and continuously. Respond to risks with avoid, transfer, mitigate, or accept. Control changes through a formal process with a Change Control Board. Capture lessons learned throughout the project, not just at the end.

The people analytics content (from Wharton) adds a valuable lens on collaboration and talent management. Collaboration networks reveal hidden patterns — who is overloaded, who is isolated, where silos exist. Measuring outcomes is harder than it looks; be careful with reliability, validity, and comparability. And when evaluating people, always consider context, interdependence, self-fulfilling prophecies, and reverse causality.

The course's strength is its practical, tool-based approach. The communication plan, risk register, change control process, and lessons learned register are immediately useful. The case studies make the concepts concrete.

The weakness is that the course is short (4 modules) and the people analytics content feels like a separate course appended at the end. But as an introduction to project risk management, this course covers the essentials clearly.
