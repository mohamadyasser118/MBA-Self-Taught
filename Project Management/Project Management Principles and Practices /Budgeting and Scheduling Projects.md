## 🧭 Course Overview

This course teaches how to schedule projects effectively. Scheduling is about figuring out what resources you need, how long tasks will take, and in what order they must happen. The course covers three main areas: resource needs and quality management (identifying resources, clarifying roles with RACI, ensuring acceptance criteria), estimating (bottom-up, analogous, parametric, dealing with uncertainty), and scheduling (activity sequencing, critical path method). The core message is that a realistic schedule requires good estimates, clear dependencies, and honest communication about uncertainty — not padded estimates that hide the true scope of work.

# Module 1 — Resource Needs and Quality Management

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Before you can schedule a project, you need to know what resources are required and who is responsible for what. This module teaches how to identify resources from the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) and how to clarify roles using a Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM) with the RACI approach (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed). It also covers quality management — specifically, the importance of defining acceptance criteria for each work package. Without clear acceptance criteria, you cannot know when a task is truly done, which leads to rework, delays, and budget overruns.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Resource Identification:** The process of determining what people, equipment, materials, and facilities are needed to complete each work package. Starts with the WBS.

- **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) Review:** Before assigning resources, revisit the WBS to ensure work packages are detailed enough. If a work package is too vague, you cannot estimate resources accurately.

- **Activity Decomposition:** Breaking down work packages into smaller, assignable activities. This is the step between WBS (deliverables) and scheduling (tasks).

- **Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM):** A tool that clarifies roles and responsibilities within the project team. Prevents role conflict and ambiguity.

- **RACI:** A specific type of RAM with four roles:
  - **Responsible:** The person who does the work. There can be multiple people responsible.
  - **Accountable:** The person who answers for the outcome. Only one person accountable per task.
  - **Consulted:** People whose opinions are sought before a decision or action. Two-way communication.
  - **Informed:** People who are kept updated on progress. One-way communication.

- **Role Clarity:** When everyone knows exactly what they are supposed to do and what others are supposed to do. Prevents overlap (two people doing the same task) and gaps (no one doing a task).

- **Acceptance Criteria:** The conditions that must be met for a work package or deliverable to be accepted as complete. Should be defined before work begins.

- **Quality Management in Projects:** Ensuring that deliverables meet requirements. Defining acceptance criteria is the first step — you cannot inspect quality in after the fact.

- **Rework:** Doing a task again because it was not done correctly the first time. Often caused by unclear acceptance criteria.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The WBS is the starting point for resource identification.** If the WBS is not detailed enough, you cannot figure out what resources you need. Go back and decompose further until each work package is clear.

2. **RACI prevents role confusion.** The case study shows two team members both trying to do the same design work because roles were unclear. A RACI matrix would have prevented this by clearly stating who is Responsible and who is Accountable.

3. **Only one person can be Accountable per task.** This is the most important rule in RACI. If multiple people are accountable, no one is accountable. The Accountable person is the one who answers if something goes wrong.

4. **Consulted is two-way; Informed is one-way.** This distinction is often missed. Consulted people provide input before decisions. Informed people receive updates after decisions. Mixing them up leads to either too many people in meetings or surprised stakeholders.

5. **Acceptance criteria prevent rework.** When team members do not know what "done" looks like, they guess. Guessing leads to rework. Defining acceptance criteria before work starts saves huge amounts of time and money.

6. **Quality cannot be inspected in at the end.** If acceptance criteria are not defined upfront, the only way to check quality is at the end — and by then, fixing problems is expensive. Define criteria early.

---

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |

| RACI | Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed | For task-level role assignment |
| Acceptance Criteria | Conditions for accepting a deliverable | Before starting each work package |
| Quality Definition | Clear, measurable standards for completion | At the start of the project |

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before estimating resources, check that your WBS is detailed enough. If a work package is larger than about 80 hours, decompose it further.

- Create a RACI matrix for major tasks. Share it with the team. Ask: "Does everyone agree on who is Responsible and who is Accountable?"

- Remember the RACI rules: Only one Accountable per task. Responsible people do the work. Consulted people give input (two-way). Informed people get updates (one-way).

- Define acceptance criteria before work begins. Write them down. Get agreement from the person who will accept the deliverable (sponsor, customer, or Accountable person).

- Do not skip acceptance criteria because you are in a hurry. Rushing leads to rework, which takes even more time.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The RACI model is simple but very effective. The key insight is the distinction between Responsible and Accountable. Responsible people do the work. Accountable people answer for it. In many organizations, the same person is both — which is fine. But when tasks cross departments, having a single Accountable person prevents the "everyone's responsibility is no one's responsibility" problem.

The case study about role confusion (Sam and Joe both trying to design) is realistic. In fast-moving projects, people step in to help, and roles blur. The solution is not to stop people from helping — it is to clarify who is Accountable. Sam can still help Joe, but Joe knows he is Accountable for the design.

Acceptance criteria are often overlooked. Teams assume everyone knows what "done" means. They do not. Writing acceptance criteria forces you to think through what complete really looks like. This prevents the painful conversation at the end: "This is not what I asked for."

# Module 2 — Estimating

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Estimating is figuring out how long activities will take and how much they will cost. Good estimates come from the people doing the work. The module covers three estimating techniques: bottom-up (breaking work into small pieces and estimating each), analogous (using historical data from similar tasks), and parametric (using a formula based on consistent historical data). It also covers the problem of padded estimates — when team members add extra time to protect themselves. The module argues that padding is harmful because it hides the true uncertainty. Instead, teams should acknowledge uncertainty openly and use contingency reserves.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Activity Duration:** The amount of work time required to complete a specific task. Does not include waiting time or non-working days.

- **Bottom-Up Estimating:** Breaking work packages down into small activities and estimating each one individually. Sum them up for the total. Most accurate but most time-consuming.

- **Analogous Estimating:** Using historical data from similar past tasks to estimate current tasks. Less accurate than bottom-up but faster. Good for early planning.

- **Parametric Estimating:** Using a formula based on consistent historical data. Example: if installing flooring takes 2 hours per 100 square feet, a 500 square foot room takes 10 hours. Requires good historical data.

- **Contingency Reserve:** Extra time or money added to the project to account for known uncertainties. Example: a risk that a key supplier might be late.

- **Management Reserve:** Extra time or money added for unknown unknowns — things you cannot predict. Controlled by management, not the project team.

- **Padding:** Adding extra time to an estimate to protect against being blamed for missing the deadline. Different from contingency because padding is hidden and not tied to specific risks.

- **Estimation Bias:** Systematic errors in estimating. Common biases: optimism bias (assuming everything will go well), anchoring (sticking to an initial number), and padding (adding extra to be safe).

- **Transparency in Estimation:** Openly discussing uncertainties and risks rather than hiding them in padded estimates. Leads to better project outcomes.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The best estimates come from the people doing the work.** No one else knows the details of the task. The project manager should ask, not guess.

2. **Bottom-up is most accurate but takes time.** For large, complex projects, bottom-up estimating is worth the effort. For small, routine projects, analogous or parametric may be enough.

3. **Padding estimates is harmful.** When team members add extra time to protect themselves, the true uncertainty is hidden. Management sees a comfortable estimate and may add more pressure. The padding grows. No one knows the real estimate.

4. **Instead of padding, use contingency reserves.** If there is a specific risk (e.g., a new technology might be hard), add a contingency reserve for that risk. This is transparent and can be tracked.

5. **Open discussion of uncertainty is best practice.** The case study shows a team nervous about tasks they have never done before. The best response is not to hide this — it is to discuss it openly. Acknowledge the uncertainty. Build in contingency. Then track progress against the contingency.

6. **Do not punish people for missing padded estimates.** If team members add padding because they fear being blamed, that is a management problem, not a team problem. Create a culture where realistic estimates are safe.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Bottom-Up Estimating | Estimate small pieces, sum up | Detailed planning, complex projects |
| Analogous Estimating | Use historical data from similar tasks | Early planning, when detail is low |
| Parametric Estimating | Use formula based on historical data | When historical data is consistent and reliable |
| Contingency Reserve | Extra time/money for known risks | After identifying specific uncertainties |
| Management Reserve | Extra time/money for unknown unknowns | Added by management, not team |
| Transparent Estimating | Open discussion of uncertainty | Always — best practice |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Parametric Estimate:** Estimate = (Quantity per unit) × (Number of units)

- **Bottom-Up Total:** Total = Σ (Estimates of individual activities)

- **Contingency Reserve:** Added to base estimate for specific risks

- **Total Project Budget:** Base Estimate + Contingency Reserve + Management Reserve

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Ask the people doing the work for estimates. Do not guess yourself.

- Use bottom-up estimating for important or complex tasks. It takes time but saves more time later.

- Do not punish realistic estimates. If team members are afraid to give honest numbers, you will never know the true scope of work.

- Instead of padding, add contingency reserves for specific risks. This is transparent and allows you to track whether the risk actually happens.

- Discuss uncertainty openly. Say: "We have never done this before, so our estimate is uncertain. We will track progress and adjust."

- If you are a team member, do not pad your estimates. Explain the risks instead. Good project managers will appreciate the honesty.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The padding problem is real and widespread. Team members add extra time because they fear being blamed for missing a deadline. Management sees the padded estimate and adds more pressure. The padding grows. The real estimate is lost. The solution is cultural: do not punish people for honest estimates. Accept that estimates are uncertain. Track progress and adjust.

The case study about a team facing unfamiliar tasks is realistic. When people have never done something before, their estimates are uncertain. The best practice is to acknowledge this openly, not hide it. Use a range (3-5 weeks instead of 4 weeks). Build in contingency. Check in frequently.

Bottom-up estimating is the gold standard for a reason. When you break work into small pieces (work packages of 8-80 hours), the estimates become much more accurate. The time spent decomposing and estimating is repaid many times over in reduced rework and schedule surprises.

# Module 3 — Scheduling

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Scheduling is about putting activities in the right order and figuring out how long the project will take. Activities have dependencies — some must happen before others (mandatory), some are based on best practice (discretionary), and some depend on outside events (external). The Critical Path Method (CPM) is the key tool: it calculates the earliest and latest each activity can start and finish. The critical path is the longest path through the project — it determines the minimum project duration. Any delay on the critical path delays the entire project. Non-critical activities have float (slack) — they can be delayed without affecting the project finish.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Activity Sequencing:** Putting project activities in the order they must be performed. Based on dependencies.

- **Mandatory Dependency (Hard Logic):** A dependency required by the nature of the work. Example: you cannot paint a wall until the drywall is installed.

- **Discretionary Dependency (Soft Logic, Preferred Logic):** A dependency based on best practice or preference, not physical necessity. Example: you could design before testing, but you might choose to test early prototypes.

- **External Dependency:** A dependency on something outside the project. Example: waiting for a supplier to deliver materials, waiting for regulatory approval.

- **Critical Path Method (CPM):** A scheduling technique that calculates the earliest and latest start and finish times for each activity. Identifies the critical path.

- **Critical Path:** The longest path through the project network. Determines the minimum project completion time. Activities on the critical path have zero float.

- **Early Start (ES):** The earliest an activity can start, given its predecessors.

- **Early Finish (EF):** ES + Duration.

- **Late Start (LS):** The latest an activity can start without delaying the project.

- **Late Finish (LF):** LS + Duration (or the latest an activity can finish without delaying the project).

- **Float (Slack):** The amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying the project. LS - ES = LF - EF. Activities on the critical path have float = 0.

- **Forward Pass:** Calculating early start and early finish by moving forward through the network.

- **Backward Pass:** Calculating late start and late finish by moving backward through the network.

- **Milestones:** Significant events or achievements in the project. Usually have zero duration.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The critical path determines the project duration.** No matter how fast you do other tasks, the project will not finish faster than the critical path. This is the most important insight in scheduling.

2. **Float is not free.** Non-critical activities have float, but using float may change the critical path. If you delay a non-critical activity too much, it can become critical.

3. **Dependencies must be understood before scheduling.** You cannot build a schedule without knowing which activities depend on which. Start with mandatory dependencies, then add discretionary and external.

4. **External dependencies are risky.** When you depend on someone outside the project (supplier, regulator, another department), you have less control. Build in extra contingency for external dependencies.

5. **The critical path can change.** As the project progresses, delays on non-critical activities can make them critical. The critical path is not fixed — recalculate regularly.

6. **A realistic schedule includes resource availability.** The Critical Path Method as taught here assumes unlimited resources. In reality, resources are limited. If two critical activities need the same person at the same time, one must wait — this changes the critical path. This is called resource leveling.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Mandatory Dependency | Required by nature of work | Always — the foundation of scheduling |
| Discretionary Dependency | Based on best practice | To optimize the schedule |
| External Dependency | Outside project control | To identify risks |
| Critical Path Method (CPM) | Calculate early/late start/finish, find critical path | For any project schedule |
| Forward Pass | Calculate ES and EF | To find earliest project finish |
| Backward Pass | Calculate LS and LF | To find float |
| Float (Slack) | LS - ES | To see which activities have flexibility |
| Milestones | Zero-duration events | To mark important achievements |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Early Finish (Forward Pass):** EF = ES + Duration

- **Late Start (Backward Pass):** LS = LF - Duration

- **Float (Slack):** Float = LS - ES = LF - EF

- **Critical Path:** Path where all activities have Float = 0

- **Project Duration:** EF of the last activity on the critical path

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Always start scheduling by identifying mandatory dependencies. What must happen before what? This is non-negotiable.

- Use the Critical Path Method for any project with more than a few activities. Even a simple network diagram reveals insights.

- Focus management attention on the critical path. Delaying a critical path activity delays the project. Delaying a non-critical activity may not — but watch float.

- Recalculate the critical path regularly. As work progresses, the critical path can shift.

- Be careful with external dependencies. Add contingency. Communicate early with external parties.

- If resources are limited (they always are), do resource leveling after finding the critical path. You may need to adjust the schedule.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Critical Path Method is one of the most valuable tools in project management. It seems simple — find the longest path — but the implications are profound. Any delay on the critical path delays the project. Managers can spend all their energy speeding up non-critical activities and accomplish nothing. The discipline of identifying the critical path forces you to focus on what actually matters.

The distinction between mandatory and discretionary dependencies is often overlooked. Teams assume a sequence is required when it is actually just habit. Challenging discretionary dependencies can shorten the schedule significantly. "Why do we have to do A before B? Could we do them in parallel?"

The case study about acceptance criteria connects scheduling to quality. If acceptance criteria are not defined, rework happens. Rework adds activities to the schedule — often on the critical path. Defining acceptance criteria early is a scheduling tool as much as a quality tool.

# ✅ Course-Level Takeaways

This course provides the essential tools for creating realistic project schedules. The key insights: start with a detailed WBS, identify resources, and clarify roles using RACI. Estimate using bottom-up, analogous, or parametric methods — and be transparent about uncertainty instead of padding. Schedule activities by understanding dependencies, then use the Critical Path Method to find the minimum project duration. The critical path determines when the project will finish — focus management attention there.

The course's strength is its practical, tool-based approach. RACI, CPM, bottom-up estimating, and acceptance criteria are immediately useful. The case studies (role confusion, padding estimates, acceptance criteria) make the concepts concrete.

The weakness is that the course does not cover resource leveling (handling limited resources) or scheduling software in depth. But as an introduction to project scheduling, this course covers the essentials clearly.
