# Module 1 — Introduction to Getting a Consulting Job

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Consulting is fundamentally about teamwork, requiring good listening, logical communication, business acumen, and the ability to work well with experienced clients. The case interview tests these skills by simulating real consulting problem-solving scenarios. Consulting firms use a pyramid staffing model (senior partners at top, junior analysts at bottom) balancing experience and learning. Networking is crucial — the course covers how to conduct informational interviews and make strong impressions to secure case interview invitations.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Consulting Teamwork:** Requires good listening, logical communication, business acumen, ability to work with experienced clients.

- **Case Interview:** Simulates real consulting problem-solving under ambiguity. Tests structured thinking, business sense, mental math, communication.

- **Pyramid Staffing Model:** Senior partners at top (fewest), junior analysts at bottom (most). Balances quality, cost, and learning.

- **Two Interview Types:**
  - **Behavioral (Fit) Interview:** Resume and personal questions. Tests self-awareness, leadership, problem-solving, entrepreneurial drive.
  - **Case Interview:** Problem-solving under ambiguity. Tests structured thinking, business acumen, mental math.

- **Leverage:** Using a small input to move a big problem. Consultants provide leverage to clients. In firms, leverage means delegation (seniors delegate to juniors).

- **Finders, Minders, Grinders:**
  - **Finders:** Partners who find new business and maintain client relationships.
  - **Minders:** Manage projects, teams, and client interactions.
  - **Grinders:** Less experienced staff who perform daily work (data collection, analysis).

- **Utilization:** Staying billable. Consultants are a fixed cost; they must generate enough value to cover their costs by doing client work.

- **On the Beach / On the Bench:** Not staffed on a project. Minimize by networking, helping with proposals, upgrading skills.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Consulting is an apprenticeship.** You learn by doing, observing senior consultants, practicing with feedback, and teaching juniors.

2. **The pyramid structure (finders, minders, grinders) balances quality, cost, and learning.** Seniors ensure quality; juniors learn and perform less expensive tasks.

3. **Case interviews are the gatekeeper.** No case = no job. Practice is essential.

4. **Networking is crucial.** Many qualified candidates never get interviews because they did not network. Informational interviews are low-risk ways to build relationships.

5. **Consulting is about making other people successful.** Be like John Stockton (assists leader). Teamwork and selflessness matter.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

|-----------------|-----------|----------------|

| Finders, Minders, Grinders | Roles by experience level | Career progression |

| Behavioral Interview | Fit and personal questions | Job interviews |
| Informational Interview | Low-risk fact-finding | Networking |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

- **Client Satisfaction:** Satisfaction = Perception - Expectations

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Practice case interviews with peers. Do not skip this. No case = no job.

- Network before you need something. Build relationships through informational interviews.

- Be self-aware. Consulting firms want candidates who are coachable, not arrogant.

- Use university resources (career centers, consulting clubs, alumni networks).

# Module 2 — Consulting Teams and Leverage

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Consulting firms use a pyramid structure (finders, minders, grinders) to balance quality, cost, and learning. Leverage means delegation — seniors delegate lower-value tasks to juniors, allowing them to focus on higher-value work. Understanding project profitability is critical: fixed fee is most common, but performance-based models are emerging. Utilization (staying billable) is essential for junior consultants. Generative AI is changing team structures, enabling smaller, more skilled teams.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Leverage in Consulting:** Using a small input to move a big problem. In firms, leverage means delegation (seniors delegate to juniors). Raises profitability by raising bill rate or reducing costs.

- **Pyramid Structure:** Fewer senior roles (partners), many junior roles (analysts). Balances quality (seniors ensure quality), cost (juniors less expensive), and learning (juniors learn by doing).

- **Finders:** Partners who find new business, maintain client relationships, shape firm strategy.

- **Minders:** Manage projects, teams, client interactions. Ensure projects run on time and budget.

- **Grinders:** Less experienced staff. Perform data collection, research, analysis, interviews, presentations.

- **Solo Entrepreneurs:** Play all three roles. Finding business is priority #1.

- **Project Profitability:**
  - **Fixed Fee (most common):** Project cost is set. Efficiency increases profit.
  - **Performance-based (emerging):** Payment depends on value delivered (like commission).

- **Utilization:** Staying billable. Consultants are a fixed cost; they must generate enough value to cover costs by doing client work.

- **On the Beach / On the Bench:** Not staffed on a project. Minimize by networking, helping with proposals, training.

- **GenAI Impact on Consulting:**
  - Acts as a new, low-cost layer beneath traditional roles.
  - Teams becoming smaller and more skilled (one analyst can replace multiple traditional roles).
  - Changes team structure and client expectations.

- **Hourglass Career Path:**
  - Analyst: generalist, learn aggressively, say yes
  - Senior manager: specialize, say no
  - Partner: generalist again, solve client problems, say yes

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **The pyramid structure is the economic model of consulting.** Seniors ensure quality; juniors provide lower-cost labor and learn. Without leverage, the model breaks.

2. **Generative AI is changing the pyramid.** Teams are becoming smaller and more skilled. One analyst with AI can replace multiple traditional roles.

3. **Utilization is essential for junior consultants.** Being "on the beach" too long signals you are not valuable. Take ownership of your staffing.

4. **Fixed fee is the most common payment model.** Efficiency increases profit. Performance-based models are emerging but still rare.

5. **The hourglass career path is common in generalist firms.** Generalist → specialist → generalist. Specialization happens at senior manager/junior partner level.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Leverage (Pyramid) | Few seniors, many juniors | Understanding firm economics |
| Finders, Minders, Grinders | Roles by experience level | Career progression |
| Project Profitability | Fixed fee vs. performance-based | Pricing consulting work |
| Utilization | Staying billable | Junior consultant career management |
| Hourglass Career Path | Generalist → specialist → generalist | Long-term career planning |
| GenAI Impact | Smaller, more skilled teams | Future of consulting |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

- **Profitability (Fixed Fee):** Profit = Fixed Fee - (Consultant Hours × Hourly Cost) - Overhead

- **Utilization Rate:** Billable Hours / Total Available Hours

---

## F. Practical Implications

- As a junior consultant, focus on staying billable. Take ownership of your staffing.

- Do simple tasks well (meeting minutes, executive communications). These build reputation.

- Understand project profitability. Even if you are not a partner, knowing how money is made helps you make better decisions.

- Embrace AI as a tool. It will not replace consultants, but consultants who use AI will replace those who do not.

---

- [x] Explain consulting leverage (pyramid structure)
- [x] Distinguish finders, minders, grinders
- [x] Understand project profitability (fixed fee, performance-based)

- [x] Explain how GenAI is changing consulting teams

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The pyramid structure is the economic engine of consulting. Without leverage (juniors doing lower-cost work), the model would not work. But it also creates a learning environment — juniors learn by doing, with oversight from seniors.

The GenAI point is timely. Teams are becoming smaller and more skilled. One analyst with AI can do what three analysts did before. This will change staffing models and career paths.

The hourglass career path (generalist → specialist → generalist) explains why consultants can work across industries early in their careers, then specialize, then become generalist partners. It is not a linear path.

# Module 3 — Project Preparation

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Preparation before a project starts is critical. Read the statement of work carefully. Research the client's business, industry, competitors, suppliers, customers. Review financial statements (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow). Get smart quickly using online resources, colleagues, books, podcasts. Internal consultants have advantages (knowledge, relationships) but also challenges (poorly scoped projects, lack of transparency). Act like an external consultant: clarify objectives, document plans, formalize processes.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Before Project Starts:**
  - Understand project scope, role, logistics (travel, client expectations)
  - Read statement of work carefully (goals, duration, success metrics)
  - Research client's business, industry, competitors, suppliers, customers
  - Review financial statements (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow)
  - Talk to colleagues familiar with client or industry

- **Getting Smart Quickly (Consulting Superpower):**
  - Use online resources (company reports, credible websites, financial databases)
  - Use books, podcasts, industry publications
  - Talk to colleagues and contacts
  - Share findings with team

- **Internal Consulting Challenges:**
  - Poorly scoped projects (vague objectives → unclear expectations → trouble)
  - Executives keep projects low-profile (hinders collaboration, transparency)
  - Using outdated or insufficient data
  - Lack of credibility (internal consultants often ignored)

- **Internal Consulting Advantages:**
  - Knowledge of industry, company, culture
  - Existing relationships
  - Time (not traveling, can build deeper understanding)

- **Act Like an External Consultant:**
  - Clarify objectives
  - Document plans
  - Formalize processes
  - Push for resources and transparency
  - Use data and structured approaches

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Preparation builds confidence.** Researching the client and industry before the project starts makes you credible from day one.

2. **Getting smart quickly is a consulting superpower.** Consultants work on diverse projects across industries. The ability to learn rapidly is essential.

3. **Internal consultants have huge opportunities but face unique challenges.** The same knowledge and relationships that are advantages can become messy short-cuts. Act like an external consultant.

4. **Financial statements tell a story.** The income statement shows profitability; balance sheet shows assets/liabilities; cash flow shows liquidity. Read them before the project starts.

5. **Do not hide bad news.** Escalate issues promptly to managers who can help resolve them.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Statement of Work (SOW) | Project scope, goals, duration, metrics | Project initiation |
| Financial Statement Analysis | Income statement, balance sheet, cash flow | Understanding client health |
| Getting Smart Quickly | Online research, colleagues, books, podcasts | Before project starts |
| Internal Consulting Best Practices | Act like external consultant, clarify objectives | Internal projects |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before any project, spend time researching. Do not show up unprepared.

- Read the client's annual report and 10-K. Understand their business model.

- For internal consultants, formalize processes even if they seem unnecessary. Documentation prevents scope creep.

- If you are a junior consultant, escalate bad news immediately. Do not hide it.

---

## G. Learning Outcomes

By completing this module, I can now:

- [x] Prepare before a project starts (research client, industry, financials)
- [x] Get smart quickly using multiple sources
- [x] Understand internal consulting advantages and challenges

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The "getting smart quickly" superpower is real. Consultants cannot be experts in every industry, but they can become conversant in weeks. This requires disciplined research habits.

Internal consulting is harder than external in some ways. External consultants have "outsider" credibility; internal consultants are often ignored. Formal processes (project charters, status reports) help.

# Module 4 — Networking and Recruiting

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Networking is important and takes effort. Professionals should continually network because we cannot predict all opportunities. Networking is about building genuine, relevant relationships — not superficial interactions. Be generous, be curious, be patient. Informational interviews are low-risk ways to learn about firms and build relationships (do not ask for a job). Consulting firms look for problem-solving skills, entrepreneurial drive, proven leadership, and strong communication. Resumes are bait — they should be concise, error-free, relevant, and impactful using PAR (Problem, Action, Result) format.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Networking Principles:**
  - Be relevant (provide context when reaching out)
  - Be generous (give value, do good work, help others)
  - Be patient (relationships take years, not days)
  - Build relationships before you need them

- **Informational Interview:**
  - Low-risk fact-finding mission (20 minutes)
  - Do NOT ask for a job
  - Be prepared, ask smart (not generic) questions
  - Listen; goal is to make a good impression and START a relationship

- **What Consulting Firms Look For:**
  - Problem-solving skills (structured thinking)
  - Entrepreneurial drive (initiative, bias toward action)
  - Proven leadership (team, client, community)
  - Strong communication (clear, logical, concise)
  - Grit, business sense, curiosity, integrity, client service mindset

- **Resume Best Practices:**
  - Resumes are "bait" to get an interview (that is it)
  - Use PAR (Problem, Action, Result) format for bullets
  - Quantify achievements
  - Have different resumes for different roles (consulting vs. finance vs. marketing)
  - Get brutal feedback, keep refining

- **Behavioral Interview:** Tests fit, self-awareness, leadership, problem-solving, entrepreneurial drive. Expect questions about achievements, failures, team experiences.

- **Case Interview:** Tests structured thinking, business acumen, mental math, communication. Simulates real consulting problem-solving.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Networking is a long-term investment.** Do not network only when you need a job. Build relationships over years.

2. **Informational interviews are the most effective networking tool.** Low risk, high reward. People are generous with their time if you are prepared.

3. **Consulting firms openly share what they look for.** Read their websites. Attend their events. Do informational interviews.

4. **Resumes are bait, not the full story.** They need to be good enough to get an interview, but the interview gets the job.

5. **Be yourself — on your best day.** Do not be fake. Firms want to hire smart, ambitious, dependable, fun people.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Informational Interview | Low-risk fact-finding | Networking, recruiting |
| PAR (Problem, Action, Result) | Bullet format for resumes | Resume writing |
| Behavioral Interview | Fit and personal questions | Job interviews |
| Case Interview | Simulated problem-solving | Job interviews |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Start networking early. Do not wait until you are job searching.

- Send informational interview requests. Be respectful of time. Prepare smart questions.

- Customize your resume for consulting. Use PAR format. Quantify achievements.

- Practice behavioral interview questions with peers.

- Be likeable. Firms hire people they want to work with.

---

- [x] Identify what consulting firms look for
- [x] Write a consulting resume (PAR format, quantified achievements)

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The informational interview is underutilized. Many candidates network only through formal recruiting events. Informational interviews are more personal and effective. The rule "do not ask for a job" is important — it keeps the conversation low-pressure.

The PAR format (Problem, Action, Result) is the standard for consulting resumes. It forces you to be specific and quantitative. "Led team of 5 to increase sales by 20%" is better than "Responsible for sales."

# Module 5 — Behavioral and Case Interviews

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Behavioral interviews test "fit" — self-awareness, leadership, problem-solving, entrepreneurial drive. Firms want to hire smart, self-aware, fun, and eager professionals. Case interviews test structured thinking under ambiguity. The case interview is a dialogue, not an interrogation. Think out loud. Use data and basic math. Listen to the interviewer — they will give clues. Make a clear recommendation at the end, even with limited data. Practice is essential. No case = no job.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Behavioral Interview (Fit Interview):**
  - Tests self-awareness, leadership, problem-solving, entrepreneurial drive
  - Expect questions about achievements, failures, team experiences, leadership
  - Goal: demonstrate you are smart, self-aware, fun, and eager

- **Common Behavioral Questions:**
  - "Tell me about a time you led a team."
  - "Tell me about a failure and what you learned."
  - "Why consulting? Why this firm?"

- **Case Interview Structure:**
  - Interviewer presents a business situation
  - Candidate outlines a structured plan
  - Candidate engages in dialogue, asks logical questions, analyzes data
  - Candidate makes recommendation at the end

- **Case Interview Best Practices:**
  - Think out loud (show your thinking)
  - Use data and basic math (mental agility)
  - Listen to the interviewer (they will give clues)
  - Make it a business discussion, not an interview
  - Finish strong: summarize, recommend, next steps

- **Practice:** Essential. Case interviews are unique. Cannot succeed without practice. Practice with peers, use online resources, record yourself.

- **What Firms Say About Case Interviews:**
  - "We want to see you think." – BCG
  - "Make it a business discussion, not an interview." – Bain
  - "Stop and think, don't just start talking." – Deloitte
  - "Listen. The interviewer will give you tips." – McKinsey

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Case interviews test structured thinking under ambiguity.** There is rarely one right answer. The process matters more than the answer.

2. **The case interview is a dialogue, not an interrogation.** Engage with the interviewer. Ask clarifying questions. Build rapport.

3. **Listen to the interviewer.** They will give clues. If you are going down the wrong path, they will redirect you.

4. **Practice is non-negotiable.** No case = no job. Practice with peers, use online case libraries, record yourself.

5. **Be yourself — on your best day.** Do not try to be someone else. Firms want authentic people.

---

## D. Frameworks, Models & Tools

| Framework / Tool | What It Is | When to Use It |
|-----------------|-----------|----------------|
| Behavioral Interview | Fit and personal questions | Job interviews |
| Case Interview | Simulated problem-solving | Job interviews |
| Case Interview Structure | Outline plan, ask questions, analyze, recommend | Case interviews |
| Practice | Peer practice, online cases, recording | Interview preparation |

---

## E. Formulas & Equations

> *No formulas in this module — conceptual frameworks only.*

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Practice case interviews with peers. Do at least 20-30 cases before real interviews.

- Use online case libraries (consulting firms publish sample cases).

- Record yourself practicing. Listen for clarity, structure, and confidence.

- In behavioral interviews, be specific. Use PAR format even when speaking.

- Be likeable. Smile. Build rapport. Firms hire people they want to work with.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The case interview is the gatekeeper. Many candidates with excellent resumes fail at the case interview because they did not practice enough. It is a skill like any other — it requires deliberate practice.

The advice to "think out loud" is crucial. Interviewers cannot read your mind. If you are silent, they cannot give you credit for your thinking. Narrate your process.

The point about listening is underappreciated. Interviewers give clues. If you ignore them, you will fail.
