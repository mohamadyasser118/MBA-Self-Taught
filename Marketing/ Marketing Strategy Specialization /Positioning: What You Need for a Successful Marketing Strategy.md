## 🧭 Course Overview

This course focuses on positioning as the central element of marketing strategy. Positioning connects the target group (who) with the value proposition (what). The course covers the entire marketing process: market environment analysis (Five Cs: customers, company, competitors, collaborators, context), segmentation (geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral), differentiation (points of parity and points of difference), and positioning (positioning statements, perceptual maps, spiderweb maps). The core message is that positioning is not what the company believes, but what the target group perceives — and it must be dynamic, adapting to changing customer preferences.

# Module 1 — Introduction to Positioning

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Positioning underpins the entire marketing and commercial plan. There are two main approaches: start with a product and then find a target segment interested in it, or start with a well-defined target group and develop a product that meets their needs. The marketing process begins with market environment analysis using the Five Cs (customers, company, competitors, collaborators, context/PEST). Positioning answers "who is the product for?" and "what value does it offer?" The marketing process can be viewed as value assessment (Five Cs), value creation (positioning), and value delivery/capture (marketing mix).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Positioning:** Designing a company's offering to meet the needs of a specific target segment with a unique value proposition. Creates a clear, distinctive image in the customer's mind.

- **Two Approaches to Positioning:**
  - **Product-first:** Start with product/value proposition, then identify market segments interested in it.
  - **Target-first:** Start with well-defined target group, understand their needs, then develop value proposition.

- **Five Cs Framework (Market Environment Analysis):**
  - **Context (PEST):** Political, Economic, Social, Technological factors.
  - **Customers:** Purchasers, final users, prescriptors/influencers, internal customers, dealers/distributors.
  - **Company:** Resources, strengths, limitations, market image, current marketing efforts.
  - **Competitors:** Current and potential competitors (broad view).
  - **Collaborators:** Distribution channels, key opinion leaders, third parties.

- **Marketing Process as Value Creation:**
  - Value assessment → Five Cs
  - Value creation → Positioning (target group + value proposition)
  - Value delivery and capture → Marketing mix (4 Ps)

- **Benefits of Good Positioning:** Facilitates product selection, enhances customer benefits, increases brand value, organizes marketing strategies, aligns marketing mix.

- **Consequences of Poor Positioning:** Weak brand image, low ROI, price-only competition.

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Positioning is the bridge between market analysis and the marketing mix.** Without clear positioning, the 4 Ps are disconnected and ineffective.

2. **The Five Cs provide a complete market picture.** Many companies focus only on customers and competitors, ignoring collaborators (distribution channels, partners) and context (PEST factors).

3. **Customers are not monolithic.** Distinguish between purchasers, users, influencers, internal customers, and distributors. Each may have different needs.

4. **Competitors should be viewed broadly.** Telecom companies compete not only with other telecoms but also with OTT players like Skype or Google.

5. **Honest company self-assessment is essential.** Overestimating strengths or ignoring limitations leads to failed positioning.

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Start every marketing plan with Five Cs analysis. Do not skip to the 4 Ps.
- Distinguish between different customer types (purchasers, users, influencers). Tailor value propositions accordingly.
- Be honest about company strengths and limitations. Overconfidence leads to failed positioning.
- Positioning must be customer-perceived, not company-believed.

# Module 2 — Segmentation

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Segmentation divides the market into groups that share similar characteristics, needs, and behaviors. The segmentation continuum ranges from mass market (one offering for all) to segment of one (customized for individual customers). Segmentation criteria include geographic, demographic, psychographic (values, attitudes, personality), and behavioral (usage, benefits sought, brand attitudes). Effective segmentation must be measurable, accessible, profitable, differentiable, and actionable. Segmentation strategies include concentrated (one segment), selective specialization (multiple segments), market specialization (one segment with multiple products), product specialization (one product adapted for multiple segments), and full market coverage (differentiated or undifferentiated marketing).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Segmentation Continuum:**
  - **Mass market:** One offering for a broad, homogeneous market.
  - **Market segments:** Groups with similar characteristics/behaviors.
  - **Niches:** Very small, specialized segments within market segments.
  - **Segment of one:** Customized for individual customers.

- **Segmentation Criteria:**
  - **Geographic:** Country, region, city, postal code, climate, culture.
  - **Demographic:** Gender, age, education, income level.
  - **Psychographic:** Values, attitudes, personality, aspirations (extrovert/introvert, innovator/laggard).
  - **Behavioral:** Decision roles, usage levels, benefits sought, brand attitudes, purchase frequency, occasion of use.

- **Criteria for Effective Segmentation (Measurable, Accessible, Profitable, Differentiable, Actionable):**
  - **Measurable:** Data available to identify and size segments.
  - **Accessible:** Can effectively communicate and serve the segment.
  - **Profitable:** Large enough with low margins or small enough with high margins.
  - **Differentiable:** Segments respond differently to marketing mixes.
  - **Actionable:** Marketing efforts elicit a response.

- **Segmentation Strategies:**
  - **Concentrated:** One segment, one product. Deep knowledge but high risk.
  - **Selective specialization:** Multiple segments, different products. Each segment profitable on its own.
  - **Market specialization:** One segment, multiple products/services tailored to that segment.
  - **Product specialization:** One product adapted for different segments (e.g., piano for kids, bands, professionals).
  - **Full market coverage (differentiated):** Different products for different segments (L'Oréal house of brands).
  - **Full market coverage (undifferentiated):** One product for all segments.

- **Evolution of Segmentation (Music industry example):** 1950s-1990s: mass market, broad genres, less demanding customers. Today: highly fragmented, many small segments, demanding customers, short product lifecycles.

- **Long Tail:** E-commerce enables profitable serving of niche markets (Amazon example).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Digitalization has enabled hyper-fragmentation.** The long tail (Amazon, Netflix) allows profitable serving of niche segments that were previously inaccessible due to physical distribution constraints.

2. **Demographic segmentation has limitations.** Assuming video games are only for young people is incorrect. Combine with psychographic and behavioral criteria for richer understanding.

3. **Behavioral data is powerful.** Barcodes, loyalty cards, CRM systems, and ethnography reveal actual behavior, not just stated intentions.

4. **Segmentation must be dynamic.** Markets evolve. Segmentation strategies must be regularly reviewed and updated.

5. **Differentiated marketing has higher potential but also higher costs.** The L'Oréal house of brands strategy (mass, luxury, professional, active user segments) is expensive but effective.

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Do not rely on demographic segmentation alone. Combine with psychographic and behavioral data.
- Use loyalty cards and CRM systems to track actual behavior, not just stated preferences.
- For niche markets, the long tail (e-commerce) makes profitable serving possible.
- Segmentation must be actionable. If you cannot reach or serve the segment, it is useless.

# Module 3 — Differentiation and Value Proposition

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Differentiation creates unique attributes that provide reasons for customers to choose a product over competitors. The value proposition communicates the benefits and reasons to buy. Points of parity (PoP) are attributes shared with competitors — necessary to be considered in the category. Points of difference (PoD) are unique advantages that distinguish the brand. The Three Circles Model (Urbany & Davis) visually compares customer needs, company offerings, and competitor offerings. Differentiation strategies include product, service, employee, technology, and brand image differentiation.

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Differentiation:** Offering a product with attributes and values perceived as different by target customers compared to competitors.

- **Value Proposition:** The set of benefits and experience promised to the customer. Includes differentiated attributes. Often expressed through the brand promise.

- **Frame of Reference:** The market context where a product competes. Must be defined from the customer's need perspective, not just product category.

- **Points of Parity (PoP):** Attributes or benefits shared with competitors. Necessary to be considered in the category. Must be met before focusing on differentiation.

- **Points of Difference (PoD):** Unique advantages or superior attributes perceived by customers. Criteria: relevant, exclusive, trustworthy, credible.

- **Three Circles Model (Urbany & Davis):**
  - Circle 1: Customer needs (explicit and latent)
  - Circle 2: Company offerings (as perceived by customers)
  - Circle 3: Competitor offerings
  - Intersections reveal points of parity, points of difference, and gaps.

- **Criteria for Points of Difference:**
  - **Relevant:** Important to target customer group.
  - **Exclusive:** Distinctive and superior to competitors.
  - **Trustworthy/Credible:** Customers believe the claim.
  - Aligns with customer beliefs and corporate brand values.

- **Levels of Differentiation:**
  - Product attributes (ingredients, features)
  - Product benefits (saving time, convenience)
  - Values (caring for family, sustainability)

- **Differentiation Strategies:**
  - **Product:** Design, packaging, features, perceived quality.
  - **Service:** Customer support, delivery, problem-solving.
  - **Employee:** Competencies, courtesy, problem-solving abilities.
  - **Technology:** R&D, access to superior technology.
  - **Brand Image:** Emotional connections, brand perception.

- **Reverse Positioning:** Removing attributes rather than adding them to better meet customer needs. Example: Ikea (no delivery, no assembly, lower prices).

- **Break-Away Positioning (Blue Ocean Strategy):** Creating new market space by challenging industry assumptions. Example: Cirque Du Soleil (circus + theater).

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Marketing is fundamentally about differentiation.** Either be best-in-class on cost or offer unique value.

2. **Points of parity must be satisfied before points of difference matter.** A product that fails on basic category expectations cannot be saved by unique features.

3. **In developed economies, differentiation focuses on benefits and values, not just attributes.** Functional attributes are table stakes.

4. **The Three Circles Model reveals gaps and opportunities.** Areas where customer needs are unmet by either company or competitors are opportunities.

5. **Reverse positioning (less is more) can be powerful.** Ikea removed services and lowered prices, creating a new value proposition.

6. **Blue Ocean strategy creates new markets.** Cirque Du Soleil did not compete in the struggling circus market; they created a new category (circus + theater).

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Before differentiating, ensure points of parity are met. A hotel cannot differentiate on family-friendly activities if basic cleanliness fails.

- In developed markets, move beyond attribute differentiation to benefits and values.

- Use the Three Circles Model to visualize where you stand relative to competitors and customer needs.

- Consider reverse positioning (removing features) for cost-sensitive segments.

- For truly innovative offerings, consider break-away positioning to create a new category.

Gaps are opportunities.

Reverse positioning (Ikea) is counterintuitive. Conventional wisdom says "add more features." Ikea removed features (delivery, assembly) and won. Sometimes less is more.

# Module 4 — Positioning

## A. Core Idea of the Module

Positioning is what the target group perceives, not what the company believes. It is dynamic and can change over time. This module covers positioning statements (target group, value proposition, frame of reference, reason to believe), perceptual maps (2D and spiderweb), alternative positioning strategies (reverse, break-away), and key market trends impacting positioning (hyper-competition, trading up/down, data analytics, long tail, new generations, extreme customers, purpose-driven positioning).

---

## B. Key Concepts & Definitions

- **Positioning (Ries & Trout):** Creating a clear, distinctive image in the customer's mind amid a flood of communication messages.

- **Positioning Statement Formula:** "For [target group], Brand X is the [value proposition] among [frame of reference] because [reason to believe]."

- **Positioning Statement vs. Tagline:** Taglines are shorter, catchy phrases used in marketing communications. Examples: Volvo ("for security"), Pepsi ("The Pepsi Generation").

- **Perceptual (Positioning) Maps:** Matrices representing key product attributes valued by target market. Visualize how brands are perceived and identify market opportunities.

- **Steps to Create Perceptual Maps:**
  1. Identify most important attributes (market research).
  2. Survey customers to rate brands on attributes.
  3. Prioritize attributes by importance.
  4. Plot brands on 2D map using two key attributes.
  5. Identify customer segments as clusters (circle size = segment size).

- **Spiderweb Perceptual Map (Multidimensional):** Incorporates several attributes simultaneously. Shows relative brand positioning across multiple attributes and preferences of different market segments.

- **Citroën Berlingo Case Study:** In late 1990s, small industrial vehicles were functional but lacked aesthetics. Citroën created a hybrid (work + personal use) with rounded design, air conditioning, flashy colors. Clear positioning for professional users with dual needs. Competitors were surprised.

- **Alternative Positioning Strategies:**
  - **Reverse Positioning:** Removing attributes (Ikea).
  - **Break-Away Positioning (Blue Ocean):** Creating new market space (Cirque Du Soleil, Busuu).

- **Key Market Trends Impacting Positioning (8 trends):**
  1. Hyper-competition (globalization, commoditization)
  2. Trading up and down (premium for well-being, price-sensitive for other categories)
  3. Access to information and internet (reduced brand control)
  4. Data analytics (customized offerings)
  5. Long tail (profitable niche markets via e-commerce)
  6. New generations (millennials have distinct values, consumption patterns)
  7. Engaging extreme customers (passionate, obsessed, creative users for innovation)
  8. Positioning with purpose (social impact, sustainability)

---

## C. Main Arguments & Insights

1. **Positioning is not what the company believes; it is what the target group perceives.** Market research is essential.

2. **Positioning is dynamic.** Customer preferences evolve. Positioning must be regularly reviewed and adjusted.

3. **Perceptual maps reveal competitive positioning and gaps.** Underserved segments (clusters with no nearby brands) are opportunities.

4. **The marketing mix must align with positioning.** Samsung Galaxy S7 (premium, selective distribution) and Ryanair (no-frills, direct online) are opposite examples of consistent alignment.

5. **Citroën Berlingo succeeded because they understood that users wanted the vehicle for both work and personal life.** Traditional competitors focused only on functionality.

6. **Positioning with purpose (social impact) is not just ethical — it is profitable.** Research shows meaningful brands outperform others economically.

---

## F. Practical Implications

- Write a positioning statement for your brand using the formula. If you cannot, your positioning is unclear.

- Test positioning with market research. What the company believes is irrelevant; what customers perceive matters.

- Use perceptual maps to identify underserved segments. Clusters with no nearby brands are opportunities.

- Ensure marketing mix (4 Ps) aligns with positioning. Inconsistent mix confuses customers.

- Consider purpose-driven positioning. Customers increasingly value brands that contribute to societal well-being.

## H. My Reflections & Critical Thoughts

The Citroën Berlingo case is a classic example of customer-centric positioning. They did not ask "how can we make a better industrial vehicle?" They asked "how do users want to use the vehicle?" The answer: both work and personal life. Competitors focused only on functionality missed the opportunity.

Perceptual maps are powerful but depend on selecting the right attributes. Wrong attributes lead to misleading maps. Market research to identify important attributes is essential.

Positioning with purpose (sustainability, social impact) is not just ethical — it is profitable. Unilever's sustainable brands grow faster than their other brands. Consumers vote with their wallets.
